A Musical Portrait

Johann Sebastian Bach

Life, Times & Music  ·  Eisenach 1685 – Leipzig 1750

Origins: A Family of Musicians

Johann Sebastian Bach was born on 31 March 1685 in the small Thuringian town of Eisenach, in what is now central Germany. The town was steeped in history: it was here that Martin Luther had taken refuge in the Wartburg Castle in 1521, translating the New Testament into German and laying the foundations of the Lutheran faith that would shape every aspect of Bach's life and music. The Wartburg still loomed over the town in Bach's day, a daily reminder of the reforming tradition in which he was raised.

But if Luther defined the spiritual landscape of Eisenach, music defined its social one — and in particular, the Bach family. By the time Johann Sebastian was born, the Bachs had been producing professional musicians in Thuringia for four generations. Uncles, cousins, brothers — virtually every male member of the family held some musical post: as town musician, court player, church organist, or cantor. In Thuringia the name Bach had become virtually synonymous with musician, and the family was sufficiently numerous and prominent that they held annual reunions at which, according to a later family document, they sang together “in a most cheerful manner, beginning with chorales and going on to quodlibets.” Music was not art; it was trade, family tradition, and devotion all at once.

Johann Sebastian's father, Johann Ambrosius Bach, was a string player and town musician in Eisenach — a solid, respected civic appointment. The young Johann Sebastian grew up surrounded by music-making as naturally as other children grow up surrounded by conversation. He attended the Latin School in Eisenach, where the curriculum was heavily weighted towards Lutheran theology and singing, and it was clear from early on that he had inherited the family gift in extraordinary measure.

Orphaned and Apprenticed

Tragedy struck when Johann Sebastian was nine. His mother, Maria Elisabeth, died in May 1694. His father, Johann Ambrosius, died just eight months later in February 1695 — still only 50 years old, and recently remarried. Within a year the ten-year-old boy had lost both parents. He and his brother Jakob were taken in by their eldest brother, Johann Christoph, who held the organist's post at Ohrdruf, some 30 kilometres away.

Johann Christoph was a capable musician and a serious teacher. He had studied with the great Johann Pachelbel (whose Canon you know from your Hallé Encore disc CD 500) and gave his young brother a thorough grounding in keyboard technique and music theory. The years at Ohrdruf were formative but not always easy. The household was modest, Johann Christoph's own family grew, and according to a family memoir Johann Sebastian once copied out a book of keyboard pieces — works by Froberger, Kerll and Pachelbel that his brother kept locked away — by moonlight over six months, only for Johann Christoph to confiscate the completed copy. The story may be embellished, but it captures something true: a boy of extraordinary musical appetite and determination, learning in whatever ways he could.

In 1700, at fifteen, Bach won a choral scholarship to the Michaelisschule in Lüneburg, in northern Germany. The journey took him far from Thuringia for the first time — he probably walked most of the 300 kilometres — and opened his ears to new musical worlds. Lüneburg had a distinguished musical tradition; the school library contained a substantial collection of music; and the nearby court of Celle maintained a French orchestra whose style was quite different from anything Bach had encountered in Thuringia. He absorbed it all.

Early Posts: Arnstadt and the Walk to Lübeck

In 1703, barely eighteen, Bach obtained his first significant post: organist at the Neue Kirche (New Church) in Arnstadt, back in Thuringia. The appointment recognised his already exceptional abilities as a keyboard player. He had a fine new organ to play, a regular salary, and relatively light duties. He should have been content.

He was not. He quarrelled with the church council about the standard of his choir (they were, he complained, ignorant and undisciplined; they complained in turn that he confused them with strange harmonies and prolonged the chorales to impossible lengths). He played for too long; then he played for too short. He invited “a strange maiden” (almost certainly his cousin Maria Barbara, whom he would later marry) into the organ loft — a serious impropriety. And in the autumn of 1705, without adequately obtaining leave, he disappeared.

He had gone to Lübeck, some 400 kilometres to the north, to hear Dieterich Buxtehude. Buxtehude was then the most celebrated organist in northern Germany, nearing seventy, and the director of the famous Abendmusiken — public evening concerts in the Marienkirche that attracted audiences from across the region. Bach had borrowed money, obtained what he thought was adequate permission from Arnstadt, and set off — probably on foot at least part of the way. He intended to stay a month. He stayed four.

What he heard in Lübeck changed him permanently. Buxtehude's organ music — its improvisatory freedom, its bold harmonies, its emotional directness — left deep marks on everything Bach wrote for the next decade. The influence is audible in the great organ works from the Weimar period that followed, and it never entirely left him. Your Seraphim Baroque Organ Music disc CD 520 pairs Buxtehude directly with Bach; hearing them in sequence reveals exactly what the young Bach was learning in those four extraordinary months in Lübeck.

He returned to Arnstadt to face a deeply unhappy church council. The records of the subsequent inquiry survive and make delightful reading: “He has been away longer than he should have been; also on his return he has played many peculiar variations in the chorale, mixed into it many strange notes, so that the congregation was confused by it.” Bach apologised — sort of — and continued to quarrel with choir and council until he left Arnstadt in 1707.

Mühlhausen, Marriage, and Moving On

His next post was organist at the Blasiuskirche in Mühlhausen. He was now 22, and in October 1707 he married his cousin Maria Barbara Bach. The match was entirely in the family tradition; she was musical, she was from a good Bach family, and by all accounts they were well suited. They would have seven children together, of whom four survived to adulthood, including Wilhelm Friedemann and Carl Philipp Emanuel Bach, both of whom became distinguished composers in their own right.

Mühlhausen lasted barely a year. Bach composed one substantial cantata there — the celebratory Gott ist mein König BWV 71, the only cantata printed during his lifetime — before requesting his release. His letter of resignation is a remarkable document: he speaks of his wish to pursue a “well-regulated church music to the glory of God,” suggests that Mühlhausen cannot offer the resources to realise this ambition, and politely but firmly moves on. He was twenty-three years old.

Weimar: The Organ at Its Finest

In 1708 Bach moved to Weimar, as court organist and chamber musician to Duke Wilhelm Ernst. He would stay nine years, and it was at Weimar that his organ playing and organ composition reached their zenith. The Duke was a serious music lover who took religion seriously and expected high standards from his musicians. For Bach, it was a productive and reasonably congenial environment — at least for a while.

The Weimar organ works represent the summit of Bach's achievement in this form. The great Prelude and Fugue in D minor BWV 565, the Toccata, Adagio and Fugue in C BWV 564, the Passacaglia and Fugue in C minor BWV 582, the Fantasia and Fugue in G minor BWV 542 — all date from this period. They are works of extraordinary technical command and emotional range, drawing on both the north German improvisatory tradition he had absorbed from Buxtehude and the more structured Italian style he was discovering through his transcriptions of Vivaldi concertos.

It was also at Weimar that Bach began his systematic study of Italian music, particularly Vivaldi. Duke Johann Ernst had brought back scores from Italy, and Bach transcribed a number of Vivaldi concertos for keyboard and organ — a process of absorption that transformed his own compositional thinking, giving his music a new clarity of structure and rhythmic energy. The connection between Vivaldi and Bach is made audible on your Sirena Recorder Quartet disc CD 510, which opens with a Vivaldi concerto and weaves it together with Bach chorale preludes.

Weimar ended badly. When the post of Kapellmeister (head of music) fell vacant in 1716, Bach expected the promotion. He did not receive it. He sought employment elsewhere and accepted the post of Kapellmeister at the court of Prince Leopold of Anhalt-Cöthen. Duke Wilhelm Ernst, furious at being passed over by his own employee, had Bach imprisoned for a month before allowing him to leave. Bach served his time in the Duke's “court of justice” and departed for Cöthen.

Cöthen: Music Liberated from the Church

The years at Cöthen (1717–23) were, in some ways, the most creatively liberated of Bach's career. Prince Leopold was young (twenty-three when Bach arrived), genuinely musical, and Calvinist. Calvinist worship had no use for elaborate church music. Released from the obligation to produce weekly cantatas, Bach poured his energies into instrumental music with an ambition and a range he had never previously had the time to explore.

The six Brandenburg Concertos, presented to the Margrave of Brandenburg in 1721, are the fruits of this period. They are the most varied and inventive concertos of the entire Baroque era — each one a different combination of instruments, each one a different formal experiment, all six together demonstrating a range of compositional resource that no contemporary could match. The concertos were apparently ignored by the Margrave and the parts put in a drawer; they were discovered and published after Bach's death.

The violin sonatas and partitas for solo violin (BWV 1001-1006) also date from Cöthen. The Chaconne that closes the Partita No. 2 in D minor is one of the most extraordinary single movements in all music — fifteen minutes of variations on a four-bar bass line, in which Bach conjures a full harmonic world from a single unaccompanied instrument. Brahms, who later transcribed it for piano left hand alone, wrote to Clara Schumann: “On one stave, for a small instrument, the man writes a whole world of the deepest thoughts and most powerful feelings.”

The six cello suites (BWV 1007-1012) are equally remarkable — six works of impossible perfection for an instrument that was not even considered a solo instrument by most of Bach's contemporaries. Your collection includes Mischa Maisky's celebrated DG recording CD 394 — warm, rhapsodic, and full of personality. The opening Prelude of the First Suite in G major, with its endlessly unfolding arpeggios, is one of those pieces that seems to have existed before it was composed, as if Bach had simply written down something that was already there.

Tragedy interrupted this golden period. In July 1720 Bach returned from a trip with Prince Leopold to find that his wife Maria Barbara had died and been buried in his absence. He had been married thirteen years; they had seven children together. The shock must have been profound. Within a year and a half he had remarried — Anna Magdalena Wülcken, a singer sixteen years his junior, who proved an excellent partner and stepmother, bore him thirteen more children, and continued to copy out his music until the very end of his life.

Leipzig: The Weekly Miracle

In 1722 the post of Cantor of the Thomasschule in Leipzig fell vacant. It was one of the most prestigious musical positions in Lutheran Germany. The council offered it first to Georg Philipp Telemann, who declined. They offered it to Christoph Graupner, who was prevented by his employer from accepting. Then they turned to Bach. One councillor noted, with splendid condescension, that “since the best men could not be obtained, one would have to make do with the mediocre.” He meant, presumably, that Bach was third choice. He was wrong about most things.

Bach moved his large family to Leipzig in May 1723 and remained there for the last 27 years of his life. The post of Cantor carried heavy responsibilities: he was required to direct music at four city churches, to train the boys of the Thomasschule as singers, to teach Latin, and to compose a new cantata virtually every Sunday and feast day throughout the church year. The sheer volume of music he produced in his first years at Leipzig is almost incomprehensible. He composed more than 150 new cantatas in his first three years alone, while simultaneously running the school, teaching private pupils, directing concerts, and raising a very large family.

John Eliot Gardiner's Pilgrimage — the project to record all the surviving Bach cantatas by performing them on the feast days for which they were written, in a single calendar year (2000) — was one of the most ambitious musical undertakings of recent decades. Your collection holds multiple volumes of the resulting recordings on Soli Deo Gloria: CD 1 CD 2 CD 3 CD 4. Each cantata is a compressed world: a dramatic and theological argument typically lasting twenty to thirty minutes, opening with a large choral movement, moving through recitatives and arias for soloists, and closing with a Lutheran chorale — a hymn tune that the congregation knew and would sing along with. The quality never drops. Week after week, year after year, Bach produced music of this order.

The great choral works crown the Leipzig achievement. The St Matthew Passion (1727, revised 1736) sets Matthew's account of Christ's last days for double choir, double orchestra, and soloists in a work that lasts nearly three hours and encompasses every shade of human emotion. Bach himself directs the narrative through the Evangelist's tenor recitative, with Christ's words accompanied by a shimmering string halo. The work was largely forgotten after Bach's death; its rediscovery by the 21-year-old Mendelssohn, who conducted a landmark Berlin performance in 1829 with a choir of 400 singers, is one of the great stories of musical history. The Christmas Oratorio (1734), a set of six cantatas for the days from Christmas to Epiphany, is more immediately accessible — exuberant, warm, and full of the most wonderful tunes. The Mass in B minor, assembled by Bach from movements spanning his entire career and completed in the last years of his life, is a summation: in many views the greatest piece of choral music ever composed.

The Keyboard Works: A Universe in Black and White

Alongside the public music of the church and court, Bach created a parallel universe of keyboard music that was partly pedagogical, partly exploratory, and partly — one feels — simply for himself and for the pleasure of pure musical thought.

The two books of the Well-Tempered Clavier (1722 and 1742) are the most comprehensive survey of keyboard writing ever undertaken: 48 preludes and fugues, two in each major and minor key, demonstrating in practice the implications of equal temperament tuning and, more importantly, showing what an inexhaustible variety of character and expression could be drawn from the simplest formal constraint. You have the definitive modern piano recording: Angela Hewitt's luminous, beautifully voiced Hyperion account CD 7.

The Goldberg Variations (1741) — thirty variations on a bass line and harmonic scheme, framed by a gentle Aria played at beginning and end — were supposedly commissioned by a Russian ambassador called Kaiserling to help him sleep. They would have had the opposite effect on any attentive listener. The Variations have become perhaps the most meditated-upon piece in the keyboard repertoire. Glenn Gould's 1981 recording (see Recommended Recordings below) is the most famous piano account, but the work rewards discovery on harpsichord too — the instrument for which Bach wrote it.

The six keyboard Partitas (BWV 825-830), published in Bach's lifetime as Clavierübung Part I, are the most polished and overtly brilliant of his keyboard suites. Each one is a sequence of stylised dances in a single key, but the variety within that framework is astonishing: the French Overture, the Italian Concerto, the English Suites all demonstrate Bach's mastery of contrasting national styles. He was the only composer of his era who could write convincingly in all three.

The Organ Works: Monuments in Sound

Bach's organ music is the most substantial and technically demanding body of work ever written for the instrument. It spans his entire career, from the early Weimar pieces to the late chorale preludes of the Orgelbüchlein and the magisterial works of his maturity.

The Toccata and Fugue in D minor BWV 565 — the most famous organ piece ever written — appears in four of your recordings. Peter Hurford on the Decca Eclipse disc CD 514 gives a reliable, well-engineered account; Simon Preston on the DG Entrée series CD 515 brings the clarity of his Westminster Abbey playing to a focused programme of Bach organ masterworks; Piet Kee at St Bavo's in Haarlem CD 519 plays on one of the greatest Baroque organs in the world — the very Müller instrument on which the ten-year-old Mozart played in 1766. Each recording reveals something different, because the organ is inseparable from the room and the room is inseparable from the music.

Ton Koopman's two-disc Bach Organ Masterpieces on Regis CD 518 is arguably the finest set in your collection. Koopman plays at historic Dutch organs in Leeuwarden and Amsterdam, and the Penguin Guide awarded the disc three stars and its highest commendation: “Demonstration bracket — the organ itself is the co-star of the performance, magnificent and unclouded.” The Passacaglia and Fugue in C minor BWV 582 here is one of the most compelling recordings ever made of this inexorable, accumulative masterpiece.

Simon Preston's Decca recording at Westminster Abbey CD 516, made in 1963-65, captures the grandeur of a great English cathedral organ in music that was written for a rather different acoustic — the effect is magnificent if not always historically accurate. And the Seraphim two-disc Baroque Organ Music CD 520 provides an invaluable context, placing Bach's organ works alongside Buxtehude and Couperin and showing where he came from and what he transformed.

Late Works and Last Years

In his final years Bach increasingly turned away from the demands of his Leipzig post towards more abstract, speculative compositional projects. He joined a learned society, the Correspondirende Societät der musicalischen Wissenschaften, and began submitting learned canons and contrapuntal puzzles as membership offerings. The Musical Offering (1747), based on a theme given to him by Frederick the Great of Prussia during a celebrated visit to Potsdam, explores the contrapuntal possibilities of that theme in a series of canons, fugues, and a trio sonata of extraordinary beauty. The Art of Fugue (BWV 1080), left unfinished at his death, is the ultimate abstract statement: a systematic exploration of every kind of fugue, all on a single subject, ending with a quadruple fugue that breaks off at the moment Bach introduces his own name (B-A-C-H in German notation) as a countersubject.

His eyesight had been failing for years. In 1750 he submitted to operations by a travelling oculist named John Taylor — the same man who had operated, equally disastrously, on Handel. The operations left Bach blind. He recovered some sight briefly in the weeks before his death, then lost it again. He died on 28 July 1750, aged 65, of a stroke. His estate was modest: some musical instruments, printed music, a few household goods. Anna Magdalena, his widow, received no pension from the city of Leipzig and died in poverty ten years later.

Rediscovery and Legacy

For fifty years after his death Bach was known primarily to specialists and connoisseurs. His sons — Carl Philipp Emanuel and Johann Christian — were far more fashionable. The elegant, lighter style of the emerging Classical period seemed to render his dense counterpoint old-fashioned. Mozart knew and admired him; Beethoven had played the Well-Tempered Clavier as a boy. But the general public barely knew his name.

The turning point came in Berlin on 11 March 1829, when the 20-year-old Felix Mendelssohn conducted the first performance of the St Matthew Passion since Bach's death — a century after its premiere. The audience, which included Hegel and the poet Heinrich Heine, was transfixed. The reviews were ecstatic. A Bach revival began that has never stopped. Within a generation the Bach-Gesellschaft had begun the systematic publication of his complete works. By the end of the nineteenth century he was recognised as the supreme figure in Western music.

That recognition has only deepened. Bach is now understood not merely as a great composer but as the foundation on which all subsequent Western music rests. Harmony, counterpoint, form, keyboard technique — everything that came after him is shaped by what he achieved. And the music itself, heard on its own terms, remains inexhaustible: the same pieces reveal new depths on the hundredth hearing that were invisible on the first. Your collection touches most of the central repertoire. There is no better place to begin — and no end to where it leads.

Further Reading

Books
Johann Sebastian Bach: The Learned Musician
Christoph Wolff  ·  W. W. Norton, 2000

The definitive modern biography by the world's leading Bach scholar. Exhaustive, authoritative, and surprisingly readable. Wolff traces the music and the life with equal depth and care. The essential book for anyone seriously interested in Bach. Available new and secondhand; likely in most good public libraries.

Bach (Master Musicians series)
Malcolm Boyd  ·  Oxford University Press, 2000

The most accessible single-volume life-and-works in the standard reference series. Boyd is clear, well-organised and authoritative. An excellent starting point before tackling Wolff, and ideal for dipping into alongside the music.

The Faber Pocket Guide to Bach
Nicholas Anderson  ·  Faber & Faber, 2007

Compact enough to keep beside the CD player or read on a phone. Anderson provides concise, reliable notes on every major work. Particularly useful for the cantatas, where even dedicated listeners can struggle to keep track of what is what.

A Musical Portrait

Franz Schubert

Life, Times & Music  ·  Vienna 1797 – Vienna 1828

A Schoolmaster's Son

Franz Peter Schubert was born on 31 January 1797 in Himmelpfortgrund, a modest suburb on the northern edge of Vienna. His father, Franz Theodor Schubert, was a parish schoolmaster of peasant stock who had built a respectable lower-middle-class life through hard work and discipline; his mother, Elisabeth Vietz, had been a cook before her marriage. Of the fourteen children she bore, only five survived infancy. Franz was the twelfth. He grew up in the cramped quarters of a suburban schoolhouse, surrounded by the noise of pupils and the constant presence of music — for the Schubert household, like so many in musical Vienna, made music together as naturally as it ate and slept.

His father taught him the violin; his elder brother Ignaz taught him the piano. But it quickly became clear that the boy's gifts ran far beyond anything his family could supply. He had perfect pitch, an extraordinary musical memory, and an instinct for harmony that astonished his first proper teacher, the local church organist Michael Holzer. “If I wished to instruct him in anything fresh,” Holzer later recalled, “he already knew it. Consequently I gave him no actual tuition but merely talked to him and watched him with silent astonishment.”

The Choirboy and the Seminary

In 1808, at the age of eleven, Schubert won a place as a chorister in the imperial court chapel — and with it, a scholarship to the Stadtkonvikt, the imperial seminary that trained the choristers and educated the sons of the bourgeoisie. It was the finest schooling available to a boy of his class, and it transformed him. The Konvikt had a student orchestra in which Schubert played the violin, and through it he came to know the symphonies of Haydn and Mozart and the overtures of Beethoven from the inside, as a player. He devoured everything. The orchestra's conductor noticed that the small, short-sighted boy at the back of the second violins seemed to know the music better than anyone.

He also began to compose, prolifically and from a startlingly young age. Songs, string quartets for the family to play, piano pieces, the beginnings of symphonies — the music poured out of him. For a time he studied with Antonio Salieri, the imperial court composer who had taught Beethoven and, in legend if not in fact, poisoned Mozart. Salieri recognised the boy's gift and gave him a thorough grounding in counterpoint and Italian vocal style. When Schubert's voice broke in 1812 and he could no longer sing in the chapel, he stayed on at the Konvikt as a pupil for another year before the demands of ordinary life intervened.

The Reluctant Schoolmaster and the Miracle of 1814–15

His father wished him to become a schoolteacher, a secure and respectable profession, and in 1814 the seventeen-year-old Schubert dutifully took up a post as an assistant in his father's school, teaching the youngest and most fractious pupils their letters. He loathed it. The work was drudgery; the classroom was chaos; and his mind was entirely elsewhere. For while he was supposedly teaching infants to read, he was composing music at a rate and of a quality that has no real parallel in the history of the art.

In October 1814, aged seventeen, he set a poem from Goethe's Faust — Gretchen sitting at her spinning wheel, her mind turning obsessively on her absent lover. Gretchen am Spinnrade (D.118) is one of the true watersheds in musical history. In it, for the first time, the piano accompaniment is not mere support but a full dramatic participant: the right hand spins the relentless wheel, the music rises to an almost unbearable climax at the memory of Faust's kiss, the wheel falters and stops — and then, with a shudder, begins again. The German art song, the Lied, was effectively born in that single piece, written by a teenager in a noisy schoolroom.

The following year, 1815, was the most productive of his entire life and one of the most productive any composer has ever known. In that one year Schubert wrote around 150 songs — on some days, several complete masterpieces between morning and night — along with two symphonies, two masses, several stage works, and a quantity of chamber and piano music. Among the songs was Erlkönig (D.328), another Goethe setting: the terrifying ballad of a father riding through the night with his dying child, the demonic Erlking luring the boy to his death, the galloping triplets in the piano never letting up until the final, devastating spoken line, “in his arms the child was dead.” He was eighteen. You can hear the full range of his song-writing genius in the Hyperion Schubert Edition CD 538, part of the monumental complete survey, and in Bryn Terfel's warm-hearted recital of favourite songs CD 505.

Schubertiads: A Life Among Friends

Schubert escaped the schoolroom as soon as he could. From around 1817 he abandoned teaching almost entirely and threw in his lot with a circle of young artists, poets, students and civil servants who gathered around him — men who recognised his genius long before the wider world did and who supported him, fed him, gave him lodging, and provided the texts he set to music. This bohemian fellowship was the great fact of his social life. Its members included the poet Johann Mayrhofer, the law student and dilettante Franz von Schober (in whose rooms Schubert often lived), the painter Moritz von Schwind, the singer Johann Michael Vogl — an established opera star who became the foremost interpreter of Schubert's songs — and the writer Eduard von Bauernfeld.

Their evenings together became known as “Schubertiads”: gatherings, often in private houses, devoted entirely to performances of Schubert's music, with the composer at the piano, Vogl singing, dancing afterwards, and a great deal of wine, talk and laughter. They were the warm centre of an otherwise precarious existence. For Schubert never held a salaried musical post, never had a wealthy patron of the kind that sustained Haydn, never enjoyed the concert-giving career that made Beethoven independent. He lived from hand to mouth, on small fees from publishers who exploited him and on the generosity of friends. He was generous in return: easygoing, unpretentious, devoted to his art and his companions, careless of money whenever he had any.

Physically he was unprepossessing — short (barely five feet tall), stocky, round-faced, so near-sighted that he reportedly slept in his spectacles so as to be ready to compose the moment he woke. His friends called him, affectionately, “Schwammerl” — little mushroom. But at the piano, and above all in the act of creation, this modest, shy, unremarkable-looking young man was transformed into one of the supreme musical minds in history.

The Songs

Schubert wrote in every form — symphonies, masses, operas, chamber music, sonatas — but it is above all as the supreme master of the Lied that he stands alone. Over the course of his short life he composed more than six hundred songs, setting the poetry of Goethe, Schiller, Heine, Mayrhofer, Walter Scott, Shakespeare and dozens of others, and in doing so he raised what had been a minor domestic genre into one of the central art forms of the nineteenth century. No composer before him had understood so completely how music could illuminate, deepen and even transfigure a poem — how a shift of harmony could open a window onto the soul.

His genius found its fullest expression in the three great song cycles. Die schöne Müllerin (The Fair Maid of the Mill, 1823), a setting of twenty poems by Wilhelm Müller, tells of a young miller who falls in love, is betrayed, and drowns himself in the brook that has been his companion throughout — the whole sung with a freshness and lyric tenderness that makes the tragedy all the more affecting CD 396. And then there is Winterreise (Winter Journey, 1827), again to Müller, the greatest song cycle ever written and one of the bleakest, most profound utterances in all of music: twenty-four songs charting a rejected lover's wanderings through a frozen landscape towards madness and oblivion, ending with the spectral figure of the hurdy-gurdy man grinding out his tune in the snow CD 387. Schubert knew, when he wrote it, that he was mortally ill. His friends were shocked by the darkness of the music; he told them simply that he liked these songs more than all the rest, and that they would come to like them too.

The Shadow: 1822 and After

In late 1822, at the age of twenty-five, Schubert contracted syphilis. It was, in that era, effectively a sentence of slow death, and the knowledge transformed both his life and his music. The treatments — chiefly mercury, itself highly toxic — were almost as dreadful as the disease. He spent periods in hospital, lost his hair for a time and had to wear a wig, and suffered bouts of severe depression and physical agony. “I feel myself to be the most unhappy and wretched creature in the world,” he wrote to a friend in 1824. “Imagine a man whose health will never be right again… whose most brilliant hopes have perished.”

And yet from this period of suffering came an extraordinary deepening of his art. It was in the autumn of 1822, just as the illness took hold, that he began the Symphony in B minor that the world knows as the Unfinished (D.759) — two movements of such perfection and emotional weight that no one has ever satisfactorily explained why he never completed it. He simply set it aside, and turned to other things, and never returned. The two movements he did finish are among the most beloved in the orchestral repertoire CD 389 CD 398.

The Instrumental Masterpieces

The chamber music of Schubert's last years stands beside Beethoven's as the summit of the form. The String Quartet in D minor, Death and the Maiden (D.810, 1824), takes its name and its haunting slow-movement variations from one of his own songs, in which a terrified girl pleads with Death and Death answers with chilling gentleness; the whole quartet is shadowed by mortality, driven by a desperate energy CD 402 CD 407. The Octet in F major (D.803), written the same year for a wealthy amateur clarinettist, shows the sunnier side of his genius — a glorious, expansive, six-movement serenade of pure delight CD 410.

Above all there is the String Quintet in C major (D.956), composed in the last months of his life and scored, unusually, for string quartet with a second cello rather than a second viola — a choice that gives the music its extraordinary richness and depth of sonority. Its slow movement, an Adagio of almost unbearable beauty and stillness, is for many listeners the single most sublime thing Schubert ever wrote, and one of the supreme achievements of Western music. It was not performed publicly until 1850, more than twenty years after his death CD 401 CD 408.

His earlier chamber music has its own irresistible charm, never more so than in the Trout Quintet (D.667, 1819), written when he was just twenty-two during a happy summer holiday in the Austrian countryside. Its fourth movement is a set of variations on his own song Die Forelle (The Trout), and the whole work brims with the carefree high spirits of youth CD 390 CD 399. For the cello there is also the wistful Arpeggione Sonata (D.821), written for an obsolete bowed guitar and now most often heard on the cello CD 129.

At the piano, Schubert was the great lyric poet of the keyboard. The two sets of Impromptus (D.899 and D.935, 1827) are among the best-loved of all piano music — intimate, songful, by turns playful and profound CD 384. The Wanderer Fantasy (D.760) is his most virtuosic and structurally ambitious solo work, built throughout from a single rhythmic idea drawn from one of his songs CD 144. For piano duet — the favourite music-making of the Schubertiads — he wrote the great Fantasia in F minor (D.940), one of the finest works ever composed for four hands at one keyboard CD 287. And Mitsuko Uchida's recordings reveal the inwardness and poetry of his solo writing as few others have CD 388.

The symphonies, too, grew in stature across his life, culminating in the great Symphony No. 9 in C major (D.944), the Great — a work of vast scale and inexhaustible melodic invention whose “heavenly length,” in Schumann's famous phrase, demands and rewards the listener's full surrender CD 392. The complete cycle traces his whole orchestral development, from the Mozartian charm of the early symphonies to the Romantic grandeur of the last CD 391.

The Final Year

The year 1828 was Schubert's last, and one of the most astonishing final years in the history of any art. Though increasingly ill, he composed with a feverish intensity, as though he sensed how little time remained. In a matter of months he produced the String Quintet in C; the last three piano sonatas (D.958, 959 and 960), the final B-flat major sonata being one of the great valedictory utterances of the repertoire; the Great C major Symphony in its final form; the Mass in E-flat; and the collection of songs published after his death as Schwanengesang (Swan Song), including settings of Heine of an almost expressionist intensity that point far into the future.

In March 1828, for the first and only time in his life, he gave a public concert devoted entirely to his own music. It was a success, and for once he had money in his pocket — though characteristically he seems to have spent much of it standing his friends dinner. Beethoven had died the previous March; Schubert, who revered him almost to the point of worship and had lived in the same city without ever becoming intimate with him, had been a torchbearer at the funeral. Now he had barely eighteen months to follow.

Death and Posterity

In the autumn of 1828 his health collapsed entirely. He moved into his brother Ferdinand's rooms, grew steadily weaker, and in his final days — delirious with what was probably typhoid fever compounding his long illness — was still correcting the proofs of Winterreise and asking to hear Beethoven's C-sharp minor Quartet. He died on 19 November 1828, aged just thirty-one. At his own request he was buried near Beethoven; the poet Franz Grillparzer composed his epitaph: “Music has here entombed a rich treasure, but still fairer hopes.”

That phrase — mourning the masterpieces a longer life would have brought — has haunted posterity. For the cruel irony of Schubert's story is that the world barely knew what it had lost. At his death, the vast majority of his music was unpublished and unperformed: the symphonies, the late chamber works, hundreds of songs, all lay in manuscript. The Great C major Symphony was discovered by Robert Schumann in 1839, in a pile of Schubert's papers kept by Ferdinand, and given its premiere by Mendelssohn in Leipzig. The Unfinished was not heard until 1865. Decade by decade through the nineteenth century, the true scale of his achievement was gradually revealed, until Schubert took his rightful place — beside Beethoven, with whom his life had so briefly and so poignantly overlapped — as one of the supreme composers in the history of music. He had lived thirty-one years. He had changed music forever.

A Musical Portrait

Glenn Gould

Portrait of an Original  ·  Toronto 1932 – Toronto 1982

A Canadian Original

Glenn Herbert Gould was born on 25 September 1932 in Toronto, Canada, into a comfortable, music-loving household in the city's prosperous Beach neighbourhood. His father, Russell Gould, was a furrier; his mother, Florence, was an amateur pianist and singer who began teaching her son the piano almost as soon as he could reach the keys. She noticed immediately that the boy had something beyond ordinary talent: perfect pitch, an uncanny ability to read music at sight, and an absorption in sound that set him apart from other children. He was, from the beginning, someone for whom music was not a subject of study but the primary medium of his inner life.

Toronto in the 1930s and 40s was not an obvious nursery for world-class musical genius, but it gave Gould something perhaps more useful than a great conservatoire: a somewhat marginal, self-reliant, stubbornly individual outlook that he never lost. He was Canadian to the core — proud of it, shaped by it — and the particular quality of Canadian solitude, the vast cold landscape of the north that he loved passionately, runs through his artistic personality like a watermark. He once said that the idea of the north — of isolation, of space, of a world stripped of social distraction — was the most creatively fertile imaginable. He meant it literally as well as metaphorically.

The Prodigy and the Low Chair

At ten, Gould entered the Royal Conservatory of Music in Toronto, where he studied piano with Alberto Guerrero and organ with Frederick Silvester. Guerrero was a distinguished Chilean-Canadian musician who recognised his pupil's gifts and gave him a thorough, demanding training. But even as a student, Gould was already developing habits and techniques that his teacher had not taught him and would not have endorsed. Most famously, the chair: Gould played throughout his entire career on a folding wooden chair that his father had originally made for camping and that Gould had cut down to its extraordinary low height — fourteen inches from floor to seat. He sat so low at the keyboard that his eyes were barely above the level of his hands. He conducted this low, crouching posture to a uniquely intimate relationship with the instrument, as though he were inside the music rather than presiding over it from above.

He carried that chair everywhere — to every studio, every concert hall — until it became so worn and structurally compromised that it was effectively held together by memory and habit. When it finally disintegrated he had a replica made, but the original, battered and repaired countless times, remained for him a kind of talisman. He also soaked his hands and forearms in very hot water before playing, wore heavy coats and gloves even in summer, refused to shake hands (for fear of infection), and hummed and sang continuously while performing — an involuntary accompaniment audible on virtually every recording he ever made and maddening to those who found it distracting, though for many listeners it became inseparable from the music itself, a reminder that this was always a profoundly personal, embodied act.

His professional debut came in 1945 as an organist at Casavant Frères in Toronto; his piano recital debut followed in 1947. He was fifteen, already fully formed in all the ways that mattered. Reviewing his early performances, critics struggled to find the right vocabulary. He was technically immaculate, but what startled them was something harder to name: a quality of absolute concentration, of music thought through from the inside, that made even familiar pieces sound newly discovered.

The 1955 Goldbergs: An Earthquake

In January 1955, aged twenty-two, Gould made his American debut at the Phillips Collection in Washington DC and Town Hall in New York. The response was electric. Within weeks he had been signed to Columbia Records — an almost unheard-of speed for a young pianist unknown outside Canada. And in June 1955, over two sessions in Columbia's 30th Street Studio in New York, he recorded the work that would make him famous across the world: Bach's Goldberg Variations.

The Goldberg Variations had been regarded, if at all, as a dry academic exercise — a learned contrapuntal curiosity of interest mainly to scholars. Gould's recording demolished that perception entirely. He played it at tempi ranging from the reckless to the meditative, articulated the inner voices with a clarity that made the contrapuntal architecture visible as never before, and brought to the whole an energy and personality that was simply unlike anything the gramophone had previously heard. The humming was already there; so was the sense that this was a musician engaging with Bach in the most direct, unmediated way imaginable — not performing the music but thinking it, in real time. It was the first classical LP to sell over a million copies. Both that 1955 recording and the valedictory 1981 account are yours in CD 11.

Concert Hall vs. Recording Studio

Gould was already, even in his concert years, articulating a position that would eventually lead him to one of the most dramatic decisions in musical history. He believed — and argued with great intellectual force and considerable provocation — that the concert hall was a dying institution; that the relationship between performer and passive audience it enforced was fundamentally inimical to serious musical experience; and that the recording studio was not merely a means of documenting performance but a new creative medium with its own aesthetic possibilities.

His argument was not philistine. He pointed out that recording allowed for a kind of perfection impossible in live performance — not just technical perfection, but interpretative perfection, the ability to assemble from many takes the ideal realisation of a musical vision. He pointed out that the listener at home, alone with a record, could engage with music in a more concentrated and personal way than any concert audience. He was, in this as in much else, ahead of his time: he was describing, forty years early, the relationship that most people now have with recorded music.

But he also simply found the concert hall, with its rituals of applause and social performance, deeply uncongenial. He was constitutionally a private man. The stage frightened him not in the conventional sense — his nerves were entirely under control — but in a deeper, more philosophical way: he disliked the element of spectacle, of public display, that concert-giving inevitably involved.

The Retirement

On 10 April 1964, in Los Angeles, Glenn Gould gave his final public concert. He was thirty-one years old, at the absolute peak of his performing powers and international fame, and he simply walked away from the stage — permanently, as it turned out — never to return. It was one of the most dramatic and debated decisions in the history of musical performance. Friends, managers, concert promoters and critics all assumed it was a temporary withdrawal; that he would return within months. He never did.

What followed was not a retirement in any conventional sense. It was a redirection. Gould threw his extraordinary energies into recording, writing, broadcasting, and a series of innovative radio documentaries for the Canadian Broadcasting Corporation. His output, if anything, increased. He was simply doing exactly what he wanted to do, on his own terms, in his own time, free at last from the tyranny of the concert schedule, the obsequious management, the applauding crowds, the handshaking, the social performance of the musical life.

Behind the Microphone

In the studio, Gould was a perfectionist of a peculiar and highly original kind. He did not seek perfect takes in the conventional sense — a single flawless run-through of a piece — but something more interesting: the perfect assembly of many takes, each capturing a different aspect of the musical vision. He would record a passage twenty or thirty times, each slightly different in character, and then work with his producer to splice together the ideal version. He was interested in the studio as an instrument, and his recordings from the later period are in this sense composite constructions, though the joins are invisible to any but the most forensic ear.

He was also, uniquely, a practitioner and theorist of what he called “the prospects of recording” — a genuine philosopher of the medium. His essays on the subject, written with great wit and argumentative precision, are among the most searching things ever written about recorded music. He foresaw the internet — specifically, the possibility of listeners assembling their own versions of performances from multiple recorded options — with an accuracy that now seems almost uncanny.

His Musical World: Bach and Beyond

Gould's musical sympathies were wide but particular. He was above all a Bach pianist — the Goldbergs, the Well-Tempered Clavier, the Partitas, the English and French Suites, the Inventions — but he was also deeply committed to the early keyboard music of Sweelinck, Gibbons and Byrd (composers you will find in your own collection), to Beethoven (he recorded virtually all the sonatas, with results that ranged from the revelatory to the deliberately perverse), and to twentieth-century music, particularly Schoenberg, Berg and Webern. He was the great champion of Schoenberg's piano works at a time when almost no one else was playing them, and his recordings of the Three Piano Pieces Op.11 and the Suite Op.25 remain benchmarks.

He was equally particular about what he refused to play. He had almost no interest in Chopin, Liszt or most of the Romantic piano repertoire. He found Chopin merely decorative; Liszt vulgar; the cult of the virtuoso performer in general a distraction from the music itself. These views were sincerely held and entertainingly argued, and they have a coherence: Gould was drawn to music in which structure and counterpoint were primary, where the architecture of the piece was the point, and where the performer's role was to elucidate that architecture rather than to display personal feeling. In this he was, in his way, a classicist — though a classicist of extreme and idiosyncratic tastes.

He also composed. His String Quartet Op.1, written in 1955 and recorded, was a serious, ambitious work that revealed a genuine compositional voice. He wrote for his CBC radio documentaries — the “Solitude Trilogy,” three programmes about isolation and the far north, are considered among the finest examples of the radiophonic art. He was, in short, a complete musical intelligence, not merely a performer.

Solitude and Eccentricity

As the years after his retirement passed, Gould became increasingly reclusive — though “reclusive” is perhaps misleading, since he was intensely sociable in his preferred medium: the telephone. He was famous for calls lasting three, four, five hours, conducted late at night, ranging across music, philosophy, literature and life with extraordinary verve and wit. His friends cherished these calls; his acquaintances were sometimes overwhelmed by them. In person, however, he retreated from the world. He rarely left Toronto; he frequented all-night diners and truck stops, where he felt comfortable and anonymous; he lived, in his later years, largely nocturnally, composing and recording through the night.

His hypochondria grew. He was a prodigious consumer of medications — blood pressure drugs, sedatives, stimulants, vitamins — and kept meticulous records of his heart rate and blood pressure, which he took with obsessive frequency. He was convinced for much of his adult life that he had various serious ailments; some of the ailments, as it turned out, were real. He was also convinced that cold was health-giving and warmth dangerous: he wore his overcoat through Canadian summers and kept his studio at temperatures his engineers found arctic. He loved dogs with a tenderness he found difficult to extend to most humans. He talked to them, worried about them, and felt their companionship as a form of uncomplicated grace.

The 1981 Goldbergs: A Valediction

In the spring of 1981, Gould returned to the Goldberg Variations — the work with which he had begun his career twenty-six years earlier — and recorded it again. He was forty-eight, in deteriorating health, and he played it as if he knew it was the last time. Where the 1955 recording was mercurial, rhythmically propulsive and outward-facing — a young man seizing the music and offering it to the world — the 1981 account is inward, slow, contemplative, the Aria played at the beginning with a gravity and tenderness that stops the breath, the variations themselves unfolding with the patience of someone who has nothing left to prove and everything to explore. Many of the variations are taken at half the speed of the 1955 version; none of it feels slow.

He completed the recording in April 1981. It was released in September 1982 — the month he turned fifty. On 27 September, two days after his birthday, he suffered a massive stroke. He never regained consciousness. He died on 4 October 1982, aged fifty years and nine days. The 1981 Goldbergs, heard in the knowledge of what followed, carry a weight that has only deepened with time. Together the two recordings — both in your collection as CD 11 — are the most complete artistic self-portrait in the history of recorded music: a life in performance, bookended by the same thirty-two pieces, utterly transformed.

Legacy

Glenn Gould died at fifty having transformed, almost single-handedly, the way the world thinks about recorded music, about Bach, and about what a performing musician can and should be. His influence is incalculable. Every pianist who plays Bach today does so in the shadow of his example, whether they acknowledge it or not. His arguments about recording, about the concert hall, about the aesthetics of musical performance, have proved more prophetic than almost anyone recognised at the time. And the recordings themselves — the Goldbergs, the Well-Tempered Clavier, the Partitas, the Beethoven sonatas, the Schoenberg — remain, fifty years on, as alive, as personal, as inescapably present as they were when he made them. There has been no one quite like him. There will not be again.

A Genre History

The String Quartet

From Haydn to Shostakovich  ·  c.1760 – 1974

Four Instruments, One Voice

The string quartet — two violins, viola and cello — is the chamber music form that composers across two and a half centuries have chosen above all others for their most serious, most intimate and most unguarded utterances. It is the medium in which Beethoven expressed things he could not say in symphonies, in which Schubert confronted his mortality, in which Shostakovich, forced to conceal his thoughts from a totalitarian state, hid messages in plain sight. Strip away the orchestra's colour and weight, the piano's percussive brilliance, and what remains is four voices of essentially identical character, obliged to balance, to listen, to argue and to agree with no hiding place and nothing to conceal imprecision. It is the most demanding medium in music, and the most revealing.

Its history is one of the great continuous narratives in Western art: from Haydn's elegant conversational wit to Beethoven's shattering of every formal convention; from Schubert's song-saturated lyricism to the nationalist voices of Smetana and Dvořák; from Debussy and Ravel's transformation of the harmonic language to Bartók's ferocious reinvention of the whole enterprise; from Shostakovich's fifteen coded confessions to the Kronos Quartet's reimagining of what the medium could encompass. Your collection traces almost every chapter of this story.

Haydn: Inventing the Conversation

The string quartet did not spring fully formed from any single mind. It evolved gradually through the middle decades of the eighteenth century from the divertimento — light entertainment music for small groups — towards something more serious and structurally ambitious. But it was Joseph Haydn who gave it its definitive character, and for this reason he is universally recognised as the father of the form, despite the fact that he would have been puzzled by the title.

Haydn's contribution was not merely compositional but philosophical. In his Op. 33 quartets of 1781 he wrote to a friend that they were composed “in a new and quite special manner,” and the novelty was exactly this: each of the four instruments was given an equal share of the musical argument. There was no longer a dominant melody on the first violin with the others as accompaniment. Instead, a theme could pass from instrument to instrument, be interrupted, questioned, elaborated and contradicted — a conversation among four independent but mutually attentive voices. It was a musical model for something like civilised discourse, and it transformed the medium.

His Op. 76 quartets, written in 1796-97 and among the last he completed, show the form at full maturity CD 240. The ‘Emperor’ quartet, whose slow movement is a set of variations on the melody Haydn had just written as the Austrian imperial anthem (it became, with different words, Deutschland über Alles), is one of the most widely beloved. The ‘Fifths’ quartet, with its dramatic descending interval in the opening theme, is one of the most immediately striking. And the Allegri Quartet's disc includes the ‘Bird’ quartet, Op. 33 No. 3, with its delightful chirping grace notes in the first movement CD 400.

A Short Digression: The Spanish Mozart

Before moving to Mozart, one remarkable detour is worth making. Juan Crisóstomo de Arriaga (1806-1826) was a Basque composer who died in Paris three months before his twentieth birthday and left behind, among other things, three string quartets of such extraordinary accomplishment that they have earned him the sobriquet “the Spanish Mozart.” The three quartets, written when he was barely seventeen, are fluent, formally secure and melodically inspired in a way that defies their composer's age — more than competent imitations of the Haydn-Mozart style, but alive with a personality and invention entirely their own CD 2. What he might have achieved with another forty years is one of music's tantalising unanswerable questions.

Mozart: The Poet Responds

Mozart's engagement with the string quartet was both direct and deeply personal. His six “Haydn Quartets” (K.387-465), dedicated to the older composer and described by Mozart in his dedication as “the fruit of long and laborious effort,” are his response to the challenge that Haydn's Op. 33 had thrown down. They are remarkable for their expressive depth: the famous ‘Dissonance’ Quartet K.465, with its extraordinary slow chromatic introduction that seems to dissolve tonality altogether, anticipates harmonic developments of a hundred years later. Haydn, hearing the six quartets performed, turned to Mozart's father and said: “I tell you before God and as an honest man that your son is the greatest composer known to me.”

Mozart's later quintets — for two violins, two violas and cello — are if anything even finer. The Quintet in C minor K.516 and the G minor K.516 are among the supreme achievements of his chamber output, darker and more searching than the quartets CD 303 CD 304. It was a combination — four strings plus one additional voice — that Schubert would later take to its absolute summit.

Beethoven: Expanding the Universe

Beethoven approached the string quartet three times, at very different stages of his life and art, and each time he transformed it. His six Op. 18 quartets (1798-1800) are masterly early works, thoroughly in the Haydn-Mozart tradition, already marked by a characteristic energy and directness but still essentially conventional in form.

The three Razumovsky Quartets Op. 59 (1806), written for the Russian ambassador Count Razumovsky and incorporating Russian folk themes at his request, are something entirely different. They stunned contemporary listeners. One violinist, presented with the score of Op. 59 No. 1, declared that it was not music; a colleague replied that it would be music in fifty years. He was right. The sheer scale of these works — each one as long as a symphony — the complexity of their development sections, the radical harmonic adventurousness, the slow movements of almost unbearable intensity, all announced that the string quartet had ceased to be drawing-room entertainment and had become something as serious, as demanding, as a symphony or an opera CD 77.

Beethoven: Into the Unknown

But it is the five late quartets (Op. 127, 130, 131, 132 and 135), written between 1824 and 1826 when Beethoven was completely deaf and within sight of death, that mark the most extraordinary single chapter in the entire history of the form. They were incomprehensible to his contemporaries, barely performed for decades, and are now recognised as among the most profound utterances in Western music.

The Quartet in A minor Op. 132 contains a central slow movement marked Heiliger Dankgesang eines Genesenen an die Gottheit — Holy Song of Thanksgiving of a Convalescent to the Divinity — written after Beethoven's recovery from a serious illness. It is music of an otherworldly stillness and beauty, alternating between a modal chorale (marked “feeling new strength”) and a dancing D major episode, that seems to exist outside ordinary time. The Quartet in C-sharp minor Op. 131, in seven linked movements played without a break, is perhaps the single most concentrated masterpiece in the chamber repertoire — a complete emotional and spiritual journey compressed into forty minutes. And then there is the Grosse Fuge Op. 133, originally the finale of Op. 130 and so violent and dissonant that Beethoven's publisher persuaded him to publish it separately: a fugue of extraordinary ferocity that sounds like nothing else in the music of its era and a great deal in the music of ours. Your collection covers the late quartets in depth, with both the Quartetto Italiano CD 78 CD 79 and the Amadeus Quartet accounts CD 88 available for comparison.

Schubert: In Beethoven's Shadow

Schubert lived in Vienna alongside Beethoven, worshipped him from a respectful distance, was a torchbearer at his funeral in 1827, and died the following year — aged thirty-one, a year after Beethoven, having spent his entire creative life in the shadow of that overwhelming presence. His chamber music shows how a composer of genius can absorb a dominant influence without being crushed by it.

The Quartettsatz in C minor D.703 (1820), a single movement surviving from an unfinished quartet, is as dramatic and concentrated as anything Beethoven wrote — but unmistakably Schubertian in its lyricism and harmonic colour CD 400. The ‘Rosamunde’ Quartet D.804 (1824), with its slow movement based on music from his incidental score for the play of that name, has a radiant, singing quality that is entirely his own. But it is the Death and the Maiden Quartet D.810 (also 1824) that is the supreme achievement among his quartets — a work so charged with mortality and passionate desperation that it feels less like chamber music than a cry from the abyss. The slow movement is a set of variations on his own song of the same name, in which Death speaks in the bass with a terrible, seductive calm. Your collection holds no fewer than four separate recordings of this work CD 402 CD 404 CD 407 CD 408 — a tribute to its inexhaustible fascination.

The late G major Quartet D.887 (1826), the last he completed, goes still further: a vast, restless work of nearly fifty minutes, harmonically turbulent and structurally unprecedented, it anticipates the emotional world of the late sonatas and looks forward to the string writing of Brahms CD 406 CD 408. And then there is the String Quintet in C major D.956 (1828), written in the last weeks of his life and scored, like Boccherini, for string quartet plus second cello. Its Adagio slow movement — one of the most sustained passages of pure beauty in all music — seems to hover, timeless, between grief and acceptance CD 401 CD 408.

The Romantic Quartet: Mendelssohn and Brahms

The mid-nineteenth century produced string quartets of great accomplishment and occasional genius, though none quite matching the revolutionary force of the late Beethoven. Mendelssohn's six quartets span his career and show his characteristic combination of Classical formal clarity and Romantic expressive warmth; the F minor Quartet Op. 80, written in a single anguished outburst after the death of his sister Fanny, is his darkest and most emotionally raw work in any form CD 281.

Brahms, temperamentally unable to publish anything he did not consider fully achieved, wrote only three string quartets — and destroyed at least twenty others before he was satisfied. The two Op. 51 quartets (1873) and the B-flat Op. 67 (1875) are dense, richly developed works that draw on the late Beethoven tradition while speaking in an unmistakably Brahmsian voice: autumnal, contrapuntally complex, suffused with a characteristically Central European melancholy.

National Voices: Smetana and Dvořák

The later nineteenth century saw the emergence of powerful national schools of quartet-writing. Bedřich Smetana's ‘From my Life’ (1876) is one of the first explicitly autobiographical quartets: four movements depicting episodes from the composer's own life, culminating in a high E whistling through the final movement — the actual sound of the tinnitus that heralded his deafness. It is a programmatic work of startling directness, and it opened a tradition of confessional quartet-writing that would reach its fullest expression in Shostakovich. Your collection has a beautiful recording by the Pavel Haas Quartet, Czech musicians performing Czech music with native authority CD 438.

Antonín Dvořák's ‘American’ Quartet Op. 96 (1893), written during his time in Iowa, is perhaps the most universally beloved quartet after Beethoven's middle works — its pentatonic melodies and lilting syncopations infused with the spirit of American folk music, its warmth and directness entirely irresistible. It is a notable gap in the collection and appears in the recommended recordings below.

The French Transformation: Ravel

The French string quartet tradition found its most perfect expression in two works written within a year of each other: Debussy's in G minor (1893) and Ravel's in F major (1903). Both composers owed a debt to the cyclic formal techniques of César Franck, in which thematic material transforms and returns across movements, but both transcended their influence entirely. Where Debussy's quartet shimmers with impressionist colour and subtle ambiguity, Ravel's is more classical in outline but equally radical in harmonic language — its pizzicato second movement one of the wittiest and most perfectly crafted movements in all chamber music. Your collection has a recording of the Ravel alongside Haydn and Schubert on the Allegri Quartet's Naim disc CD 400.

Elgar and the English Quartet

Elgar's String Quartet in E minor Op. 83 (1919), written alongside the Piano Quintet and Cello Concerto in a remarkable late flowering, is one of the finest contributions to the English chamber repertoire: a work of sustained lyrical beauty and autumnal regret, quite different in character from the public pomp of his orchestral music. The ‘pensive and haunting’ quality he described in the slow movement is peculiarly English — a landscape heard rather than seen. Your collection pairs it with the Piano Quintet in another fine work from the same creative burst CD 174.

Bartók: The Twentieth-Century Summit

Béla Bartók's six string quartets, written between 1909 and 1939, are almost universally recognised as the greatest contribution to the form since Beethoven — a complete world in six works, each one different in character and equally extraordinary. Bartók brought to the string quartet his deep immersion in Hungarian, Romanian and Bulgarian folk music (collected in the field with a phonograph over decades), his command of the European modernist tradition, and a structural intelligence of the highest order. The results range from the fierce dissonance and brutal percussive effects of the Fourth Quartet to the night-music of the Third and the valedictory lyricism of the Sixth, written as the Second World War closed in and Bartók prepared to leave Europe for the last time. They are, collectively, a monument of the twentieth century — and a notable gap in the collection that the recommended recordings address below.

Shostakovich: Fifteen Confessions

Dmitri Shostakovich's fifteen string quartets, written between 1938 and 1974, are perhaps the most personal documents in the entire history of the form. Composed under the shadow of Stalinist terror — Shostakovich lived in constant fear of arrest, having already been publicly denounced by the regime in 1936 and again in 1948 — they encode a private emotional world that his more public works could not safely contain. The string quartet was his diary.

The Eighth Quartet (1960), written in three days in Dresden while ostensibly composing film music, is a work of devastating autobiographical intensity: Shostakovich quotes from his own earlier works, from Tchaikovsky, from a Russian revolutionary song, and from a Jewish folk melody, weaving them together in a tapestry of grief and memory. He told a friend he had written it “in memory of myself.” The Fifteenth (1974), his last, ends with a Funeral March and an Epilogue of such bleakness that the final pizzicato chord seems to extinguish a light. Your collection is extraordinarily rich in Shostakovich quartets: the complete Naxos survey by the Éder Quartet across six volumes CD 422 CD 423 CD 424 CD 425 CD 426 CD 427, the Shostakovich Quartet's complete set on Regis CD 428, the Borodin Quartet in Nos. 1 and 15 CD 429, the Taneyev Quartet in Nos. 11-13 CD 430, and the Mandelring Quartett's SACD set CD 431. It is one of the finest single-composer quartet collections imaginable.

Stravinsky and the Modern Tradition

Igor Stravinsky wrote relatively little for string quartet — the Three Pieces (1914) and the Concertino (1920) are the principal works — but they are characteristically bracing, stripped of the Romantic warmth that still permeates Bartók and pointing towards the neoclassical austerity of his later style. They mark one exit from the Romantic tradition; Webern's Five Movements Op. 5 (1909) and Six Bagatelles Op. 9 (1913) mark another, compressing the entire emotional range of a Beethoven quartet into a few minutes of barely audible intensity.

The tradition continued and diversified through the century: Britten, Tipp ett, Ligeti CD 262, Lutosławski, and dozens of others added to the canon. The Kronos Quartet, founded in 1973, reinvented the very idea of what a quartet's repertoire could encompass — commissioning new works, reimagining popular music, crossing every genre boundary with infectious energy and complete seriousness of purpose CD 508 CD 509. Their recordings in your collection show the form alive, various, and still expanding — two hundred and fifty years after Haydn first set four players talking to each other and found that they had something inexhaustible to say.

A Musical Portrait

Ludwig van Beethoven

Life, Times & Music  ·  Bonn 1770 – Vienna 1827

A Difficult Beginning

Ludwig van Beethoven was born on or around 17 December 1770 in Bonn, then a modest Rhenish city and seat of the Elector of Cologne. The qualifying phrase “on or around” is already characteristic: Beethoven himself believed he had been born in 1772, and his father Johann van Beethoven, a court tenor with a taste for alcohol and an eye for the main chance, encouraged the confusion by falsifying his son's birth certificate in an attempt to pass him off as a two-year-old prodigy. It was not the last time the facts of Beethoven's life would be obscured by myth, self-mythology, and wishful invention.

Johann van Beethoven was a talented but dissolute man who recognised in his son the gift he himself lacked and was determined to exploit it. The model was Mozart: the prodigy taken on tour, exhibited before courts and crowned heads, generating income. The young Ludwig was accordingly pushed to the keyboard from the age of four and subjected to lessons of punishing intensity. Johann was not a cruel father by the standards of the time, but he was careless, intermittently drunk, and more interested in commercial possibilities than in nurturing a child's wellbeing. The boy who emerged from this household was marked by it permanently: suspicious of authority, irascible in personal relations, given to sudden intense attachments followed by rupture, constitutionally unable to accept subordination.

But he had the music, and nothing could dim that. His first proper teacher, the court organist Christian Gottlob Neefe, recognised immediately that this was not merely a talented boy but a phenomenon. Neefe taught him Bach's Well-Tempered Clavier — all 48 preludes and fugues — and introduced him to the most demanding repertoire available. By 1782, at the age of eleven, Beethoven was deputising for Neefe as court organist. By 1784 he was on the electoral payroll in his own right. Your disc of the youthful Beethoven CD 76 gives a sense of this early flowering — music written by a teenager already possessed of formidable technique and an unmistakable voice.

Vienna and the Conquering Pianist

In 1787 the seventeen-year-old Beethoven made his first visit to Vienna and, by persistent tradition, played for Mozart, who told the audience to watch this young man: “some day he will give the world something to talk about.” Whether the meeting happened at all is debated by scholars; but Beethoven returned to Bonn within weeks on learning that his mother was dying of tuberculosis. Her death in July 1787 devastated him. The following years were darkened by his father's increasing alcoholism: Beethoven effectively became the head of the household, petitioning the electoral court to have half his father's salary paid directly to him to protect his younger brothers. He was seventeen.

In 1790 Haydn passed through Bonn twice, heard some of Beethoven's music, and offered to teach him. In November 1792 Beethoven left for Vienna, aged twenty-one, carrying an album of testimonials and the hope of a fresh start. His father died the following month; Beethoven never returned to Bonn. He studied with Haydn (briefly and unsatisfactorily — each found the other trying), then with Johann Schenk, Johann Albrechtsberger for counterpoint, and Antonio Salieri for Italian vocal style. The studies were always secondary. What Beethoven had come to Vienna to do was play the piano, and play it he did.

His improvisations were the talk of the city. He would sit at the keyboard and hold an entire room in a kind of suspended animation, taking a simple theme and transforming it through modulations and developments that no one had imagined possible, building to climaxes of overwhelming emotional force. He challenged and bested the finest pianists in the city in formal contests. The aristocracy — Prince Lichnowsky, Prince Lobkowitz, Count Razumovsky — took him up, gave him lodgings, stipends, and quartets of players to write for. It was an extraordinary position for a young man of no social standing, achieved entirely by force of musical personality.

The Shadow Falls: Deafness

By 1796, when Beethoven was still only twenty-five, he had begun to notice a buzzing in his ears. By 1800 it was impossible to ignore. The greatest composer of his age was going deaf, in an era when deafness meant not merely the loss of a physical faculty but the destruction of a career, a livelihood, and a social identity. He kept the secret for years, terrified of what it would mean if known, avoiding company and conversation, retreating into isolation.

In October 1802, during a summer stay in the village of Heiligenstadt outside Vienna, he wrote a letter to his brothers that he never sent. The Heiligenstadt Testament is one of the most remarkable documents in the history of music: a howl of grief and humiliation from a man who has contemplated suicide and chosen instead to continue. “I would have ended my life — it was only my art that held me back. Ah, it seemed to me impossible to leave the world until I had brought forth all that I felt was within me.” He sealed the letter and put it away; it was found among his papers after his death. From that moment on, something had changed. The music that followed was unlike anything that had come before — in his work or anyone else's.

The Heroic Period

Between roughly 1803 and 1812 Beethoven produced the body of work that defines him in the public imagination and that changed the course of Western music. The Third Symphony in E-flat major, Op. 55, known as the Eroica, was originally dedicated to Napoleon Bonaparte, whom Beethoven admired as the embodiment of Revolutionary ideals. When Napoleon declared himself Emperor in 1804, Beethoven erased the dedication so violently that he tore the paper: “Is he then, too, nothing more than an ordinary human being?” The symphony — three times the length of any symphony previously written, of a harmonic and structural complexity that made it almost incomprehensible to its first audiences — was dedicated instead to a patron, Prince Lobkowitz. It announced a new era.

The Fifth Symphony in C minor, Op. 67, with its famous four-note opening motif — three short notes and one long — is the most recognised piece of orchestral music ever written and probably the most analysed. It is also, heard freshly, still astounding: a single-minded fifty-minute argument that moves from darkness to triumph in one unbroken arc. Your collection holds multiple symphony cycles, including Gardiner's period-instrument Orchestre Révolutionnaire et Romantique CD 94, Karajan's imperious Berlin Philharmonic account CD 95, Rattle's Vienna Philharmonic recording CD 97, the complete Mackerras survey on Hyperion CD 96, and the LSO Live six-disc SACD set CD 106 — one of the richest symphony collections possible.

The Sixth Symphony, the Pastoral, is the great exception to the heroic mode: a five-movement evocation of the countryside, of “feelings awakened by a pleasant landscape,” the only symphony Beethoven provided with a descriptive programme. It is a work of serene happiness, more Schubert than anything else in his output, the storm in the fourth movement followed by a Shepherd's Song of extraordinary calm CD 80. The Seventh and Eighth (on the same disc with Gardiner CD 94, and on Beecham's celebrated older account CD 81) form a pair of pure energy and wit: the Seventh all propulsive rhythmic drive, the Eighth compact and playful.

The Concertos

Beethoven's five piano concertos trace his development from the elegant classicism of the First and Second to the colossal architecture of the Fifth, known as the Emperor. The Third Concerto in C minor, clearly influenced by Mozart's great C minor concerto K.491, marks the turning point — darker, more dramatic, the soloist and orchestra genuinely in conflict rather than conversation. Your collection holds Ashkenazy in the First and Third CD 75 CD 74, Perahia in the Third and Fourth with the Concertgebouw CD 90, and two accounts of the Emperor: Brendel's classic Philips recording CD 89 and Arrau with Colin Davis and the Dresden Staatskapelle CD 103.

The Fourth Concerto in G major is perhaps the most poetically beautiful of the five — its slow movement a dialogue between a quietly insistent piano and a gradually subdued orchestra that Liszt memorably described as Orpheus taming the Furies. The Emperor is the culmination: a work of imperial scale and confidence, its opening movement the longest first movement in any concerto up to that date, its finale a rondo of irresistible momentum.

The Violin Concerto in D major, Op. 61, is the only concerto he wrote for that instrument and the supreme work in the repertoire, its long lyrical lines sustained over a harmonic simplicity that disguises extraordinary structural depth. You have it in two very different accounts: Mutter with the New York Philharmonic and Masur on DG CD 85, and Zehetmair with the Orchestra of the 18th Century on period instruments CD 102. The contrast between the two — the one lush and romantic, the other lean and historically informed — illuminates the concerto from opposite ends. The Triple Concerto for piano, violin and cello, one of his more relaxed and sociable works, features the Beaux Arts Trio CD 100.

Chamber Music: Conversations in Four Parts

The three Razumovsky Quartets, Op. 59 (1806), are the chamber music equivalents of the heroic symphonies — vast in scale, revolutionary in language, deeply serious in intent. Count Razumovsky, the Russian ambassador and a keen amateur quartet player, commissioned them; at his request Beethoven incorporated Russian folk themes into the first two. Their first audiences were baffled: a violinist who played through Op. 59 No. 1 declared it could not possibly be music; a colleague replied that it would be understood in fifty years. He was right. The Lindsay Quartet's account on your shelves CD 77 is among the most admired modern recordings.

The late quartets (Opp. 127, 130, 131, 132 and 135), written between 1824 and 1826, are the summit of his achievement and among the most profound utterances in all of Western art. Completely deaf, withdrawn from public life, Beethoven turned the string quartet into a vehicle for a kind of musical thought that had no precedent and no real successor until Bartók. The Quartetto Italiano's two-volume set on Philips CD 78 CD 79, along with the Amadeus Quartet in Opp. 130, 133 and 135 CD 88, give you the complete late cycle in distinguished accounts.

The violin sonatas are among his most intimate works. The Kreutzer Sonata in A major, Op. 47 — its first movement of volcanic energy, its final rondo of almost reckless brilliance — famously inspired Tolstoy's novella of the same name. Your collection includes Perlman and Ashkenazy CD 101, Kremer and Argerich CD 84, and the Szeryng and Haebler complete survey CD 98. For the cello sonatas — five works across his entire career that effectively created the modern repertoire for the instrument — you have the du Pré and Bishop-Kovacevich account of Nos. 3 and 5 CD 73, the du Pré and Barenboim complete survey CD 99, and the Schiff and Perényi complete recording on ECM CD 104.

The Piano: A Life at the Keyboard

Beethoven's thirty-two piano sonatas span his entire creative life and constitute the most important body of keyboard music since Bach's Well-Tempered Clavier. From the early Opp. 2 and 7 through the middle-period landmarks — the Pathétique Op. 13, the Moonlight Op. 27 No. 2, the Waldstein Op. 53, the Appassionata Op. 57 — to the last five sonatas of which the Hammerklavier Op. 106 is the most monumental, they trace the full arc of his development. The Hammerklavier, over forty-five minutes long, was described by Beethoven himself as a sonata “that will give pianists something to do when it is played fifty years hence.” Hiromi Okada's recording pairs it with Bach's Partita No. 2 in a provocative and illuminating coupling CD 87.

The Diabelli Variations, Op. 120 (1823), are the piano work that most closely rivals the late quartets in depth and originality. Anton Diabelli, a Viennese music publisher, invited fifty composers to write a variation each on a waltz theme he had composed. Beethoven declined to contribute a single variation — and then wrote thirty-three of them, transforming Diabelli's simple, almost banal theme into a complete world of musical thought, from the most solemn fugue to the most comic scherzo. Paul Lewis's acclaimed Harmonia Mundi recording is in your collection CD 92. And for the most unusual Beethoven experience imaginable, András Schiff plays on Beethoven's own surviving Broadwood piano — the instrument he was given by the London firm in 1818 and treasured until the end of his life — in a recording that reveals how differently his music sounded on the instrument he actually heard in his imagination CD 93.

The Ninth Symphony and the Final Years

By 1817 Beethoven had been almost entirely deaf for several years. He communicated by writing in conversation books; he had not performed in public for a decade; his personal life was in chaos, consumed by a long and ruinous legal battle for the guardianship of his nephew Karl. And yet from this period came the most extraordinary creative outpouring of his life. The last five piano sonatas, the Diabelli Variations, the Missa Solemnis, the Ninth Symphony, and the five late string quartets — all produced between 1817 and 1826 by a man who could not hear a single note of what he wrote.

The Ninth Symphony in D minor, Op. 125, premiered on 7 May 1824, is the work for which he is most universally known. Its finale introduced a choir and soloists into a symphony for the first time, setting Schiller's Ode to Joy in a movement of overwhelming cumulative force. At the premiere, Beethoven stood on stage throughout, conducting from memory the score he could not hear. When the symphony ended, the audience erupted in applause he could not hear; one of the soloists turned him around so he could see it. He is said to have wept. Your collection includes Karajan's celebrated DG account CD 82 and Norrington's period-instrument reading on EMI CD 83.

Death and Immortality

Beethoven died on 26 March 1827 in Vienna, aged 56, of liver failure compounded by pleurisy and various other ailments. He had been ill for months, enduring four abdominal operations in the final weeks. His last documented words, on being told he had been sent twelve bottles of wine, were: “Pity — pity — too late.” There was a violent thunderstorm at the moment of death; a visitor reported that the dying man raised his fist at the lightning in a final defiant gesture. It may be apocryphal. It fits perfectly.

Twenty thousand people attended the funeral procession. Schubert — who had lived in the same city, revered Beethoven to the point of terror, and never become truly acquainted with him — was one of the torchbearers. He died the following year. Franz Grillparzer wrote the funeral oration: “He was an artist, and who shall stand beside him?”

Beethoven left no direct heirs and very little money. He left instead the most substantial and transformative body of work in the history of Western music — a body of work that defined what music could express, what forms it could take, what demands it could make on performer and listener alike. Every composer who came after him had to reckon with what he had done. Brahms spent twenty years writing his First Symphony in the knowledge that Beethoven had written nine. Wagner built an entire aesthetic on the idea that the Ninth had exhausted the possibilities of absolute music. Schoenberg began the dissolution of tonality that Beethoven's late quartets had half-anticipated. And the music itself — the Fifth, the late quartets, the Diabelli Variations, the Ninth — is still being heard, still being discovered, still yielding new meanings, two hundred years after it was written.

A Musical Portrait

George Frideric Handel

Life, Times & Music  ·  Halle 1685 – London 1759

The Year That Changed Music

In the year 1685 three of the supreme composers in the history of Western music were born within a few months of each other. Johann Sebastian Bach arrived in Eisenach in March; Domenico Scarlatti in Naples in October. And on 23 February, in the Saxon city of Halle, Georg Friedrich Händel — he would anglicise his name only after settling permanently in England — came into the world. The coincidence of birth years between Handel and Bach has fascinated posterity ever since: two composers of comparable genius, born in the same year in the same country, who never met, whose musical languages were entirely different, and whose careers could hardly have been more divergent. Bach spent his life in provincial German courts and churches; Handel conquered Europe, moved to London, became a naturalised Englishman, and transformed the musical life of a nation.

Halle in 1685 was a prosperous German town dominated by the salt trade and the Lutheran church. Handel's father, Georg Händel, was a barber-surgeon of considerable standing — a man who had risen by diligence and competence to serve at the ducal court — and he intended his youngest son for the law. Music, in his view, was no profession for a respectable man. The story goes that the young Georg Friedrich smuggled a clavichord into the attic of the family house and practised there by night, the instrument's soft tone too quiet to penetrate the floors below. Whether literally true or not, the anecdote captures something real: a musical gift so irresistible it could not be suppressed, and a family environment that offered no encouragement.

The father relented enough to allow music lessons, and when he took his son to visit his elder son — a valet at the court of the Duke of Saxe-Weissenfels — the eight-year-old Handel sat down at the chapel organ after a service and played so impressively that the Duke himself intervened and told Georg Händel senior that his son must study music. The father acquiesced. Handel was sent to study with Friedrich Wilhelm Zachow, the organist of the Marktkirche in Halle, who gave him a thorough grounding in counterpoint, harmony, and the keyboard music of the day. By the time he was eleven he was occasionally deputising for Zachow at the organ. He was already composing prolifically.

Hamburg and the Duel

When his father died in 1697, the twelve-year-old Handel was left in straitened circumstances. He enrolled briefly at Halle University to study law in 1702, in belated compliance with his father's wishes, but within a year had abandoned the pretence and moved to Hamburg — the one city in Germany with a public opera house — to seek his musical fortune. He was seventeen. He found work as a back-desk violinist in the opera orchestra, later graduating to harpsichordist, and began to learn the opera business from the inside.

Hamburg also gave him one of the more extraordinary episodes in a life not short of drama. He had become friendly with Johann Mattheson, a composer and musician of formidable talent and equally formidable ego. The two fell out after a performance of Mattheson's opera Cleopatra, in which Handel had been playing the harpsichord and declined to cede the instrument to the composer when Mattheson wished to conduct from it. Mattheson challenged him to a duel; they fought with swords in the street outside the opera house; and Handel survived only because Mattheson's blade broke against a large button on his coat. They were subsequently reconciled and remained on friendly terms. It is difficult to imagine such an episode in the life of Bach.

His two Hamburg operas — Almira and Nero — were produced in 1705. But Hamburg was a staging post, not a destination. He had fixed his sights on Italy.

Italy: The Crucial Apprenticeship

In 1706, probably supported by a Medici prince he had met in Hamburg, Handel travelled to Italy and spent the next three and a half years absorbing the Italian style at its source. He visited Florence, Rome, Venice and Naples; he met Corelli, Alessandro Scarlatti and the young Domenico Scarlatti; he heard Italian opera and oratorio performed by the best singers and players in Europe; and he composed with the furious productivity that never deserted him. The transformation was complete and permanent. He arrived in Italy a capable German musician; he left it a master of a cosmopolitan international style that drew on German counterpoint and Italian vocal lyricism in equal measure.

Rome was particularly fruitful. Pope Clement XI had banned public opera as morally objectionable, which redirected Handel's considerable operatic energies into the oratorio and the cantata. In April 1707 he composed the Dixit Dominus HWV 232, a setting of Psalm 110 of such ferocious energy and contrapuntal brilliance that it sounds unlike anything else written that decade. The double-choir passages are electrifying, the soprano writing practically impossible, and the final setting of “Gloria Patri” one of the most thrilling pages in all Baroque choral music. Your collection holds it in two recordings: the Archiv account with Simon Preston CD 225 and paired with the Bach Magnificat in a scintillating Virgin Classics disc CD 49. The Salve Regina and Nisi Dominus on CD 225 are equally fine early Roman works. The Carmelite Vespers of 1707, composed for the church of Santa Maria di Monte Santo CD 232, show the same astonishing command. He was twenty-two years old.

The Italian cantatas — secular vocal works, usually for solo voice and continuo, setting pastoral or amorous texts — poured out of him in these years. Your collection is rich in them: the Archiv Italian Cantatas with Jennifer Smith and John Elwes CD 227, the Marian Cantatas with Anne Sofie von Otter and Musica Antiqua Köln CD 230, and the Coro Live disc of Italian Cantatas and Arias from The Sixteen CD 224. His early Italian oratorio Il Trionfo del Tempo e del Disinganno — Time and Disillusion triumphant over Beauty and Pleasure — was composed for Rome in 1707 and shows the full scope of his dramatic ambitions already at work CD 204. So does La Resurrezione, a lavish Easter oratorio of 1708 that drew on the resources of the Marquis Ruspoli's household CD 207.

In Venice in 1709 he scored his first operatic triumph outside Hamburg: Agrippina, produced for the carnival season, received twenty-seven consecutive performances. The Venetians called him “il caro Sassone” — the dear Saxon. He was ready for London.

London: The Adopted Home

In 1710 Handel was appointed Kapellmeister to Georg Ludwig, Elector of Hanover — but the post was almost immediately treated as a sinecure, since he obtained leave to visit London almost at once and found the city irresistible. His opera Rinaldo (1711), the first Italian opera composed expressly for the London stage, was an immediate sensation. He returned to Hanover, obtained further leave, came back to London in 1712, and simply stayed — overstaying his leave by two years. When Queen Anne died in 1714 and the Elector of Hanover ascended the English throne as George I, Handel's position was temporarily awkward. The story that he composed the Water Music as an act of musical reconciliation — performed from a barge on the Thames for the King's river party in 1717 — is probably more legend than fact, but the result is undeniable: the Water Music suite became one of the most celebrated pieces of outdoor music ever written, and Handel's favour was restored CD 215. The same disc holds the Music for the Royal Fireworks, composed in 1749 for the Peace of Aix-la-Chapelle celebrations — originally for a band of wind instruments and drums, without strings, at the insistence of George II.

Cannons and the Chandos Anthems

Between roughly 1717 and 1720, Handel served as composer-in-residence to James Brydges, Earl of Carnarvon and later Duke of Chandos, at his magnificent palace at Cannons near Edgware. Brydges maintained a small but excellent musical establishment, and for it Handel produced eleven Chandos Anthems — settings of English psalm texts for chorus, soloists and small orchestra that are among the most immediately attractive works he ever wrote. Dignified and festive, learned but not dry, they show the German master adapting with extraordinary fluency to the English choral tradition. Your collection has all four volumes of The Sixteen's recordings under Harry Christophers CD 220 CD 221 CD 218 CD 219, and King's College Cambridge with David Willcocks provides a contrasting traditional account CD 226. Also at Cannons he produced Esther HWV 50 (1718), the work usually identified as the first English oratorio, in a private theatrical version CD 243.

The Opera Impresario

Opera was Handel's primary passion for the first two decades of his London career, and he pursued it with the energy and commercial ruthlessness of a born entrepreneur. In 1719 he co-founded the Royal Academy of Music — a joint-stock company financed by the aristocracy and dedicated to producing Italian opera seria at the King's Theatre in the Haymarket. He travelled to Germany and Italy to recruit the finest singers in Europe, including the castrato Senesino, whose employment would become one of the defining feuds of his later career.

The operas of the Royal Academy period (1719-1728) include some of his finest theatrical work: Radamisto (1720), Giulio Cesare (1724), Tamerlano (1724), Rodelinda (1725), Admeto (1727). Radamisto, your collection's earliest complete opera recording, features Joyce DiDonato and Patricia Ciofi in a star-studded Virgin Classics production CD 206. The opera Flavio, from 1723, survives in a landmark Harmonia Mundi recording with Jeffrey Gall and Derek Lee Ragin under William Christie — one of the recordings that revived serious scholarly interest in Handel's operas CD 203. And Il Pastor Fido (revised version 1734) shows the pastoral side of his operatic writing at its most refined CD 199. Atalanta of 1736 has its own Hungaroton recording in the collection CD 200.

The Academy collapsed in 1728, partly from internal feuding (the singers became more famous and more expensive than the company could afford), partly from the devastating success of John Gay's Beggar's Opera, which had satirised Italian opera seria so mercilessly that fashionable London could no longer take it entirely seriously. Handel persisted with a second company, then a third, suffering enormous financial losses. The opera wars of the 1730s — a rival Italian opera company, backed by the Prince of Wales in deliberate opposition to his father George II's support for Handel — brought him to bankruptcy and a stroke in 1737. He recovered. He always recovered.

The Turn to Oratorio

The collapse of his operatic ventures forced a creative reorientation that proved, in retrospect, the most fortunate thing that ever happened to Handel's career. As the London public tired of Italian opera, he turned increasingly to the English oratorio — a dramatic work on a biblical subject, sung in English, performed in the concert hall without scenery or costumes, by English singers. The form suited the English temperament, the Protestant tradition, the choral establishments of the country's churches, and the commercial realities of the London music market. Handel, ever the pragmatist, understood this completely and exploited it with characteristic brilliance.

The oratorios of the 1730s and 40s are in many ways the summit of his achievement. Saul (1738) — with its portrayal of jealousy, madness and tragedy that rivals Shakespearean drama — is perhaps the greatest of them all CD 242. Athalia (1733), composed for Oxford, gives Joan Sutherland and Emma Kirkby a chance to light up the title role and the virtuous queen respectively in a celebrated Decca recording CD 201. Esther in its revised 1732 London version is the collection's other Decca recording with Kirkby and Rolfe Johnson CD 202. Solomon (1748), with its ravishing double choruses and the famous “Arrival of the Queen of Sheba”, is one of the most opulent sounds in all his output CD 208. Judas Maccabaeus (1746), composed in the aftermath of the Jacobite rising of 1745 and tapping into a surge of patriotic fervour, was the biggest popular success of his career CD 231. And Jephtha (1751), his last oratorio, was composed as his eyesight failed catastrophically; a note in the manuscript in his own hand records that he was forced to stop work at a particular passage because his left eye had given out completely. The passage in question — the chorus “How dark, O Lord, are Thy decrees” — is a meditation on mortality, suffering and resignation that carries, in context, an almost unbearable personal weight CD 205.

The great odes complete the picture: Alexander's Feast (1736), setting Dryden's poem about the power of music, in the atmospheric Coro recording with The Sixteen CD 223; and the Ode for St Cecilia's Day (1739), another Dryden setting with Felicity Lott and Anthony Rolfe Johnson under Pinnock CD 229.

The Messiah

The Messiah was composed between 22 August and 14 September 1741 — twenty-three days of white-heat inspiration that produced one of the longest and most complex pieces in the choral repertoire. Handel had been invited to Dublin to direct a charitable benefit concert season; the Messiah, with its text compiled by Charles Jennens from the King James Bible and the Book of Common Prayer, was written for that occasion. Its Dublin premiere on 13 April 1742 was a triumph. The hall was so crowded that ladies had been asked not to wear hooped skirts and gentlemen to leave their swords at home in order to increase the audience capacity.

The London premiere in 1743 was cooler. The clergy objected to sacred texts being performed in a theatre; society found the idea of sitting quietly listening to scripture set to music vaguely improper. The King's decision to stand for the “Hallelujah” chorus — whether from religious fervour, stiff joints, or a desire to end the evening — established a tradition that British audiences maintain to this day. It took years for the Messiah to become the indispensable fixture of English musical life that it now is, but by the 1750s, Handel himself was conducting annual performances for the Foundling Hospital, whose chapel organ he had donated. The work became the foundation of his popular reputation.

Your collection holds four separate recordings — testimony to the work's inexhaustible fascination. The period-instrument Gardiner/Pinnock account on Philips with Margaret Marshall CD 195, the Scholars Baroque Ensemble on Naxos in an intimate chamber version CD 196, the landmark Gardiner Archiv recording with Arleen Augér and Anne Sofie von Otter CD 197, and the New College Oxford account with the Academy of Ancient Music under Edward Higginbottom CD 198. Each brings different qualities: the Archiv recording is probably the most celebrated period-instrument account of the twentieth century.

The Organ Concertos: A Personal Invention

Handel invented the organ concerto. Or rather: he was the first composer to make the form a major vehicle for public performance, and he did so in a characteristically entrepreneurial way. He would play an organ concerto as an interval entertainment between the acts of one of his oratorios, improvising freely within the written framework and displaying his own legendary keyboard gifts to a paying audience. The concertos were simultaneously crowd-pleasing entertainment and genuine artistic achievement — melodically inventive, formally varied, and full of the kind of florid passage-work that left contemporary audiences gasping.

Your collection has four organ concerto discs: Simon Preston with Menuhin on EMI CD 209, Ton Koopman's celebrated Erato recordings CD 210, and both opus sets in the authoritative Preston/Pinnock Archiv accounts — Op. 4 CD 211 and Op. 7 CD 212. Koopman in particular plays these pieces with a swagger and wit that recalls exactly the spirit of improvised theatre-interval entertainment in which they originated.

Chamber Music, Keyboard and the Vocal Recitals

Away from the public stage, Handel produced a body of chamber and instrumental music of quiet distinction. The Oboe Concertos and Sonatas show his feeling for the instrument's singing quality CD 214. The Recorder Sonatas, played by Pamela Thorby and Richard Egarr on Linn Records CD 216, are among the most immediately charming music he ever wrote. The vocal recitals in your collection illuminate his genius for the individual voice. The repertoire is beginning to receive the serious performance attention it deserves.

The Final Years

By 1751 Handel was going blind, probably from cataracts. The same surgeon who later operated on Bach tried to restore his sight; the results in both cases were catastrophic. His last years were spent revising earlier works and conducting performances of the oratorios from memory, guided by his amanuensis John Christopher Smith Jr., who had taken over much of the practical management of his musical affairs. He attended a performance of the Messiah on 6 April 1759, eight days before his death on 14 April. He died peacefully, in the early hours of Good Friday, at his house in Brook Street, London. He had requested burial in Westminster Abbey, and there he lies, in Poets' Corner, beneath a Roubiliac statue that is one of the finest monuments in the building — the composer caught in the act of writing the “I know that my Redeemer liveth.”

He left an estate of approximately £20,000 — a very considerable fortune, much of it accumulated from his oratorio performances — most of which went to family, servants, and charitable bequests. He left the organ in the Foundling Hospital chapel, the plates of his published music, and a musical legacy that has not stopped growing in esteem since the day he died. The Handel commemoration concerts of 1784, held in Westminster Abbey with an orchestra and chorus of over 500 performers, were the largest musical events England had ever seen. He was, and remains, the most completely adopted foreign composer in the history of British music.

A Musical Guide

The Development of Sonata Form

From Scarlatti to Brahms  ·  c.1740 – c.1890

What Is Sonata Form?

Sonata form is the most important formal structure in the history of Western classical music. It is the architecture used in the first movements of virtually every symphony, concerto, string quartet and piano sonata written between roughly 1760 and 1900. Understanding it does not require musical literacy — it requires only the ability to listen for contrast, tension and resolution, which is instinctive in most people. And once recognised, it transforms the experience of this music: you hear not just a succession of beautiful sounds, but an argument with a beginning, a middle and an end.

The form has three sections. In the Exposition, the composer presents two contrasting musical ideas. The first, in the home key (the tonic), tends to be firmer and more assertive; the second, in a related key (usually the dominant — five notes higher), tends to be more lyrical and relaxed. A connecting passage called the bridge modulates between them, and a closing section rounds things off. You have heard the first theme and the second theme, and you have moved away from home. In the Development, the composer takes those themes apart: fragments them, combines them, runs them through unexpected keys, intensifies them, puts them under pressure. This is where the drama lies — the sense of uncertainty, of struggle, of things being worked out. Then comes the Recapitulation: the first theme returns in the home key, then the second theme — crucially, now also in the home key rather than the dominant. The tension is resolved; you are home. Often there is a Coda (Italian for “tail”) that rounds off the movement with a final emphatic confirmation of the key.

That is the skeleton. What the great composers did with it — and how it changed across 150 years — is one of the central stories of musical history.

Precursors: Scarlatti and the Binary Seed

The seeds of sonata form lie in the Baroque binary form used in dance movements and keyboard pieces — a simple two-part structure, each half repeated, in which the first part moves to the dominant and the second returns home. Domenico Scarlatti (1685-1757), who was born in the same year as both Handel and Bach and spent much of his career at the Spanish court writing keyboard music for the Queen, composed 555 single-movement keyboard sonatas in binary form. They are not sonata form — there is no separate development section, no independent second theme as such — but in their harmonic adventures, their dramatic contrasts of texture and mood, and their journey away from and back to the home key, they contain the genetic material from which sonata form would develop. Murray Perahia's Sony disc pairs Handel and Scarlatti on the piano CD 213 — an illuminating juxtaposition of two musical worlds, both born the same year, both pointing forward to the Classical era in different ways.

The crucial missing ingredient in Baroque binary form was the concept of thematic contrast: two genuinely different musical characters inhabiting the same movement, set against each other. That idea arrived with the generation of composers born around 1710-1735 — above all Carl Philipp Emanuel Bach, Johann Stamitz, and the composers of the Mannheim school — who developed what they called the empfindsamer Stil (sensitive style): music of sudden contrasts, dramatic silences, and immediate emotional expressivity. C.P.E. Bach's keyboard sonatas in particular show an embryonic sonata form, with recognisable contrasting themes and something approaching a development section. Both Haydn and Mozart acknowledged him as a decisive influence. “He is the father,” Mozart said, “we are the children.”

Haydn: Crystallising the Convention

It was Joseph Haydn who brought all these tendencies together into a coherent, teachable, endlessly flexible formal procedure. Over roughly thirty years of intense compositional experimentation — mostly in the relative isolation of the Esterházy court in Hungary, where he had an orchestra to test his ideas with — he developed and refined sonata form across all the major genres: the symphony, the string quartet, the piano sonata, and the piano trio. By his middle period (roughly the 1770s and 80s) the form was established with sufficient clarity that it could be described, taught, and recognised by audiences. The great theorist Heinrich Koch published the first clear textbook account of it in 1782-93, citing Haydn as his primary example.

But Haydn was never a formalist. His great achievement was to make the formal conventions feel not like a cage but like a stage set on which endlessly surprising things could happen. His humour — the false recapitulations that fool the listener into thinking the development is over, the unexpected silences, the sudden pianissimo after a long forte build-up — depends entirely on the listener having internalised the formal expectation that is then subverted. The “Surprise” Symphony works as a joke only because the audience knows the rules. His Piano Sonatas of the 1780s and 90s, collected on Naxos CD 241, show the form at its most compact and witty. The String Quartets Op. 76 — written in his late sixties and among the finest things he ever wrote — show it at its most expansive and profound CD 240. The Military Symphony No. 100 CD 239 shows the form in its most public, orchestral guise: the first movement's clear exposition, dramatic development and satisfying recapitulation is a textbook example.

Mozart: Perfection Without Strain

Mozart absorbed Haydn's formal innovations and applied to them a musical intelligence of a different order: more instinctive, more lyrical, more harmonically subtle. Where Haydn's formal wit is external — you can hear him playing with the conventions — Mozart's formal mastery is invisible. The sonata form movements are so perfectly proportioned, so naturally flowing, that it is easy to miss the technical command underneath. “Too many notes,” the Emperor Joseph II reportedly said after the premiere of Die Entführung aus dem Serail. “Exactly as many as are needed, Your Majesty,” Mozart replied.

In Mozart's sonata form movements, several things are characteristic. The development sections tend to be relatively brief — not because he was lazy, but because he preferred formal elegance to dramatic struggle. His energies went into the exposition and recapitulation, where the themes themselves are so richly characterised that development is almost superfluous. The transitions (bridges between themes) are often as interesting as the themes themselves. And the relationship between the two main themes is often one of complement rather than contrast: in many Mozart sonata-form movements, the second theme feels like the lyrical fulfilment of the energy established by the first, rather than a dramatic counterweight to it.

His piano sonatas — collected complete on Philips and other labels CD 294, with individual discs including the famous K.545 “easy” sonata CD 289 and Mikhail Pletnev's searching accounts CD 290 — are the most accessible entry point to his sonata form thinking. The Piano Concertos are more complex: here Mozart took the form and adapted it to the dialogue between soloist and orchestra. He established a double exposition (the orchestra plays through the themes first, then the soloist enters and plays them again, transformed), and the interplay between the solo line and the orchestral accompaniment generates a dramatic counterpoint unique to the concerto genre. The concertos numbered 20-27 are the summit of his achievement in the form: the D minor K.466 CD 293, the A major K.488, the C minor K.491 CD 307 and the final B flat major K.595 CD 311 each represent a different emotional and formal world, but all are governed by the same serene formal intelligence. The string quintets — your collection has both volumes CD 303 CD 304 — show sonata form in chamber music at its most refined and inward.

Beethoven: The Form Under Pressure

If Mozart perfected the Classical inheritance, Beethoven subjected it to stresses it had never previously encountered. He did not abandon sonata form — he used it throughout his career, even in the most radical late works — but he systematically expanded, dramatised and ultimately transformed every element of it. The difference between a Mozart sonata-form movement and a Beethoven one is the difference between a well-proportioned Georgian townhouse and a Gothic cathedral: both are organised on coherent architectural principles, but the scale, the emotional atmosphere and the relationship between parts and whole are entirely different.

Consider the development sections. In a typical Mozart first movement, the development might last fifty bars out of a total of perhaps 250. In the first movement of Beethoven's Eroica Symphony, the development runs to nearly 250 bars — longer than the exposition — and traverses harmonic territory (including the famous entry of a completely new theme, unprecedented in the form) that leaves the listener genuinely disoriented before the triumphal return of the opening theme. The development is no longer a passage between exposition and recapitulation; it is the dramatic heart of the movement.

Consider the codas. Mozart's codas round off a movement that has already reached its conclusion. Beethoven's codas are frequently second development sections, as long and as harmonically adventurous as the first. In the Fifth Symphony CD 96 CD 97, the coda of the first movement is a ferocious final working-out of the four-note motto that has driven the movement from its opening bar. The recapitulation alone does not provide sufficient resolution; the coda must fight its way through to a definitive conclusion.

Consider the thematic material itself. Mozart's themes tend to be complete melodic statements: you can hum them. Beethoven's themes are often motivic fragments deliberately designed to be developed. The first “theme” of the Fifth Symphony is not a melody at all — it is a rhythmic cell of four notes that generates everything that follows. The first theme of the Eroica's first movement is a bare descending arpeggio, chosen precisely because it is so neutral that it can be combined with anything. These are not tunes; they are seeds.

The Razumovsky Quartets Op. 59 CD 77 show these innovations in chamber music: the first movement of Op. 59 No. 1 is a scale-expanding masterwork, its development section so extended that contemporary listeners could not follow where they were. The Hammerklavier Sonata Op. 106 CD 87 represents the most extreme expansion of piano sonata form ever attempted: a first movement lasting nearly twenty minutes, with a development section of towering complexity, a fugal finale that dissolves the very notion of sonata form by replacing it with something more ancient — and in doing so, points directly towards the late quartets CD 78 CD 79, where sonata form is retained as a structural skeleton but is animated by an emotional and harmonic language so far from Classical conventions as to be unrecognisable. András Schiff playing the sonatas on Beethoven's own Broadwood piano CD 93 reveals how differently these works sounded on the instrument for which they were conceived.

The Mozart-Beethoven Comparison: A Practical Guide

The contrast is most vivid when you hear comparable works side by side. Take the first movements of Mozart's Piano Concerto No. 20 in D minor K.466 CD 293 and Beethoven's Piano Concerto No. 3 in C minor Op. 37 CD 74. Both are in the minor key; both are dramatic and serious in tone; Beethoven was clearly thinking of the Mozart when he wrote his. Yet the differences are fundamental. Mozart's first movement runs to about 350 bars; Beethoven's to nearly 500. Mozart's development is swift and purposeful; Beethoven's is a long, turbulent journey through multiple keys. Mozart's recapitulation feels like a peaceful homecoming; Beethoven's feels hard-won. Most tellingly: Mozart's movement ends with a question — a mysterious pianissimo that dissolves into the piano's cadenza — while Beethoven's ends with a decisive, emphatic assertion of the tonic key. Mozart lives with ambiguity; Beethoven insists on resolution.

Or compare their violin sonatas. Mozart's were originally conceived as “piano sonatas with violin accompaniment” — the piano is primary, the violin decorative. Your collection includes multiple volumes CD 324 CD 325. In Beethoven's sonatas, the violin and piano are equals, and their dialogue — sometimes collaborative, sometimes genuinely argumentative — is itself a structural device. The Kreutzer Sonata Op. 47 CD 101, with its massive first movement and its blazing finale, sounds like a symphony compressed into two instruments. Tolstoy wrote a novella about the psychological effects of listening to it.

Schubert: Time Transformed

Schubert's relationship with sonata form is perhaps the most individual of any major composer. He inherited the same formal conventions as Beethoven, but his musical imagination operated on entirely different principles. Where Beethoven's forms are driven by motivic conflict and resolution — a drama of struggle and triumph — Schubert's are driven by harmonic colour and lyrical flow. His second themes are not complementary ideas in the dominant but often completely independent songs in unexpected keys: the flat submediant (a third below the tonic) is one of his favourite harmonic moves, and it gives his sonata-form movements a characteristic sense of stepping sideways into a different light.

His development sections do not typically fragment and transform the themes dramatically; instead, they explore long harmonic journeys, drifting through remote keys with a sense of being beautifully, dreamily lost. Schumann described the Great C major Symphony CD 392 as having “heavenly length” — not a criticism but a description of a different relationship to musical time, in which the journey matters as much as the destination. The first movement of the Great C major, with its vast spans and unhurried unfolding, is Schubertian sonata form at its most characteristic: enormous, patient, luminous.

The late piano sonatas CD 386 take the form still further from Beethoven: the great B-flat major Sonata D.960 (not in this volume but recorded by Richter among others) unfolds its first movement in a state of profound contemplative stillness quite unlike anything in Beethoven's sonatas. The Death and the Maiden Quartet D.810 CD 402 CD 407 and the String Quintet D.956 CD 401 apply Schubertian sonata thinking to chamber music: in the Quintet's first movement, the formal landmarks are there — exposition, development, recapitulation — but they are navigated at a pace and with a lyrical generosity that feels entirely unlike Beethoven. The result is not lesser; it is different.

Brahms: The Conservative Revolutionary

Johannes Brahms occupies a special place in the history of sonata form because he was simultaneously the most reverent and the most creative inheritor of the Classical tradition. He worshipped Beethoven to the point of paralysis: he spent twenty-one years writing and destroying attempts at his First Symphony, haunted by the fear of what Beethoven had done and what it left for anyone else to do. When the First Symphony finally appeared in 1876 CD 125, it was immediately described as “Beethoven's Tenth” — a description Brahms found exasperating but understandable.

His distinctive contribution to sonata form was the technique Arnold Schoenberg later called developing variation: a process in which the themes are not dramatically contrasted and then developed in a separate section, but are continuously varied and transformed throughout the movement, so that exposition, development and recapitulation blur into a single unbroken process of metamorphosis. His violin sonatas CD 119 and cello sonatas CD 124 are particularly fine examples of this technique in chamber music. The Piano Concerto No. 1 in D minor CD 116 — originally sketched as a symphony — is one of the most densely wrought sonata-form movements in the repertoire: its development section is a labyrinth of motivic transformation in which every element of the first movement's material is continuously reinvented. The Fourth Symphony Op. 98 CD 118 CD 122, its finale a set of variations on a Baroque bass line, shows Brahms reaching back even further into musical history — past Beethoven, past Mozart, to the contrapuntal foundations on which all Western formal music rests.

Brahms also represents the end of a chapter. After him, the pressures on sonata form became irresistible. Wagner had proposed that “endless melody” — a continuous, harmonically unstable flow with no clear formal articulation — was the musical future. Liszt had invented the symphonic poem, in which narrative programme took the place of abstract formal structure. By 1890, the perfectly proportioned Classical sonata-form movement that Haydn had crystallised and Mozart had perfected seemed to belong to another world. What came after is another story. But the achievement of those hundred and fifty years — from Haydn's early quartets to Brahms's Fourth Symphony — represents one of the most sustained and coherent formal traditions in any art form in history.

A Musical History

The History of Chamber Music

Four Centuries of Intimate Conversation  ·  c.1600 – present

The Intimate Art

Chamber music is music made by a small group of players, each with their own independent part, in a room rather than a concert hall. The word “chamber” is simply a translation of the Italian camera — a room, a private space, as opposed to the church or the theatre. For most of its history, chamber music was not public at all: it was made by and for the people performing it, in the drawing rooms and salons of the educated classes, as a form of civilised entertainment that was also a form of intellectual discipline. You had to be a competent musician to participate. The pleasure was participatory as much as it was auditory.

This origin shapes everything about chamber music's character. Because it was private, it could be adventurous. Because it was intimate, it could be subtle. Because there was no conductor, no single controlling authority, it was genuinely conversational — the four players of a string quartet engage with each other as equals, passing ideas between them, agreeing, disagreeing, interrupting, completing each other's sentences. The greatest chamber music in the repertoire has the quality of overheard thought: the composer thinking aloud, in dialogue with himself, at a depth and candour that public music rarely achieves.

Baroque Origins: The Sonata da Camera

The distinction between music for the church (sonata da chiesa) and music for the chamber (sonata da camera) crystallised in the seventeenth century. The church sonata was grave, contrapuntal, serious; the chamber sonata was lighter, dance-based, entertaining. Both were typically written for two treble instruments (usually violins) and a continuo group (harpsichord or organ plus a bass instrument), a texture known as the trio sonata. Despite the name, three players are the minimum only if the continuo player is counted once; in practice four players were common.

Handel's Seven Trio Sonatas for Two Violins HWV 386-391, played by The Brook Street Band CD 244, are a late and magnificent example of the form: suave, elegant, harmonically adventurous, shot through with the Italian lyricism Handel absorbed in Rome. Telemann, prolific beyond all modern imagining, left an equally large body of chamber music that ranges from the formally strict to the exuberantly experimental; his Sonatas, Suites and Small Ensemble Works with Heinz Holliger CD 447 show how inventively the Baroque period exploited wind instruments in chamber textures. And the Sirena Recorder Quartet's disc of Vivaldi, Bach, Boismortier and others CD 510 is a reminder of how naturally the Baroque idiom suited consorts of wind instruments, a tradition stretching back to the Renaissance.

J.S. Bach left no music explicitly labelled as chamber music, but his six Sonatas and Partitas for Solo Violin BWV 1001-1006 are among the supreme achievements of the repertoire: a single instrument made to carry the weight of implied polyphony, dance, and philosophical argument simultaneously. Monica Huggett's period-instrument account CD 17 and Nathan Milstein's legendary 1975 Deutsche Grammophon recording CD 28 represent the two ends of interpretative history: historically informed performance and the grand modern tradition respectively. His cello sonatas with Martha Argerich in both the Maisky recordings CD 29 show Bach's chamber writing at its most intimate and searching.

Haydn: The Father of the String Quartet

The string quartet — two violins, viola and cello — is the central form of chamber music, and Joseph Haydn effectively invented it. Not in a single act of creation, but through decades of patient experiment: his early divertimenti for four strings gradually acquired structural coherence, thematic discipline, and formal sophistication until, by the Op. 33 quartets of 1781, the form was essentially established. Haydn himself was aware of the significance. In the Op. 33 preface he claimed they were written “in a new and special manner”, and he was right.

What was new was the democratic equality of the four voices. In earlier chamber music, the top line (first violin) was primary and the others were accompaniment. In Haydn's mature quartets, the melodic interest passes freely between all four instruments; the cello may carry the tune while the violins provide texture; the viola may initiate a development that the first violin then transforms. The quartet became a conversation of four individuals rather than a monologue with three supporters. His late Op. 76 set, recorded by the Kodály Quartet CD 240, represents the summit of his chamber achievement: the “Fifths” quartet's opening with its plunging intervals, the “Emperor” quartet's variations on what became the Austrian national anthem, the “Sunrise” quartet's glowing first movement. These are works written by a man of sixty-five at the height of his powers, with nothing to prove and everything to express.

Boccherini, Haydn's near-contemporary and occasional rival, brought his own elegant, slightly melancholy Italian voice to the string quintet — a form he preferred to the quartet, usually adding a second cello to give the texture a richer bass. His String Quintets Op. 37 played by Ensemble Explorations CD 111 are the finest examples: long-spun melodies over beautifully judged accompaniments, with a charm that can suddenly tip into genuine pathos. The familiar Boccherini Minuet appears in context on a broader survey disc CD 114.

Mozart: The Perfected Conversation

Mozart received the form from Haydn (his six quartets dedicated to Haydn are his explicit acknowledgement of the debt) and brought to it qualities that Haydn, for all his genius, could not quite match: a melodic fertility that seems literally inexhaustible, a harmonic sensitivity of almost painful acuity, and — perhaps most importantly for chamber music — an ability to write for four instruments as if they were four characters in an opera, each with a distinct personality and something urgent to say.

His string quintets, adding a second viola to the quartet, are by common consent the summit of his chamber output. The extra voice adds warmth and harmonic richness, and Mozart exploits it with an ease that makes the writing seem inevitable. The two volumes of his complete quintets tell the full story CD 303 CD 304 — from the G minor K.516 (perhaps his most emotionally complex chamber work) to the radiant C major K.515, the late quintet form serving him as flexibly as the symphony or the concerto. His Clarinet Quintet K.581, written for his friend Anton Stadler, added a completely new tonal colour to the chamber palette; your collection holds it alongside the Brahms Clarinet Quintet in the Nash Ensemble's distinguished recording CD 302, and again in Thea King's account with the English Chamber Orchestra CD 315.

His Piano and Wind Quintet K.452, which he described in a letter to his father as the best thing he had ever written, opened up still another sound world: piano with oboe, clarinet, horn and bassoon, a combination of startling freshness CD 327. The late piano trios K.542, K.548 and K.564 CD 285 show chamber writing at its most transparent and conversational; and the violin sonatas, in Anne-Sophie Mutter's authoritative recordings CD 324 CD 325, chart the evolution of the duo sonata from a keyboard piece with optional violin to a genuine partnership of equals.

Beethoven: From Dialogue to Drama

What Beethoven did to chamber music is analogous to what he did to the symphony: he took a form designed for polite entertainment or refined pleasure, and made it bear the full weight of human experience. His early string quartets are clearly in Haydn's tradition; by the Razumovsky Quartets Op. 59 (1806), written for a Russian count in Vienna who maintained a professional quartet, the form had been stretched to accommodate something that contemporaries found baffling and that we can now recognise as one of the most dramatic achievements in all chamber music. The Lindsay String Quartet's recording captures their argumentative intensity CD 77.

His late quartets, composed in the last three years of his life while he was completely deaf, represent the most extraordinary single body of chamber music ever written. The Op. 131 in C sharp minor runs seven movements without a break; the Op. 132 in A minor contains a slow movement headed “Holy song of thanksgiving from a convalescent to the Divinity” that achieves a stillness outside the normal categories of musical experience; the Op. 135 asks, in a motto prefixed to the finale, “Must it be? — It must be!”, which may be comic or may be the most compressed philosophical statement in music. The Quartetto Italiano's celebrated Philips recordings of both volumes remain a touchstone CD 78 CD 79, while the Amadeus Quartet illuminates Op. 130 and the Grosse Fuge CD 88 — that extraordinary fugue Beethoven originally appended to Op. 130, which his publisher persuaded him to publish separately because it was too difficult and too long.

His duo sonatas are equally transformative. The violin sonatas — including the monumental Kreutzer Op. 47, with Perlman and Ashkenazy CD 101, and Szeryng and Haebler in the complete set CD 98 — redefined what two instruments could express together. The cello sonatas, covered in Jacqueline du Pré's legendary EMI recordings CD 73 CD 99, are the foundation of the cello's chamber repertoire.

Schubert: The Lyrical Revolution

Schubert's chamber music is the most personal and emotionally direct he ever wrote, which is saying a great deal. He composed prolifically in the medium throughout his short life, and his late chamber works — written in the last three years before his death at thirty-one — are among the most beloved pieces in the entire repertoire.

The String Quintet in C major D.956, adding a second cello rather than Boccherini's second cello (the same choice, but the effect is entirely different), was written in the last weeks of his life and is by many musicians' reckoning the most profound single piece of chamber music ever written. Its Adagio — sustained, luminous, achingly beautiful — seems to belong to a different dimension of experience from ordinary concert music. The Alban Berg Quartet with Heinrich Schiff gives the definitive account CD 401, and the Belcea Quartet with Valentin Erben offers a more recent perspective CD 408.

The String Quartet No. 14 ‘Death and the Maiden’ D.810 takes its nickname from the Lied of the same name whose theme forms the basis of the slow movement variations: the gaunt, repeated chords of Death answering the girl's pleas make for one of the most immediately gripping quartet movements ever conceived. The Lindsays CD 402 CD 407 and the Alban Berg Quartet CD 404 are the recordings to know. The Late Chamber Music for Strings, including the G major Quartet D.887 in the Brandis Quartet's recording CD 397 and the Fitzwilliam Quartet's account CD 406, complete the picture of his string writing.

The Piano Quintet ‘The Trout’ D.667 — scored unusually for piano, violin, viola, cello and double bass, with variations on his own song in the fourth movement — is among the sunniest and most immediately appealing things he ever wrote, a complete contrast to the late quartets' weight. Your collection holds it in four recordings: Brendel with the Cleveland Quartet CD 390, The Schubert Ensemble in two different accounts CD 399 CD 403, and Brendel again with Zehetmair CD 405. The two piano trios CD 395 and the expansive Octet D.803 for clarinet, horn, bassoon, two violins, viola, cello and double bass CD 410 further demonstrate how naturally the chamber medium suited his imagination.

Brahms: Guardian of the Tradition

Brahms composed in every chamber medium — string sextets, piano quartets, piano quintets, violin sonatas, cello sonatas, clarinet quintet — with an equal command and a consistent seriousness of purpose. He was the great conservative of Romantic music, the composer who believed that the formal traditions of Haydn, Mozart and Beethoven were still capable of being extended, refined and deepened without being abandoned. His chamber music is the proof of that belief.

His three Violin Sonatas — played by Leonidas Kavakos and Yuja Wang with blazing conviction CD 119 — are the finest since Beethoven's: the first, nicknamed ‘Rain’ from the use of his own song “Regenlied”, is perhaps the most immediately beautiful; the third, in D minor, is the most dramatic. The two Cello Sonatas with Yo-Yo Ma and Emanuel Ax CD 124 are the heart of the cello's Romantic repertoire. And the Clarinet Quintet Op. 115, written at the end of his life when he had decided to stop composing but was persuaded back by the playing of the clarinettist Richard Mühlfeld, is a work of autumnal beauty that pairs ideally with Mozart's in the Nash Ensemble's recording CD 302. The Piano Quartet No. 3 Op. 60 on the double concerto disc CD 126 shows his piano chamber writing at its grandest and most turbulent.

The Nationalists: New Voices in Old Forms

The late nineteenth century saw composers from across Europe bring their national folk traditions into the chamber music idiom, enriching it with new rhythms, new modal harmonies, and new emotional directness that the Austro-German mainstream sometimes lacked.

Dvořák is the supreme figure here. His chamber music is among the most immediately lovable in the repertoire: the Piano Trios, especially the F minor Op. 65 and the ‘Dumky’ Op. 90 with its alternating slow and fast Czech dance movements, both recorded by the Joachim Trio CD 161; the Piano Quartet No. 2 Op. 87 with András Schiff CD 162; and the Piano Quintet No. 2 Op. 81 CD 170, whose opening melody is one of the most perfectly shaped in all chamber music. Smetana's String Quartet No. 1 ‘From My Life’, written after he had gone deaf and incorporating a piercing high E in the final movement representing the ringing in his ears, is the most personal of all nationalist chamber works — raw autobiography in musical form, played by the Pavel Haas Quartet CD 438.

Grieg's Cello Sonata in A minor CD 182 brings Norwegian lyricism to the duo sonata. Elgar, working in the English tradition that had been largely dormant for two centuries, produced a String Quartet and Piano Quintet of unexpected emotional depth in 1918, near the end of his creative life, recorded by the Medici Quartet and John Bingham CD 174. Vaughan Williams contributed two String Quartets rooted in English folksong and modal harmony, recorded by the Medici String Quartet CD 487. And the young Juan Crisóstomo de Arriaga — a Basque prodigy who died at twenty, having written three string quartets that hold their own in the Classical tradition — is represented in the Guarneri Quartet's recording CD 2.

The Twentieth Century: Chamber Music Survives Everything

The upheavals of the twentieth century — atonality, serialism, neo-classicism, minimalism — all posed challenges to the chamber music tradition, and the tradition answered all of them. The string quartet in particular proved almost indestructible as a vehicle for serious compositional thought.

Shostakovich's fifteen string quartets are the most significant body of chamber music written in the second half of the twentieth century. Composed across forty years under the pressure of Soviet cultural politics, they form a sustained private diary that the symphonies, with their public ambition, could not be. The famous Eighth Quartet Op. 110 — ostensibly a memorial to victims of fascism, actually a deeply personal meditation on his own fate — quotes his own works, Beethoven, and Russian folk songs in a collage of anguished memory. Ligeti's chamber works — including the two String Quartets and the Chamber Concerto under Boulez CD 262 — show how the post-war avant-garde could push chamber music into entirely new sonic territory without losing its capacity to communicate. The Art of Fugue

Bach, Counterpoint & the Architecture of Musical Thought  ·  c.1400 – present

What is a Fugue?

A fugue begins with a single voice, alone. It states a short melodic idea — the subject — complete and unaccompanied. Then a second voice enters and states the same subject, usually a fifth higher, while the first voice continues with a new melody that fits against it. A third voice enters with the subject again, and a fourth, each one joining a texture of ever-increasing density and complexity. By the time all the voices have entered, something extraordinary is happening: four independent melodic lines, each saying something different, are fitting together with perfect logic, like the interlocking parts of a mechanism that was designed to be exactly this shape. The opening section — the exposition — is complete.

What follows in a fugue is a series of episodes (passages where the subject temporarily disappears, the voices exploring the melodic material freely) and entries (where the subject returns, sometimes in new keys, sometimes upside down, sometimes at double speed or half speed, sometimes played simultaneously against its own mirror image). The goal is always to bring the voices together in a way that feels both inevitable and surprising — an intellectual architecture that generates emotional heat.

This is counterpoint: the art of combining independent melodic lines. It had been the central technique of Western music for three centuries before Bach, and he brought it to a perfection that has never been surpassed and has barely been equalled.

Before Bach: The Long Lineage

Counterpoint has its roots in the medieval practice of adding new voices to plainchant, and the Renaissance polyphony of Palestrina, Lassus and Byrd refined it into an art of grave beauty. The fugue as a specific form emerged gradually during the seventeenth century from the ricercar (literally, a searching or seeking — a term that Bach would use again in the Musical Offering) and the canzona. Pachelbel, whose Canon appears as a little harmonic exercise on a disc of Baroque favourites in your collection CD 3, was one of Bach's teachers' teachers and a significant figure in the development of keyboard counterpoint in Germany. Buxtehude, the great Danish-German organist whose playing Bach famously walked two hundred miles to hear, brought the organ toccata and fugue to a dramatic intensity that directly shaped what Bach would do with the form.

But it is Georg Philipp Telemann who stands as the most interesting transitional figure — a near-contemporary of Bach who was regarded in their own time as the greater composer, and who brought contrapuntal technique to ensemble music with a lightness and variety that Bach never quite matched in that medium CD 447. History's reassessment has reversed their relative reputations almost completely.

Bach's Contrapuntal Universe

For Bach, counterpoint was not a technique but a way of thinking. It permeated everything he wrote: the harmonisation of a chorale, the working out of a concerto ritornello, the construction of a da capo aria. But it found its most concentrated and self-conscious expression in three works that form a trilogy of contrapuntal summits: the Well-Tempered Clavier, the Musical Offering, and the Art of Fugue.

The Well-Tempered Clavier — a prelude and fugue in every major and minor key, assembled in two books of twenty-four pairs — is the foundation of the keyboard repertoire and the most important single set of pieces in the Western tradition. Book One was assembled around 1722, partly as a teaching collection; Book Two followed in the 1740s, more varied and more demanding. The title refers to a system of keyboard tuning that allows all twenty-four keys to be used without any sounding unacceptably out of tune — the “well-tempered” tuning is not quite our modern equal temperament, and different keys retain slightly different characters, which Bach exploits with systematic imagination. Angela Hewitt's complete recording brings the pianist's natural lyricism to music that can sound merely mechanical in lesser hands CD 7. András Schiff's separate accounts of each book are among the most searching ever made CD 8 CD 9. And Sviatoslav Richter's legendary 1969 recordings have a architectural grandeur all their own CD 10.

What makes the WTC inexhaustible is the variety of fugue types it contains. The C major fugue of Book One is in three voices and almost Mozartian in its clarity; the C sharp minor is a vast five-voice construction of immense gravity; the B flat minor of Book One is a four-voice meditation of almost unendurable beauty. Each of the forty-eight is a distinct personality. Kerman's book on Bach's keyboard fugues argues that this variety is the point — that Bach was demonstrating not a formula but a capacity, showing that the fugue was not a constraint but an inexhaustibly flexible vehicle for musical thought.

The organ toccatas and fugues are a different matter entirely: public, extrovert, thrilling. The Toccata and Fugue in D minor BWV 565 — almost certainly Bach's most famous piece, though its attribution has been questioned — deploys fugal technique for maximum dramatic effect, the repeated entries of the subject building tension across the full compass of the instrument. Simon Preston's Archiv recordings present both the famous toccatas and the grander Preludes and Fugues BWV 532 and 552 CD 62 CD 515, while Ton Koopman explores a wider selection of the organ works CD 61. The Prelude and Fugue in E flat major BWV 552 — the ‘St Anne’ — is the grandest of all, its three-section fugue combining three subjects in the final section with a confidence that seems almost casual.

Fugue appears throughout Bach's other keyboard collections. The Two-Part Inventions and Sinfonias are the elementary schoolroom — short two- and three-voice pieces teaching the student how voices can be made to imitate and combine CD 18. The Partitas — suites of dance movements — incorporate fugal techniques into their overtures and gigues CD 19 CD 20 CD 21. And the English Suites open with full-scale preludes that are essentially concerto movements built on imitative counterpoint CD 24.

Even the solo violin and cello works are not exempt. The Sonatas for solo violin contain full four-voice fugues written for a single instrument — an apparently impossible task that Bach solves by implying the absent voices through careful melodic construction, chord-breaking, and the strategic placement of double-stops CD 17 CD 28. The Chaconne in D minor from Partita No. 2, heard in Ida Haendel's account CD 217, is a set of variations on a ground bass in which fugal imitation is one of many techniques used to sustain a vast architectural span of fifteen uninterrupted minutes.

The Art of Fugue: The Summit

Die Kunst der Fuge BWV 1080 is Bach's last and strangest masterpiece, and one of the most discussed works in the entire repertoire. It is a systematic demonstration of virtually every contrapuntal device known to Baroque theory, all built on a single subject of deceptive simplicity: a plain D minor scale, rising and falling, that Bach then proceeds to turn upside down, run in reverse, diminish, augment, combine against its own mirror image, set in stretto (entries overlapping before the subject is complete), and work through every type of fugue and canon the tradition had accumulated over two centuries.

It consists of fourteen fugues (called Contrapuncti in the original publication) and four canons, arranged in an order of increasing complexity. The opening Contrapunctus I is almost austere in its plainness: a simple four-voice fugue in D minor, the subject stated calmly, the voices entering one by one. By Contrapunctus IV Bach is running the subject against its own inversion simultaneously; by Contrapunctus VIII he is combining three independent subjects in triple counterpoint; by Contrapunctus XIV — the last and unfinished one — he has introduced a third subject that spells out in the German musical letter names B-A-C-H (B flat, A, C, B natural), as if signing his name to what was clearly intended as a final testament.

The work breaks off in the middle of Contrapunctus XIV, 239 bars in, mid-phrase. Bach's son Carl Philipp Emanuel added a note to the first edition explaining that the composer had died while working on this fugue. Whether this is literally true, or whether a completed version exists somewhere, has been debated for two and a half centuries. The incompleteness has become part of the work's identity: it ends not with a conclusion but with a question, the final chord hanging in the air, the most complex fugue yet attempted suddenly silent.

There is also a mystery about how the work should be performed. Bach specified no instruments. The original publication presented it on two staves, like a keyboard score, but it has been argued — compellingly — that the spacing of the voices is often unplayable on a single keyboard, suggesting it was conceived as an abstract demonstration rather than a practical performance piece. In practice it has been performed on solo harpsichord or piano, by string quartets, by chamber ensembles, by brass groups, and by small orchestras. Each medium reveals different aspects. The work itself neither endorses nor excludes any of them.

Beethoven's Furious Answer: The Grosse Fuge

Beethoven revered Bach. He had learned the Well-Tempered Clavier as a child and it shaped his harmonic imagination for life. When he returned to fugal technique in his late works, he did so not in imitation of Bach but in a spirit of violent confrontation — as if he was taking the intellectual architecture of fugue and subjecting it to the full pressure of Romantic emotional intensity, to see how much it could bear.

The Grosse Fuge Op. 133 was originally the finale of the String Quartet Op. 130. It was considered so extreme, so intractable, so apparently unplayable, that his publisher persuaded Beethoven to replace it with a lighter movement and publish the fugue separately. The conductor Igor Stravinsky, a century later, called it “an absolutely contemporary piece of music that will be contemporary forever”. It runs for about seventeen minutes — longer than most complete Baroque fugues — and covers extraordinary dynamic and emotional range, from passages of almost lyrical tenderness to eruptions of dissonant violence that still startle after repeated hearings. The Amadeus Quartet's recording presents it alongside Op. 130 as Beethoven originally intended CD 88. The final movements of the Hammerklavier Sonata CD 87 contain a similarly ferocious fugal finale for solo piano, occupying a comparable position in his piano output to the Grosse Fuge in his chamber music: the furthest point his musical thought ever reached.

Romantic Homage: Liszt and the B-A-C-H Fugue

The letters of Bach's name spell out a chromatic four-note motif — B flat, A, C, B natural — that has fascinated composers for three centuries. Bach used it himself in Contrapunctus XIV. Schumann quoted it. But it was Liszt who made it the basis of two substantial works that are among the finest things he ever wrote for the instrument he reinvented.

The Prelude and Fugue on the name B-A-C-H, written for organ and later transcribed for piano, is one of the most ambitious organ works of the Romantic era. The fugue builds with colossal patience from the four-note subject through episodes of increasing complexity and harmonic daring until a final climax that is simultaneously an act of homage and a declaration of artistic independence. Chantal de Zeeuw's organ recording CD 264 is paired with Liszt's organ transcription of Bach's own weeping Cantata BWV 12 — an illuminating context. The piano version of the B-A-C-H fugue, alongside Brahms's Variations and Fugue on a Theme by Handel, appears in Michael Ponti's recording CD 265. Brahms's Handel Variations conclude with a fugue that is a masterclass in Romantic counterpoint: rigorous, dense, and building to a conclusion of granite certainty.

The Twentieth Century: Shostakovich's Forty-Eight

In 1950, Shostakovich attended the Bach bicentennial celebrations in Leipzig as a Soviet delegate and heard Tatiana Nikolayeva perform the Well-Tempered Clavier. He was so moved that he returned to Moscow and in three months of concentrated work wrote his own set of twenty-four preludes and fugues — one in each major and minor key, following Bach's model exactly, but in a language that is unmistakably his own: sometimes ironic, sometimes lyrical, sometimes bleak, often deeply personal in ways that are hard to articulate but impossible to mistake.

The result is one of the most extraordinary acts of creative homage in musical history. Shostakovich did not attempt to write like Bach; he wrote like himself, within a structure borrowed from Bach, and the result illuminates both composers. The D minor Prelude and Fugue that opens the set has a plainness and gravity that directly echoes Contrapunctus I; the D flat major, one of the most beautiful, has a lyrical breadth that is entirely Romantic; the A minor has a savage, almost terrifying energy. Nikolayeva herself made the definitive recording, issued on Hyperion CD 434 — she had premiered the work, and her understanding of its emotional topography is without equal.

Why the Fugue Endures

Kerman, writing about Bach's keyboard fugues, resists the temptation to treat them as abstract intellectual exercises. His point — and it is the point of this article too — is that the fugue's intellectual rigour and its emotional directness are not in opposition but are the same thing. The discipline of counterpoint, the obligation to make independent voices work together, creates its own kind of drama: the drama of order achieved against difficulty, of coherence maintained against the centrifugal pull of four simultaneous melodic lines. When it works — when the fugue subject returns in the bass after a long absence, or when two subjects combine to make something neither could have achieved alone — the pleasure is simultaneously intellectual and physical, a satisfaction as immediate as a resolved chord and as lasting as a mathematical proof.

The Art of Fugue was not a popular success in Bach's lifetime. The first edition sold poorly; unsold copies were liquidated at less than the price of the paper. It was rediscovered by Forkel, Bach's first biographer, and by the early nineteenth century had begun its slow ascent to its present status as the most admired single achievement in the history of counterpoint. The incomplete last fugue, with its unanswerable question, is perhaps the most eloquent silence in all music: the supreme demonstration of a technique that had occupied a lifetime, broken off before its completion, leaving us to wonder what the resolution might have been.

A Listener's Guide

Great Quartets of the Last 50 Years

The Ensembles, the Recordings & What to Seek Out  ·  c.1970 – present

The Golden Age

The last half-century has been, by any measure, a golden age of string quartet playing. The number of professional ensembles of the highest quality has never been greater, the standard of technical accomplishment has never been higher, and the range of recorded repertoire — from Haydn's earliest efforts to music commissioned last year — has never been more comprehensively documented. If you want to explore 19th century chamber music in depth, the problem is not scarcity but embarrassment of riches: there are four or five recordings of almost every major quartet that might reasonably claim to be definitive, and choosing between them is one of the more pleasurable difficulties a collector faces.

Your collection already holds an impressive range of quartet performances — from the Quartetto Italiano's Beethoven and Schubert to the Alban Berg Quartet and the Lindsays, from the Borodin Quartet's Shostakovich to the Pavel Haas Quartet's Smetana. What follows is a guide to the major ensembles of the past fifty years, what they do best, and where the significant gaps in the collection lie — most of them well worth adding to the wishlist.

The Founding Elders

Three quartets dominated the scene from the 1950s into the 1980s and set the standards against which everything since has been measured.

The Quartetto Italiano (1945–1980) brought an Italian vocal warmth and classical purity to the Viennese repertoire that no subsequent ensemble has quite replicated. Their Philips recordings of the Beethoven late quartets remain touchstones of the form CD 78 CD 79, and their complete Mozart quintets CD 304 are similarly authoritative. The tone was always singing, the ensemble always unified, the interpretative approach always in service of the music rather than the performers.

The Amadeus Quartet (1947–1987) was the great British ensemble of the postwar era: four Central European émigrés who found in England both safety and a musical home. Their Beethoven had a grand architectural sweep and an emotional generosity that made it enormously popular; their recording of Op. 130 with the Grosse Fuge remains a significant account CD 88. They dissolved when their viola player Peter Schidlof died in 1987, all four having played together for four decades — a continuity of ensemble that is almost impossible to sustain in the modern music world.

The Guarneri Quartet (1964–2009) was America's pre-eminent ensemble for forty-five years. Their Beethoven cycle on RCA was long considered the standard American account, their Brahms was magnificent, and they brought a searching intelligence to everything they touched. Their recording of the Arriaga quartets in your collection shows the Guarneri at full stretch on less familiar repertoire CD 2.

The Alban Berg Quartet: Vienna Perfected

The Alban Berg Quartet (1971–2008) was, for most of its existence, simply the finest quartet in the world. Founded in Vienna with a rigorous commitment to technical perfection and a Viennese interpretative tradition stretching back to the composers themselves, they recorded virtually the entire central repertoire for EMI and Teldec and produced a body of recordings that has proved almost uniquely resistant to supersession.

Their Schubert is the place to start. The String Quintet with Heinrich Schiff is the recording against which all others are measured CD 401, and the two Schubert quartet discs in your collection confirm the level of their engagement with this composer CD 404. Their Beethoven complete cycle on EMI — not yet in your collection — is one of the great monuments of recorded chamber music and an ideal wishlist addition. Their late Beethoven in particular — Op. 131, Op. 132, the Grosse Fuge — achieves a combination of technical perfection and emotional depth that is deeply Viennese in spirit and has not been surpassed.

The Lindsays: British Passion

The Lindsay String Quartet, later known simply as The Lindsays (1965–2005), brought to chamber music a quality that the Viennese tradition sometimes sacrificed for polish: raw emotional commitment. Their ensemble playing was not always as immaculate as the Alban Berg Quartet's, but they were willing to take risks — to let the music breathe dangerously, to slow down where another group might press forward, to expose the psychological extremities of the great late works rather than smooth them over.

Their Beethoven is represented in your collection by the Razumovsky Quartets CD 77, and their Schubert by two separate recordings of the Death and the Maiden quartet CD 402 CD 407. Their complete Beethoven cycle on ASV — long unavailable but now findable — is one of the most individual accounts ever made: unpredictable, sometimes rough-hewn, always alive. Their Schubert complete series is equally personal. Both reward comparison with the Alban Berg.

The Fitzwilliam and the British Tradition

The Fitzwilliam String Quartet (1968–present) occupies a special place in British chamber music history through their association with Shostakovich himself, who entrusted them with premieres and heard in them a quality of commitment to his music that he valued highly. Their Schubert is more rarely discussed but of real distinction, and their recording of the G major Quartet D.887 CD 406 is one of the most penetrating accounts of that challenging and undervalued work. The Allegri String Quartet, a long-lived British ensemble, appears in your collection in both Elgar programme recordings CD 176 CD 177 and in a Schubert disc that also includes Haydn and Ravel CD 400.

The Belcea Quartet (1994–present) represents the outstanding British quartet of the present generation: four players of different national origins who met at the Royal College of Music and have built an international reputation through the rigour and emotional intensity of their playing. Their Schubert String Quintet with Valentin Erben CD 408 shows what they can do with the central repertoire. Their complete Beethoven cycle, recorded for the Alpha label over several years, is among the finest recent accounts — more searching and less polished than the Alban Berg, more controlled than the Lindsays, very much their own achievement. An ideal wishlist item for anyone exploring the late quartets.

The Takács Quartet: From Budapest to Boulder

The Takács Quartet (1975–present) was formed at the Franz Liszt Academy in Budapest and began its career as one of the great Hungarian quartets in the tradition of the Kodály and Bartók Quartets. Since relocating to the University of Colorado at Boulder in 1983, they have gradually become what many consider the finest quartet currently performing, combining a Central European intensity and technical exactitude with an American openness and willingness to engage with the full breadth of the repertoire.

Their Beethoven complete cycle on Decca is arguably the finest currently available: deeply considered, technically immaculate, emotionally committed without tipping into excess. It is one of the most important single gaps in your collection and belongs at the top of any wishlist. Their Bartók complete cycle on Decca is equally celebrated — the reference recording of arguably the 20th century's greatest quartet cycle, played with ferocious energy and complete understanding of Bartók's complex language. Their recordings of Schubert and Brahms confirm that they are one of those rare ensembles that seem equally at home wherever they turn.

The Emerson String Quartet: American Authority

The Emerson String Quartet (1976–2023) was America's most celebrated quartet for nearly five decades, winning nine Grammy Awards and building a discography of extraordinary breadth and quality on Deutsche Grammophon. Their most distinctive feature was alternating which violinist played first — Eugene Drucker and Philip Setzer swapping leadership between pieces — which gave them a flexibility of personality that single-leader quartets sometimes lack.

Their complete Beethoven cycle on Deutsche Grammophon is the most technically accomplished American account ever made: the playing is of breathtaking precision, the interpretations are always thoughtful, and the sound quality is exceptional. It is essential listening for any serious Beethoven collection. Their complete Brahms is equally fine — the three string quartets, the two piano quintets (with Emanuel Ax), the clarinet quintet (with David Shifrin) — a complete survey of the Brahms chamber repertoire that sets the standard for the current era. Their Schubert — the Death and the Maiden and the late quartets — and their Mendelssohn complete are further outstanding achievements. All are on DG and all are strong wishlist candidates.

Eastern Europe: The Great Traditions

The Borodin Quartet (1945–present, with changed personnel) holds a unique place in the Shostakovich discography through their direct personal connection with the composer. He played piano with them, discussed his quartets with them, and trusted their understanding of what he was attempting. Their EMI recordings of the complete fifteen quartets — spanning several decades and different lineup generations — have the authority of firsthand knowledge. Your collection has their recording of Nos. 1 and 13 CD 429; their complete set on EMI/Warner is an obvious wishlist addition for anyone who wants to go deeper.

The Éder Quartet brings a distinctly Hungarian flavour to the Shostakovich complete cycle in your collection CD 422 CD 423 CD 424 CD 425 CD 426 CD 427. The Mandelring Quartett's complete Shostakovich CD 431 offers a German perspective on the same cycle — more contained emotionally but immaculately played.

The Pavel Haas Quartet (2002–present) from Brno, Czech Republic, has established itself remarkably quickly as one of the most individual and exciting ensembles of the current generation. Their Smetana recording in your collection CD 438 shows their exceptional engagement with Czech nationalist repertoire. Their recordings of Dvořák — the American Quartet Op. 96 paired with Janáček on Supraphon — are among the finest currently available, with an idiomatic directness and rhythmic vitality that makes rival recordings sound cautious by comparison. Essential for the wishlist.

The New Generation

Several younger quartets have emerged in the last twenty-five years to challenge and in some cases surpass their predecessors.

The Jerusalem Quartet (1996–present) has built its reputation primarily in the Central European repertoire: their Brahms on Harmonia Mundi is warmly regarded, their Beethoven and Schubert on the same label show a combination of youthful energy and mature thoughtfulness. The Danish String Quartet (2002–present) has attracted widespread attention for bringing a Nordic sensibility to the standard repertoire while also championing Scandinavian composers; their recording of Nielsen, Sibelius and Abrahamsen alongside a Beethoven Op. 131 shows an ensemble of remarkable versatility and intelligence. The Cuarteto Casals (1997–present) from Barcelona has made a strong mark in Haydn and Brahms, bringing Iberian warmth and rhythmic vitality to repertoire that can sometimes sound neutral.

The Hagen Quartett (1981–present) from Salzburg occupies a special position — a sibling group of exceptional refinement whose Deutsche Grammophon recordings of Schubert, Mozart and Brahms are among the finest of the current era. Their Death and the Maiden is a notable alternative to the Alban Berg and Lindsay accounts in your collection, and their complete Brahms quartet recordings are outstanding.

A Long-Form Portrait

The Cello and Its Music

Four Centuries of the Most Human Instrument  ·  c.1600 – present

Why the Cello Speaks to Us

Of all the instruments in the Western classical tradition, the cello is the one that sounds most like the human voice. Not the violin, which can soar above the voice's range into a purity that is almost inhuman. Not the viola, which has the right register but a tendency towards a slightly nasal quality. The cello sits in exactly the range of the speaking voice — baritone to tenor — and its tone, particularly in the middle register, has a warmth, a resonance, a slight roughness that mirrors the qualities of a voice under emotional pressure. When a great cellist plays a slow melody, something happens in the listener that bypasses rational analysis: the sound connects directly to something felt rather than thought.

This is not merely a romantic impression. Acousticians have noted that the cello's fundamental frequencies and overtone patterns closely match those of the human larynx. Neurological research has shown that the brain processes cello sound in ways that overlap with speech processing. And centuries of listening have confirmed the intuition: there is something in the cello's voice that feels like confession. It does not declaim or display; it tells you something private. It is the instrument of inner life.

Your collection is extraordinarily rich in cello music — perhaps the single most concentrated area of your holdings. Seven recordings of Rostropovich alone; four of Jacqueline du Pré; Mischa Maisky, Yo-Yo Ma, Steven Isserlis, Heinrich Schiff, Roel Dieltiens, Zara Nelsova, Julian Lloyd Webber, Natalie Clein, Gautier Capuçon. The Bach Suites in four separate recordings. Five different accounts of the Dvořák Concerto. This article is an attempt to do justice to that richness — to trace the cello's four-hundred-year journey from anonymous support instrument to the most beloved solo voice in classical music, with your own collection as the guide.

The Instrument: How It Works and Why It Resonates

The cello is a bowed string instrument with four strings tuned C-G-D-A from bottom to top, an octave below the viola. It is held between the knees, supported on a spike (the endpin) resting on the floor — though the endpin is actually a relatively late innovation: Baroque and early Classical cellists held the instrument entirely with their legs, which gave them less freedom of movement but a more intimate physical connection with the instrument. The strings are set in vibration by a horsehair bow, and the resonances are amplified by the hollow wooden body, which acts as both resonator and loudspeaker.

What makes the cello's sound unique among string instruments is the combination of string length (about seventy-five centimetres of vibrating string, compared with thirty-two on a violin), string thickness, and the particular dimensions and arching of the body. These combine to produce a fundamental tone that is rich in lower harmonics — warm, complex, with a natural vibrato that seems almost inherent in the instrument rather than applied by the player. The cello also has exceptional dynamic range: from a thread of pianissimo sound in the upper register to a fortissimo in the low C string that you feel in your chest as much as hear with your ears.

The great cellos are all old instruments, invariably made by the great Cremonese luthiers of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries — Stradivari, Guarneri, Montagnana, Gofriller. Jacqueline du Pré played the 1712 Stradivarius now known as the Davidov Stradivari. Rostropovich owned and played multiple historic instruments. Yo-Yo Ma has long played a 1712 Montagnana. These instruments are worth millions; their tone is irreplaceable; and the relationship between a great cellist and a great instrument is one of the most intimate collaborations in all of musical performance.

The Baroque Cello: From Continuo to Soloist

For most of the seventeenth century, the cello was an anonymous instrument. It played the bass line in the continuo group that underpinned virtually all Baroque ensemble music — filling in the harmony, supporting the soloists, providing the rhythmic foundation on which everything else rested. It was necessary, even indispensable, but it was not the point. The soloists were the violinists, the singers, the trumpeters; the cello was the floor on which they stood.

The transformation began in Bologna and Venice in the last decades of the seventeenth century, when a handful of composers — among them Giacomo Battistini, Giuseppe Jacchini, and most significantly Antonio Vivaldi — began writing music that exploited the cello as a solo instrument in its own right. Vivaldi's twenty-seven surviving cello concertos are among the glories of the Baroque repertoire and a revelation for anyone who knows only the Four Seasons: they are inventive, technically demanding, melodically abundant, and full of passages that show Vivaldi listening acutely to what the cello could do that no other instrument could. Roel Dieltiens with the Ensemble Explorations gives us these works with all the rhetorical vivacity and technical command the period-instrument tradition has developed CD 468. Yo-Yo Ma takes an entirely different approach in his Vivaldi's Cello with Ton Koopman and the Amsterdam Baroque Orchestra — playing on a baroque cello and gut strings, exploring what happens when a modern virtuoso subjects himself to the constraints of period performance CD 490. The comparison between the two approaches is itself a small education in what historical performance practice means in practice.

The John Garth concertos — an English set of six, from the 1750s, only recently revived — show how the Italian cello concerto tradition spread northward through Europe, Richard Tunnicliffe bringing both technical fluency and stylistic understanding to music that has been unjustly neglected CD 185. And Jian Wang's Baroque Album with the Camerata Salzburg offers a fine sampler of Baroque cello repertoire that crosses national boundaries — Italian, German, French — in a single disc CD 115.

The Bach Cello Suites: Six Pillars of the Repertoire

If you had to choose a single body of work that defined the cello as a concert instrument and demonstrated what it was capable of at its absolute summit, you would choose the six suites for unaccompanied cello that Johann Sebastian Bach composed around 1717-23. They are simultaneously the foundation of the cello's technical vocabulary and its most enduring artistic achievement: six works each consisting of a prelude and five dances, ranging from the relatively accessible First Suite in G major to the extraordinary Sixth Suite in D major, written for a five-string instrument and reaching to a difficulty that still challenges the greatest players today.

The story of the Suites' discovery is almost as remarkable as the music itself. Bach wrote them in his thirties, probably for Christian Ferdinand Abel, the court musician at Cöthen. After Bach's death they passed into relative obscurity, copied and preserved but rarely performed. Then in 1889, a thirteen-year-old Spanish cellist called Pablo Casals found a copy in a second-hand music shop in Barcelona, recognised what he was looking at, and spent the next thirteen years studying the works before performing any of them publicly. His 1936-38 recordings, made in a Paris studio, were the first ever of the complete Suites and launched the works on their journey to the central position they now occupy in the repertoire.

What Bach achieved in these six suites is nothing less than the complete illusion of polyphonic music from a single melodic line. The cello has one string bowing at a time (mostly), yet Bach implies two, three, and even four voices simultaneously through strategic placement of double-stops, through the direction and momentum of melodic lines that the listener's ear completes, and through a bass line implied by the lowest notes of arpeggiated chords. The Prelude to the First Suite — almost certainly the most recognisable piece ever written for solo cello — is essentially a continuous broken-chord figuration, yet it creates a sense of harmonic progression so inevitable and so satisfying that it has been loved and played for three centuries without its mechanism once becoming tiresome.

Your collection holds four complete recordings of the Suites, each representing a quite different approach to the same musical problem.

Mstislav Rostropovich recorded the Suites twice, and both accounts are in your collection. The first recording, made in 1991 for EMI CD 23, is monumental in scale — Rostropovich treating the Suites as statements of the grandest ambition, each dance movement given its full architectural weight, the phrasing as grand and rhetorical as if the whole Berlin Philharmonic were present. The tempos are often slower than period-instrument accounts, the vibrato richer, the emotional temperature higher. It is Rostropovich imposing his enormous personality on the music, and the result is, depending on your taste, either overwhelming or over-scaled. His second recording CD 38, made a decade later, shows a slightly more reflective and in some ways more moving approach: the playing is no less technically commanding but there is something more inward about it, as if Rostropovich in his seventies was hearing things in the music that Rostropovich in his fifties had not yet reached.

Heinrich Schiff's recording on Philips CD 26 represents a contrasting approach: cleaner in texture, more classically proportioned, with less vibrato and a more transparent tone. Schiff was one of the great cellists of his generation — more naturally musical than many more famous names, with a sense of line and phrase that is consistently beautiful. His Suites are an excellent middle path between the Rostropovich grandeur and the full HIP austerity: they sound like Bach played by a musician who loves and understands the music without needing to make a statement about it.

Ralph Kirshbaum's account on Virgin Classics CD 34 brings a lyrical warmth that is perhaps the most immediately attractive of the four: less imposing than Rostropovich, less austere than a period-instrument version, with a natural singing quality in the slow movements that is genuinely beautiful. Kirshbaum taught at the Royal Northern College of Music for many years and his Suites have the generosity of a natural teacher sharing something he loves.

Each of the six suites has its own character. The First in G major is open and welcoming, its famous Prelude the most immediately accessible thing Bach wrote for the instrument. The Second in D minor is darker and more introspective, its Sarabande one of the most moving slow movements in all his output. The Third in C major is jubilant and extrovert, the Fourth in E flat major more complex and harmonically adventurous. The Fifth in C minor is perhaps the most mysterious — Bach specifies that the top string should be retuned a tone lower (scordatura), which gives the whole suite a different acoustic character, a slightly veiled quality — and its Prelude, a long free fantasia that suddenly freezes into a fugue, is one of the strangest and most beautiful things he wrote. The Sixth in D major is the summit of the technical demands: originally requiring a five-string instrument, it projects a grandeur and complexity that make it feel almost orchestral.

The Bach Cello Sonatas

Less universally known than the Suites, the three Sonatas for viola da gamba and harpsichord BWV 1027-1029 are works of extraordinary beauty that sit perfectly on the cello. Bach wrote them for a bowed keyboard instrument midway between viola and cello in range, but they have found their natural home in the cello repertoire, and the partnership of Mischa Maisky and Martha Argerich in your collection — presented in two separate recordings — is one of the most celebrated chamber recordings of the late twentieth century CD 29 CD 39. The combination of Maisky's Romantic tone and Argerich's volcanic energy with Bach's contrapuntal rigour creates something that critics have found variously magnificent and misguided — but it is never dull, and the sheer musical electricity between these two players is palpable even on disc.

Haydn and the Birth of the Cello Concerto

Joseph Haydn composed two cello concertos that together mark the cello's arrival as a major vehicle for the concerto form. The more famous is the Concerto No. 2 in D major, long thought to be the only Haydn cello concerto, which is a work of unclouded Classical elegance: a singing first movement, a deeply lyrical slow movement, and a finale of infectious good humour. But the Concerto No. 1 in C major has the more interesting history: it was believed lost until 1961, when a copy was found in the Prague National Museum. It is an earlier work — perhaps from the 1760s — and in some ways even more attractive, with a bold first movement that shows a Haydn not yet fully Classical in style, still experimental and searching.

Maria Kliegel with the Cologne Chamber Orchestra under Helmut Müller-Brühl gives a clean, stylish account of all three concertos (including a third, less certain attribution) CD 236. Steven Isserlis with the Orchestra of the Age of Enlightenment on RCA Victor brings both historical knowledge and genuine musical warmth to the two main concertos CD 238. Isserlis is a player of exceptional intelligence: he thinks deeply about the historical context of everything he plays, and his interpretations are always considered without ever feeling academic.

Beethoven: Five Sonatas, Three Periods, One Instrument

Beethoven's five sonatas for piano and cello are unique in the chamber music literature: they span his entire creative life, from the two early Op. 5 sonatas of 1796 (written when he was twenty-five and performing with the virtuoso cellist Jean-Louis Duport in Berlin) to the two late Op. 102 sonatas of 1815, which stand as directly as any of his late works on the threshold of the world the late quartets would inhabit. Between these extremes sits the magnificent Op. 69 Sonata in A major (1808) — perhaps the finest of the five, certainly the most immediately beloved, with a cello theme of such natural beauty that it sounds inevitable the first time you hear it.

The Op. 5 sonatas are unusual in that the piano has the dominant role — the cello is still something of a junior partner, as befitted the instrument's status in 1796 — but already the writing has a vigour and a harmonic boldness that marks them as unmistakably Beethoven. By Op. 69 the two instruments are fully equal partners, the famous opening melody passed between them in a dialogue of complete mutual respect. And in the Op. 102 sonatas — especially the C major, with its strange, spare opening and its fugal finale that looks directly towards the late quartets — the cello is asked to do things it had never been asked to do before: to sustain long silences, to enter unexpectedly in new keys, to participate in a musical discourse that is austere, demanding, and ultimately transcendent.

Jacqueline du Pré's recording of the Third and Fifth Sonatas with Stephen Bishop-Kovacevich CD 73 has a directness and emotional presence that is characteristic of everything she did. The complete set with Daniel Barenboim CD 99 — her then-husband — is a more complex achievement: the partnership is occasionally unequal (Barenboim's piano can overwhelm her in the more extrovert passages), but in the slow movements and the quieter exchanges there is an intimacy that no subsequent recording has quite replicated. András Schiff and Miklós Pérenyi offer a more classically balanced account in the complete Beethoven music for piano and cello CD 104 — playing on instruments tuned a semitone lower than modern pitch, which gives the whole set a slightly different colouring, the cello richer and warmer in the lower register, the piano less brilliant but more intimate.

Schubert and the Arpeggione Sonata

Among the strangest and most beautiful stories in the chamber music repertoire is that of the Arpeggione Sonata D.821. In November 1823, a Viennese guitar-maker named Johann Georg Stauffer invented an instrument he called the arpeggione — a six-string, fretted, guitar-shaped instrument played with a bow, tuned like a guitar but bowed like a cello. Schubert, always interested in new possibilities, wrote a sonata for it in that same year, probably for the Viennese virtuoso Vincenz Schuster. The arpeggione promptly vanished from history: Schuster gave a few concerts with it, nobody else showed much interest, and within a decade the instrument was completely forgotten. The sonata survived only because it was published posthumously in 1871, by which time everyone had forgotten the arpeggione existed, and it was simply played on cello — or, as Schubert's gentle, floating melodic writing lends itself equally well to the viola, sometimes on that instrument instead.

In the hands of a great cellist, the Arpeggione Sonata is one of the most beguilingly lovely things Schubert ever wrote: a single long-spun melody that seems to have no beginning and no end, harmonies that drift sideways into unexpected lights, a slow movement of almost hypnotic beauty. The fact that it was written for an instrument nobody now plays gives it a slightly ghostly quality — as if it exists slightly outside the normal categories, belonging to no fixed world.

Rostropovich and Benjamin Britten recorded it together twice, and both recordings are in your collection CD 129 CD 409. These are among the most celebrated chamber collaborations in recorded history: Britten, a cellist's pianist of genius, provides exactly the right kind of support — present but not dominant, always listening, always responsive — while Rostropovich plays with a lyrical warmth that is here, unusually, kept within scale. The two recordings are different in small but perceptible ways: the later one, made for Decca in 1968, is marginally more restrained; the earlier EMI version has a slightly more impulsive quality. Both are essential. The same pairing is found on a second disc that adds Schumann's Fünf Stücke im Volkston, five short pieces of such natural charm that Schumann wrote them in two days and described them as “something very easy and cheerful” CD 409.

Mischa Maisky's Songs without Words disc with Daria Hovora explores the cello's capacity for pure song — transcriptions of Schubert Lieder arranged for cello and piano CD 394. It is a disc that divides opinion: some find the transcriptions an intrusive middleman between Schubert's vocal writing and the listener, while others — Maisky's considerable admirers — find that the cello reveals something in the melodies that the voice, paradoxically, sometimes obscures. It is at minimum a fascinating experiment, and at best a deeply moving one.

Brahms: Depth and Density

Brahms wrote two cello sonatas that are among the most demanding and rewarding works in the chamber repertoire. The First in E minor Op. 38 (1865) is the more classically proportioned: three movements, relatively compact, with a finale based on a Bach fugue subject that declares Brahms's loyalties with characteristic directness. The Second in F major Op. 99 (1886) is a different matter entirely: four movements, much larger in scale, with a piano writing of orchestral density that stretches both instruments to their limits. The first movement's opening theme — that characteristic Brahmsian gesture of thirds moving stepwise upward — announces something more passionate and less contained than the First Sonata, and the rest of the work lives up to the announcement. The slow movement is one of the most beautiful things he ever wrote for any medium.

Yo-Yo Ma and Emanuel Ax recorded these sonatas for Sony in what has become the benchmark account of the modern era CD 124. Ma brings his characteristic combination of technical perfection and emotional openness — he never sounds as if he is trying very hard, which is either a sign of supreme mastery or of a certain surface gloss, depending on your view. Ax is the ideal partner: warm, supportive, rhythmically secure, never allowing his own formidable technique to unbalance the partnership. The recording has been in print continuously since 1985 and remains the recording most listeners encounter first.

The Double Concerto for violin and cello Op. 102 — Brahms's last orchestral work, written as a gesture of reconciliation towards his old friend the violinist Joseph Joachim, with whom he had quarrelled — is one of the most fascinating failures in the repertoire: too self-consciously retrospective to be fully satisfying, it occasionally reaches the grandeur of the symphonies but more often settles into a courtly academicism. Isaac Stern and Yo-Yo Ma with Claudio Abbado and the Chicago Symphony give it the best possible advocacy CD 126, and the partnership between the two soloists has a warmth and mutual respect that make the best moments genuinely moving.

Schumann and Grieg

Robert Schumann's Cello Concerto in A minor Op. 129 (1850) is the most underrated major cello concerto in the repertoire. Written in the same miraculous burst of inspiration that produced the three string quartets and the Piano Quintet, it was not performed until after Schumann's death and has had to fight for recognition against the more immediately spectacular Dvořák and Elgar ever since. It is a work of unusual formal design — three movements played without a break, the cello sometimes accompanied by the orchestra, sometimes in solo passages of complete intimacy — and its emotional world is characteristic Schumann: inward, melancholic, with sudden illuminations of lyrical beauty.

Steven Isserlis with the Deutsche Kammerphilharmonie Bremen under Paavo Järvi gives a searching and sympathetic account, surrounded by related Schumann chamber works and songs in a disc that takes the concerto seriously as the late masterwork it is CD 416.

Edvard Grieg's Cello Sonata in A minor Op. 36 is one of the finest Scandinavian chamber works of the Romantic era: a single-movement work of concentrated lyricism with a Norwegian folk quality in the outer sections and a middle movement of haunting simplicity. Alban Gerhardt and Piers Lane give a characterful account in a disc that pairs it with other Grieg chamber pieces CD 182.

The Supreme Concerto: Dvořák

If you asked a hundred professional cellists to name the greatest cello concerto in the repertoire, at least ninety would answer Dvořák Op. 104. It is not merely the most popular — though it is that — but in the considered judgment of players and listeners who have spent their lives with the form, the most perfectly constructed, the most emotionally complete, the most richly satisfying. Brahms, who spent his life resisting sentimentality and was not given to easy enthusiasms, heard the completed work and said: “Why on earth didn't I know that one could write a cello concerto like this? If I had only known, I would have written one long ago!”

It was written in 1895 during Dvořák's last year in America, when he had just heard that his sister-in-law Josefina Kounicová — a woman he had loved as a young man and who had become a lifelong close friend — was gravely ill. He wove the melody of her favourite song, “Leave Me Alone”, into the slow movement, and when he returned to Bohemia and learned she had died, he added a coda to the finale that quotes the same melody, transforming what had begun as a joyful conclusion into something more valedictory and complex. The whole concerto has this quality: ostensibly Romantic and outward-looking, with grand orchestral tuttis and virtuosic solo writing, but fundamentally personal, shaped by love and loss in ways that seep through the brilliant surface.

Your collection holds five recordings, which is exactly the right number because no single version captures everything.

Rostropovich with the Berliner Philharmoniker under Karajan exists in two formats in your collection CD 165 CD 166. This is one of the great cello-and-orchestra partnerships in recording history: Karajan and Rostropovich shared an intense musical sympathy, and the result is playing of enormous presence and power. Rostropovich's tone in the slow movement is the closest anything on record comes to a human voice singing in that register; the finale builds with an inevitability that feels like the culmination of a life's experience. It is the grandest possible reading, and for many listeners the definitive one.

The du Pré live BBC recordings CD 167 offer something different: the rawness and immediacy of live performance, du Pré's playing captured at the height of its powers in a single take, imperfect and therefore more alive than any studio account. Her Dvořák was famous for being slightly reckless — she played it at a tempo slightly faster than the conductor usually wanted, with a physical freedom that sometimes threatened the ensemble but always generated electricity. The live recordings preserve that quality.

Gautier Capuçon with the Frankfurt Radio Symphony Orchestra brings a more modern approach: leaner in tone, cleaner in ensemble, more scrupulous about the score's details CD 160. Capuçon is one of the finest young French cellists of his generation, and his Dvořák is intelligent and well-considered, if lacking the volcanic personal investment of Rostropovich or du Pré. Maria Kliegel's account with the RPO CD 163 pairs it with the Elgar and provides a reliable if less individually characterised alternative. And Jan Vogler with the New York Philharmonic Strings in The Secrets of Dvořák's Cello Concerto CD 171 takes a musicological approach — exploring the work's origins and Dvořák's revisions — that offers a genuinely different way of hearing a familiar work.

The Elgar: An English Farewell

Edward Elgar composed his Cello Concerto in E minor Op. 85 in 1919, three years after the First World War had ended, at a time when the world he had celebrated in the Enigma Variations and the two symphonies seemed definitively gone. It is a work of renunciation: not a grand concerto in the Romantic tradition, not a showpiece, not a display of virtuosity, but a long, quiet meditation on loss. The opening is extraordinary — a single rhetorical gesture in the cello, alone, unaccompanied, followed by a long silence — and the rest of the work never quite abandons that quality of speaking privately to itself.

The premiere in October 1919 was a disaster. The conductor Albert Coates spent most of the rehearsal time on his own programme, leaving Elgar barely enough time to work through the concerto. The performance was ragged, the critical reception dismissive, and Elgar — already wounded by the war's devastation of the world he valued — largely withdrew from composition. The Cello Concerto sank into obscurity for three and a half decades, occasionally performed but rarely loved.

Then in 1965, a twenty-year-old cellist called Jacqueline du Pré recorded it for EMI with John Barbirolli conducting the London Symphony Orchestra. It became one of the most celebrated recordings in the history of classical music — not because it was technically flawless (it is not) or because it was historically informed (it is emphatically not), but because du Pré had found in this music something that spoke to her with absolute directness, and her playing communicates that connection with an immediacy that bypasses analytical response. The performance is too slow in some places, too fast in others, the string tone is sometimes rough, the ensemble occasionally loose. None of this matters in the slightest. What matters is that when the solo cello sings the second movement's main theme, or when the finale's elegy unfolds in the closing pages, you are hearing a twenty-year-old woman playing music she understood with her whole being.

The du Pré live recordings in your collection include Elgar performances of this quality CD 167, and the compilation A Lasting Inspiration preserves further evidence of her extraordinary gifts CD 521. Natalie Clein brings a different set of qualities to the concerto — more contained, more careful about Elgar's markings, with a tone that is warmer and less raw than du Pré's — in her recording with the Royal Liverpool Philharmonic CD 173. Steven Isserlis, recording for Hyperion with the London Symphony and Richard Hickox, brings his characteristic scholarly depth: his liner notes alone would justify the purchase, and his playing has an emotional intensity that he achieves without du Pré's recklessness CD 522. Julian Lloyd Webber, who did as much as anyone in the 1980s to build the cello's popular audience in Britain, presents it in a programme of favourite concertos that includes also the Dvořák and Saint-Saëns CD 523.

The Twentieth Century: Strauss, Bloch and Beyond

Richard Strauss's Don Quixote Op. 35 is one of the great showpieces for the solo cello in the orchestral repertoire. It is a set of variations on a theme of “knightly character” depicting the adventures of Cervantes's Knight of the Sad Countenance — the tilting at windmills, the encounter with the sheep he mistakes for an army, the solo journey on a magic horse (represented by a rapid string tremolo), the final disillusionment and death. The solo cello throughout represents Don Quixote himself, and Strauss wrote some of the most demanding and rewarding solo writing in the orchestral literature for the part. Rostropovich with Karajan and the Berliner Philharmoniker is again the benchmark recording CD 445: Rostropovich plays the hero with a grandeur and a pathos that make the final pages — where Don Quixote returns to his senses and dies quietly — almost unbearably moving. Karajan's orchestral support is magnificent throughout.

Steven Isserlis also recorded the Elgar Concerto alongside Bloch's Schelomo and the Kabalevsky Concerto CD 522. Bloch's Schelomo (Solomon), composed in 1916, is a rhapsody for cello and orchestra of extraordinary emotional intensity, loosely based on the Book of Ecclesiastes; it is one of the most powerful single movements ever written for the instrument and Isserlis's account is outstanding. Mischa Maisky's Adagio compilation disc CD 503 offers a survey of the cello's Romantic showpiece tradition: Saint-Saëns, Fauré, Respighi, Dvořák, Glazunov and Tchaikovsky in orchestral arrangements with the Orchestre de Paris. And the extraordinary Cello Century of British Women Composers with Catherine Wilmers and Simon Marlow CD 528 illuminates a less familiar corner of the repertoire: music for cello by British women composers from across the twentieth century, much of it unfamiliar but all of it worth knowing. Julian Lloyd Webber's Unexpected Songs CD 524 takes a still more eclectic approach, mixing arrangements and original pieces in a programme designed to show the cello's versatility across different musical worlds.

Portrait Gallery: The Great Cellists in Your Collection

The recordings in your collection are not just evidence of great music but portraits of exceptional human beings. Let us take each of the major cellists in turn.

Pablo Casals (1876–1973) does not appear in your collection, but he is the presiding spirit of it. Born in Catalonia, trained in Barcelona and Brussels, he was the first cellist to make the Bach Suites central to the repertoire, the first to demonstrate that the cello could sustain a solo concert without vocal or orchestral support, the first to record the great concertos in accounts that defined the standard for subsequent generations. His technique — the pivoting left hand, the vibrato produced from the arm rather than the finger — was revolutionary. His recordings, mostly made in the 1930s and 50s, remain compelling even through the technical limitations. He also refused to perform in Fascist Spain or in countries that recognised Franco's government, living in exile in Prades for years: a man whose musical and moral lives were equally serious.

Jacqueline du Pré (1945–1987) is present in your collection more powerfully than any other cellist CD 73 CD 99 CD 167 CD 521, and she remains one of the most mythologised figures in classical music history. She began performing publicly at the age of seven; by twenty she was acclaimed as the greatest young cellist in the world; at twenty-eight she was diagnosed with multiple sclerosis and within two years had lost the ability to play. She died at forty-two, having given the world a little over a decade of active performing. The recordings she left behind have a quality that is genuinely difficult to explain in purely musical terms: an immediacy, a nakedness of emotion, a sense that she was playing not for an audience but for herself, putting on record something essential about what it felt like to be alive and to love music with that particular ferocity. Her Elgar recording is the most important single cello recording of the twentieth century. Her Beethoven, her Dvořák, and her Brahms are nearly as significant.

Mstislav Rostropovich (1927–2007) was simply the greatest cellist of the twentieth century: technically the most accomplished, musically the most authoritative, and personally the most imposing. He commissioned more major works for the cello than any other player in history — Prokofiev, Shostakovich, Britten, Lutoslawski, Dutilleux, Schnittke all wrote for him specifically — and he played them all with a force of personality that made them his own. His seven appearances in your collection CD 23 CD 38 CD 129 CD 165 CD 166 CD 409 CD 445 show him in Bach, Schubert, Dvořák and Strauss — a range that captures his breadth without exhausting it. He was also a conductor of considerable ability and a dissident of genuine moral courage: he was expelled from the Soviet Union in 1974 after years of supporting his friends Solzhenitsyn and Sakharov, and he returned to Moscow only after the fall of the Soviet Union, conducting a cello recital at the fallen Berlin Wall on the night of its demolition.

Mischa Maisky (born 1948) was one of Rostropovich's students and carries something of his teacher's Romantic intensity, though with a more lyrical and less monumental approach. His partnership with Martha Argerich in the Bach Sonatas CD 29 CD 39, his Schubert Lieder transcriptions CD 394, and his Adagio compilation CD 503 show a cellist of very considerable gifts whose slightly plummy tone and tendency towards expressive excess do not prevent him from reaching genuine depth. He also has one of the most extraordinary personal histories in classical music: born in Riga, he was imprisoned in a Soviet labour camp in 1970 on charges whose exact nature remains unclear, was released through the intervention of Rostropovich, and rebuilt his career from scratch in the West. His playing always carries a sense of hard-won emotional knowledge.

Yo-Yo Ma (born 1955) is the most publicly visible cellist of his generation — indeed, one of the most publicly visible classical musicians of any kind. Born in Paris to Chinese parents, raised in New York, trained at Juilliard and Harvard, he has spent his career systematically expanding the boundaries of what a classical cellist does: the Bach Suites, the Brahms Sonatas and Double Concerto CD 124 CD 126, Baroque repertoire on period instruments CD 490, crossover projects with Appalachian folk music, tango, Brazilian music and bluegrass. Some musicians find his eclecticism evidence of insufficient depth; others find it evidence of a genuinely open and curious intelligence. His playing is technically flawless and consistently beautiful, if occasionally lacking the raw edge that characterises the very greatest cellists.

Steven Isserlis (born 1958) is Britain's most consistently interesting cellist — a scholar-musician of the first order who thinks deeply about the historical context of everything he plays, writes about music with unusual intelligence, and performs with an emotional commitment that belies his scholarly reputation. His three appearances in your collection CD 238 CD 416 CD 522 show him in Haydn, Schumann and Elgar respectively — a range that reflects his particular strengths. He plays gut strings on a nineteenth-century bow, which gives his tone a more complex and slightly rougher character than modern-string players; whether this is authentic or eccentric (or both) remains a productive point of debate among his listeners.

Heinrich Schiff (1951–2016) was one of the great Austrian musicians of his generation: a cellist of exceptional natural musicality whose Bach Suites CD 26 and Schubert Quintet CD 401 are among the finest recordings he made. He later became a conductor of considerable ability, particularly in Schubert and Brahms, but his cello playing in its prime had a singing quality that was immediately recognisable: warmth without sentimentality, precision without coldness.

Zara Nelsova (1918–2002) was a Canadian cellist of Russian origin whose Decca recordings of the 1950s CD 526 show a player of formidable gifts who was never quite given the recognition she deserved — partly, it has been argued, because she was a woman in a field then dominated entirely by men, and partly because her concert career was centred in North America rather than Europe. Her recordings of the Bloch and Barber cello pieces are remarkable, and her tone has an individuality and depth that mark her as a major artist.

Roel Dieltiens is the Baroque specialist whose recordings of Vivaldi CD 468, Boccherini CD 111 and Dvořák CD 170 represent the best of the historically informed performance tradition applied to cello repertoire. He plays with a period bow, gut strings, and a historical instrument, which gives his performances a transparency and a rhetorical directness that modern-instrument accounts cannot replicate.

A Musical History

The Historically Informed Performance Revolution

How Scholars & Musicians Transformed the Way We Hear Old Music  ·  c.1960 – present

A Revolution Nobody Voted For

Sometime in the 1970s and 1980s, without a manifesto, without a founding moment, without any democratic vote or institutional decision, the way classical music was performed changed fundamentally and apparently permanently. It happened in recording studios and in small concert halls, driven by a handful of obsessive scholars, lutenists, gambists and keyboard players who were convinced that the music of the Baroque and early Classical eras was being performed in ways its composers would not have recognised — and who set about demonstrating, through research, through instrument building, and through performance, what those composers had actually had in mind.

This movement goes by various names. Historically Informed Performance (HIP) is the most neutral academic term. “Authentic performance” was popular for a while and is now largely abandoned, because it implied a certainty about historical accuracy that nobody seriously claims. “Period performance” or “original instruments” describes a specific subset of the movement. “Early music” is the broadest and least precise label, covering everything from medieval plainchant to late Beethoven depending on who is using it. Whatever you call it, the revolution it produced has transformed every corner of the classical world, not just the Baroque: modern orchestras play Bach differently than they did in 1970, the way singers approach Handel has fundamentally changed, and even the performance of Beethoven and Brahms has been reshaped by questions the HIP movement raised.

Your collection maps this revolution with exceptional completeness. The Academy of Ancient Music, John Eliot Gardiner, Trevor Pinnock and the English Concert, Christopher Hogwood, Ton Koopman, Philippe Herreweghe, The Sixteen, Monica Huggett, the Orchestra of the Age of Enlightenment — all are here, in recordings that span four decades of the movement's development. What follows is an attempt to understand what these musicians were doing and why it mattered, with your own collection as the guide throughout.

What Historically Informed Performance Actually Means

The central claim of HIP is simple to state and deeply complicated in practice: that music written before a certain date — let us say, approximately 1800 — was composed for instruments, voices, rooms and performers very different from those of the modern world, and that performing it on modern instruments in modern concert halls with modern technique produces something quite different from what the composer imagined. Therefore, we should use instruments and techniques as close as possible to what the composer would have known, in order to hear what they intended.

The specific differences are numerous and significant. The strings of a Baroque violin were made of gut, not modern steel, which gives them a less brilliant but more complex and slightly rougher tone. The bow was different — shorter, more curved, differently weighted — which makes different articulation natural and certain Baroque bowing styles physically impossible on a modern bow. The woodwind instruments of Mozart's time had fewer keys and produced a different quality of tone, particularly in the lower register. The natural (valveless) trumpet of the Baroque era could play only certain notes of the scale, which is why Baroque trumpet parts are almost always confined to the upper register. The harpsichord, which plucks rather than strikes its strings, produces a fundamentally different sound from the piano: precise but without the ability to vary dynamics by varying touch. And the fortepiano — the instrument for which Mozart and early Beethoven wrote — was a much lighter, more responsive, more transparent instrument than the modern concert grand, with a different balance between treble and bass and a natural decay that makes long-held notes impossible.

Beyond instruments, there are questions of ensemble size, tuning, articulation and ornamentation. Bach's choral works were performed at the Thomaskirche in Leipzig with forces of perhaps twenty-four singers and a dozen or so instrumentalists. The nineteenth century inherited idea of Bach choruses requiring massive choral forces — the 1829 performance of the St Matthew Passion under Mendelssohn used a chorus of nearly three hundred — is simply a historical misunderstanding, however magnificent the results. Similarly, the tuning of Baroque music was typically a semitone lower than modern concert pitch (A=415 rather than A=440), which changes the actual key the music sounds in and gives the instruments a slightly different character. And the rhythmic freedom of Baroque music — the notes inégales, the inequality of notated equal notes that was standard practice in French music, the improvised ornamentation that performers were expected to add — cannot be reproduced without historical knowledge.

What HIP is emphatically not, despite the caricature that its critics have sometimes promoted, is pedantic literalism or the subordination of music to scholarship. The greatest HIP musicians — Gardiner, Pinnock, Herreweghe — are first and foremost musicians who happen to have made themselves experts in historical performance practice. Their recordings crackle with life and rhythmic energy; they are not museum exhibits. As Gardiner has said repeatedly, the historical research is not an end in itself but a means of getting closer to what the music actually is — and the music, when you get close enough to it, turns out to be astonishing.

What Was There Before

To understand what the HIP revolution changed, it is necessary to understand what it replaced. By the early twentieth century, the performance of Baroque music had largely been absorbed into the interpretative conventions of the Romantic era. Bach's orchestral suites and Brandenburg Concertos were performed by full symphony orchestras with string sections of fifty or sixty players. His organ works were transcribed for large Romantic organs and played with an opulence of tone and a slowness of tempo that would have appalled him. His choral works were performed with cathedral choirs of sixty voices and orchestras to match. The St Matthew Passion was treated as a kind of Romantic oratorio, its individual movements given the same sustained, legato, heavily phrased treatment that was applied to Brahms and Wagner.

The most famous single example is Leopold Stokowski's orchestral transcription of the Bach Toccata and Fugue in D minor — a work originally written for a single organ, here expanded for full Romantic orchestra with the fugue given out by the massed strings, the climaxes swelled with brass and percussion into something that would not have sounded out of place in a Wagner opera. The transcription was featured in Disney's Fantasia (1940) and introduced millions of people to Bach, which is unambiguously good; but it is as far from what Bach wrote as a watercolour reproduction is from an oil painting.

Even the more scholarly conductors of the mid-twentieth century — men like Furtwängler, Bruno Walter and Otto Klemperer, who were serious musicians and serious intellectuals — applied the interpretative conventions of their era to Baroque music without questioning them. The result was beautiful, often magnificent, but historically remote. The recordings in your collection provide occasional evidence of this earlier tradition against which the HIP revolution can be measured.

The Founders: Harnoncourt, Leonhardt and the Vienna Revolution

The most important founding figure of the HIP movement is Nikolaus Harnoncourt (1929–2016), an Austrian cellist and conductor who founded the Concentus Musicus Wien in 1953 with the specific aim of performing Baroque music on period instruments according to historical principles. He began with music of the seventeenth century and worked forward, taking twenty years before he felt ready to tackle Bach.

What distinguished Harnoncourt from his contemporaries was not just the instruments he used but the intellectual seriousness with which he approached the questions of historical performance. He wrote extensively about the rhetoric of Baroque music — the idea that Baroque composers used musical gestures as a kind of language, a code of affects and meanings that audiences of the time would have understood intuitively — and argued that modern performances, by ignoring this rhetorical dimension, were not just historically inaccurate but musically incomplete. His book Baroque Music Today: Music as Speech remains one of the clearest expositions of why HIP matters.

In 1971, Harnoncourt and the Dutch harpsichordist and conductor Gustav Leonhardt embarked on one of the most ambitious recording projects in classical music history: a complete recording of all Bach's church cantatas, alternating between their two ensembles. The project took twenty-seven years to complete and produced forty-five volumes. Your collection holds two representative volumes from the Harnoncourt side: the cantata disc for the first Sunday after Epiphany with BWV 72, 73, 111 and 156 — but wait, those are Gardiner's. The Harnoncourt Bach Cantatas with Concentus Musicus Wien are specifically represented by CDs 50 and 65. The St Matthew Passion CD 50, conducted by Harnoncourt for Teldec with his Concentus Musicus Wien, is one of the most controversial and ultimately most influential recordings ever made: faster than the received tradition, with instruments tuned low, rhythms sharply pointed, and an energy that shocked listeners accustomed to the grand, slow, Romantic approach. The Bach cantatas CD 65 show Harnoncourt at the level of individual works: the famous Wachet auf Cantata BWV 140 and the equally celebrated BWV 147 (containing the chorale known in English as Jesu, Joy of Man's Desiring) given in performances that have the quality of freshly discovered music rather than overfamiliar repertoire.

Christopher Hogwood and the Academy of Ancient Music

Christopher Hogwood (1941–2014) founded the Academy of Ancient Music in Cambridge in 1973, and over the next two decades built it into the most famous period-instrument orchestra in the world. His ambitions were scholarly as well as musical: he was a music historian of the first order, and his recordings were always accompanied by extensive liner notes presenting the historical evidence for the performance choices made. His approach was, if anything, more rigorously scholarly than Harnoncourt's — less willing to impose a personal interpretative vision, more content to let the music speak through historically informed means.

The most famous product of this approach was his complete recording of the Bach orchestral works, of which your collection holds both the Brandenburg Concertos CD 44 and his recording of the Bach Magnificat with the Choir of King's College, Cambridge CD 48. The Brandenburg recording was genuinely revelatory when it appeared: the period instruments gave the music a transparency and a rhythmic precision that was entirely new to most listeners, and the smaller ensemble allowed inner voices to emerge that were simply inaudible in full-orchestra performances. Critics who had grown up with Karajan's Brandenburgs found it either liberating or cold, sometimes both at once.

His Handel recordings are equally significant in your collection. The Water Music and Music for the Royal Fireworks with the Academy of Ancient Music CD 215 were for many listeners the definitive accounts for decades: played on period instruments, at the right pitch, with natural trumpets and original oboes, they have a brilliance and a period authenticity that modern-instrument accounts can only approximate. Similarly, the Academy's recording of the Messiah with the New College Oxford choir and Edward Higginbottom CD 198 represents the HIP approach to Handel's most famous work at its most transparent and historically considered. The Albinoni concertos with the AAM under Hogwood and Andrew Manze CD 5 demonstrate the same principles applied to Italian Baroque concertos: leaner, faster, more rhythmically incisive than any modern-instrument account.

Trevor Pinnock and the English Concert

Trevor Pinnock (born 1946) was one of the finest harpsichordists of his generation before he became a conductor, and his performances have always carried the fingerprints of a keyboard player: rhythmically precise, texturally clear, with a feeling for the harmonic architecture of each phrase that comes from playing all the parts simultaneously on a single keyboard. He founded the English Concert in 1972, and over four decades built a discography of extraordinary range and quality, almost exclusively on the Archiv Produktion label.

The Corelli Concerti Grossi Op. 6 with the English Concert CD 153 are among the finest things Pinnock ever recorded: works that epitomise the Italian Baroque concerto at its most refined, played with a warmth and a rhythmic flexibility that puts to rest any idea that HIP performances are necessarily mechanical. The Handel Organ Concertos with Simon Preston as soloist — both the Op. 4 CD 211 and the Op. 7 CD 212 — are equally distinguished: Pinnock provides exactly the right kind of orchestral support for Preston's improvisatory, theatrical approach to the solo writing. His Haydn — the Sturm und Drang symphonies CD 233 and the Nelson Mass CD 247 — shows how naturally the HIP approach extended from Baroque into Classical: the orchestral textures lighter and more transparent than modern-instrument accounts, the tempos often faster, the emotional contrast more extreme. The Handel Italian cantatas with the English Concert CD 227 and the Ode for St Cecilia's Day CD 229 complete the picture of one of the great HIP ensembles at the height of its powers.

John Eliot Gardiner: Bach, Total and Complete

If there is a single figure who has done more than anyone else to demonstrate what HIP can achieve at its greatest, it is Sir John Eliot Gardiner (born 1943). He founded the Monteverdi Choir as a Cambridge undergraduate in 1964, when he was twenty-one; he has conducted it ever since. With it and his associated orchestra, the English Baroque Soloists, he has produced over five decades a discography of extraordinary depth and consistency, ranging from Monteverdi in the seventeenth century to Berlioz and Brahms in the nineteenth.

Gardiner's relationship with Bach is the central fact of his musical life. He has described Bach as the composer he most consistently returns to and the one whose music he least fully understands — which is the highest kind of tribute. His recordings of the Bach Passions are landmarks of the recording era: the St Matthew Passion on Archiv with the English Baroque Soloists and Monteverdi Choir achieves a dramatic intensity and a textual clarity that remains unsurpassed. Philippe Herreweghe's St Matthew Passion recorded for Harmonia Mundi CD 55 offers an alternative vision of the same work: more contemplative, more inward, cooler in temperature but no less profound.

The Bach cantatas in your collection represent two sides of the Gardiner achievement. The four volumes from his Bach Cantata Pilgrimage CD 66 CD 67 CD 68 CD 69 CD 70 CD 71 represent one of the most extraordinary events in recent musical history. In the year 2000, Gardiner led his ensemble on a pilgrimage: they performed all of Bach's surviving church cantatas — nearly two hundred of them — in the churches they were written for, on the liturgical Sundays and feast days they were composed for, travelling across Europe through the entire church year. The recordings, issued on Gardiner's own Soli Deo Gloria label, captured the project as it happened, imperfections and all. The result is a body of recordings unlike anything else in the discography: not just technically accomplished but spiritually engaged in a way that fifty years of studio recording had never quite achieved. The Christmas Oratorio CD 54, also with the English Baroque Soloists and Monteverdi Choir for Archiv, shows the same qualities in the context of a single large work: energy, rhythmic vitality, vocal clarity and a sense of the dramatic arc of the whole that is rarely matched. And the Archiv Messiah with Arleen Augér and Anne Sofie von Otter CD 197 remains one of the landmark recordings of the entire HIP movement — the work restored to something like its intended scale and performing style after more than a century of massive choral and orchestral inflation.

What makes Gardiner's Handel so outstanding is the combination of historical fidelity and theatrical intelligence. He understands that Handel was above all a man of the theatre, that his oratorios and dramatic works have a dramatic propulsion that academic care can easily stifle, and that the job of a HIP conductor in this repertoire is not to reconstruct a museum exhibit but to release the dramatic energy that the music contains.

Gardiner's extension of HIP principles into the early Romantic era is equally significant. His recordings with the Orchestre Révolutionnaire et Romantique — an orchestra using the instruments of the early nineteenth century, gut strings, natural brass, period woodwind — produced the Beethoven Symphonies 7 and 8 CD 94 that shocked and invigorated the Beethoven discography when they appeared. Faster than any modern-instrument account, more rhythmically incisive, with a transparency of texture that revealed inner voices normally buried under modern orchestral weight — these are performances that make familiar works sound genuinely new. His Schubert symphonies with the same orchestra CD 389 applied the same principles to a composer not usually considered a HIP target, and the results were equally revelatory: the Unfinished in particular has an urgency and a forward momentum that challenges every modern-instrument account. And his Schumann Das Paradies und die Peri with the Monteverdi Choir and ORR CD 414 showed that the approach could work even into the full Romantic period, the period instruments giving Schumann's orchestration a clarity and a transparency that modern brass and strings sometimes obliterate.

Ton Koopman and the Amsterdam Baroque

The Dutch harpsichordist and organist Ton Koopman (born 1944) has pursued HIP with a scholarly thoroughness that has made him one of its most prolific and comprehensive practitioners. His own cantata project — a complete recording of Bach's sacred and secular cantatas with his Amsterdam Baroque Orchestra and Choir, eventually running to sixty-seven CDs — rivals Gardiner's Pilgrimage in ambition if not in spiritual intensity. Your collection holds a representative volume CD 64 that shows Koopman's approach: warm, well-considered, with excellent soloists and a beautifully balanced ensemble. His organ recordings — the Bach toccatas and organ works CD 61 and the Bach Organ Masterpieces on Regis CD 518 — show a different dimension: an organist of the first rank playing historical instruments with complete technical command and interpretative intelligence. The Handel Organ Concertos with the Amsterdam Baroque Orchestra CD 210 demonstrate his ability to make these works sound like what they were: interval entertainments played by a virtuoso improviser in a theatre, not august concert pieces. And his collaboration with Yo-Yo Ma in Vivaldi's Cello CD 490 produced one of the most interesting experiments in the HIP discography: what happens when a major modern virtuoso submits to the constraints of period performance?

Philippe Herreweghe and the Flemish Tradition

Philippe Herreweghe (born 1947) is the most purely vocal of the great HIP conductors — a man who came to conducting from choral singing, who understands music primarily as a function of the voice, and whose best recordings have a textural refinement and a sense of vocal line that is immediately distinctive. He founded the Collegium Vocale Ghent in 1969 as a student in Ghent, and his approach to Bach was shaped from the beginning by Harnoncourt, who invited him to participate in the complete cantata recordings.

His St Matthew Passion on Harmonia Mundi CD 55 — there are actually two recordings of this work in his discography, made some years apart — is the most searching of all HIP accounts of this work: more inward than Gardiner, more contemplative than Harnoncourt, with a choral sound of extraordinary beauty and an almost chamber-music sensitivity to the text. Where Gardiner drives the drama, Herreweghe inhabits it.

His Rameau Les Indes Galantes orchestral suites with La Chapelle Royale CD 375 show his gift for French Baroque repertoire: a music of extraordinary rhythmic refinement and harmonic colour that requires precisely the kind of HIP approach he brings — light, dance-inflected, with an improvisatory quality in the ornamentation and a perfect command of the French style. Rameau was one of the later discoveries of the HIP movement, and these recordings were among the first to demonstrate what his orchestral writing could sound like in authentic hands.

The Vocal Revolution: Emma Kirkby, Countertenors, and The Sixteen

No aspect of the HIP revolution produced more immediate shock than its approach to voices. The nineteenth and early twentieth century ideal of vocal production — large, vibrato-rich, projected to fill a theatre without amplification — was applied without question to all pre-modern vocal repertoire. The result was Handel oratorios performed by operatic sopranos and tenors with operatic technique, and Bach cantatas sung by conservatoire-trained voices accustomed to Brahms and Mahler. The HIP movement pointed out that none of these vocal styles had any historical basis in the performance of Baroque music, and began the search for singers trained in a different tradition.

Emma Kirkby (born 1949) became the iconic voice of the HIP vocal revolution — not the only important voice, but the one that first made the alternative audible to a wide audience. Her tone is light, pure, almost entirely without vibrato, with an extraordinary clarity of articulation and a sense of rhetorical inflection that makes every word audible and dramatically weighted. She sounds, as one critic memorably put it, “like a voice that has never been near a conservatoire”. Her recordings in your collection include the Handel oratorios Athalia CD 201, Esther CD 202, and La Resurrezione CD 207, and the Purcell vocal collection Music for a While CD 358 — all essential documents of the HIP vocal approach at its best.

Equally radical was the revival of the countertenor voice — the high male voice cultivated by falsetto technique that had been standard in Baroque music but had been displaced in the nineteenth century by the contralto. Alfred Deller had pioneered its revival in Britain from the 1940s onwards, and by the 1970s countertenors were appearing in virtually every major HIP recording. James Bowman, David Cordier, Andreas Scholl and others appeared regularly with Hogwood, Pinnock and Gardiner; the effect of hearing a high male voice in the alto part of a Bach cantata or Handel oratorio, rather than a female contralto, fundamentally changed the character of the music.

Harry Christophers (born 1953) and The Sixteen represent perhaps the most sustained achievement in HIP choral performance of the past forty years. Founded in 1979, The Sixteen built their reputation specifically in Handel — a composer the movement had in some ways neglected in favour of Bach — and brought to his music the same combination of vocal refinement, historical understanding and theatrical intelligence that Gardiner applied to the cantatas. The four volumes of Chandos Anthems in your collection CD 218 CD 219 CD 220 CD 221 show them at their most characteristic: a choral sound of exceptional beauty and ensemble, with soloists drawn from the choir's own ranks, period orchestra, and a feeling for the particular warmth of Handel's English choral writing that no rival ensemble has quite matched. The Handel operatic arias with Sarah Connolly CD 222, the Alexander's Feast CD 223, the Italian cantatas with Elin Manahan Thomas CD 224, and the Esther for Coro CD 243 complete a picture of sustained Handelian achievement that is without parallel in the recorded legacy.

The Purcell revival was a separate but parallel strand of the HIP movement, driven partly by the tercentenary of Purcell's death in 1995 and partly by the recognition that English Baroque music had been even more comprehensively neglected than its Continental equivalent. The Dido and Aeneas with Lorraine Hunt Lieberson on Harmonia Mundi CD 351 is one of the most moving recordings in the HIP catalogue: Hunt Lieberson's Dido has a quality of tragic dignity and emotional depth that transcends the pedagogical questions entirely. The Music for a While disc with Kirkby, Bowman and others CD 358 is the ideal introduction to Purcell's song output, and the London Baroque's chamber music disc CD 357 shows the instrumental side of the same tradition.

The Instrument Makers: Building the Sounds Back

Behind every HIP recording lies an instrument builder. The performers cannot use historical instruments without the craftsmen who have reconstructed them, often from a combination of surviving originals, written descriptions, paintings and educated guesswork. This is a craft that has developed enormously since the 1960s, and the quality of period instruments now available to performers has transformed what HIP performance can achieve technically.

The baroque violin — gut strings, shorter neck, differently angled, played with a shorter bow held differently — is now available in multiple excellent reconstructions, and Monica Huggett's recording of the Bach Violin Sonatas and Partitas on baroque violin CD 17 shows what the instrument can do in skilled hands: a tone that is more complex and less brilliant than the modern violin, with a natural articulation that makes Bach's implied polyphony clearer and more natural. Reinhard Goebel and Musica Antiqua Köln, heard in the Bach Panorama collection CD 33, brought a level of technical virtuosity on period instruments that left even the most sceptical listeners unable to dismiss HIP as technically inferior to modern playing.

The natural trumpet is perhaps the most dramatic illustration of instrument reconstruction. Modern orchestral trumpets are valved, which means the player can produce any note in the scale; the natural trumpet of the Baroque era had no valves and could only produce the notes of the harmonic series, which meant that Baroque trumpet parts were confined almost exclusively to the very high register where the harmonics are close enough together to produce a complete scale. This is why Bach's and Handel's trumpet writing is so demanding and so distinctive — it requires a technique of extreme control in the upper register that modern players, used to valves, rarely develop. Recordings made with natural trumpets — as all the Gardiner, Pinnock and Hogwood Bach recordings are — have a brilliance and a period authenticity in the brass that period-instrument trumpeters have spent decades developing.

The Fortepiano: Reconsidering Mozart and Early Beethoven

One of the most transformative implications of HIP has been the reconsideration of keyboard music written before approximately 1820. The modern concert grand piano — the Steinway or Bösendorfer with its steel strings, felt hammers, iron frame and capacity to sustain notes almost indefinitely — is as different from Mozart's fortepiano as a modern car is from an eighteenth-century carriage. Both serve the same basic function, but everything about the experience of operating them is different.

Mozart's fortepianos were light, responsive instruments with a rapid decay of tone, a clear distinction between treble and bass registers, and a dynamic range that was relatively limited by modern standards but capable of extraordinary subtlety within that range. Playing Mozart's piano concertos on such an instrument reveals balance relationships between soloist and orchestra that are impossible to achieve on a modern grand: the fortepiano sits naturally within the texture of a period orchestra rather than dominating it, making the dialogue between soloist and ensemble genuinely conversational rather than the soloist-versus-orchestra drama of modern performances.

Andreas Staier's recording of the Mozart Piano Concertos Nos. 9 and 17 on fortepiano with Concerto Köln CD 288 is one of the finest available demonstrations of what the fortepiano reveals in Mozart: a lightness, a transparency, a particular kind of ornamentation and decoration that are simply not natural on a modern instrument. Staier is one of the most gifted historical keyboard players alive, and his understanding of Mozart's own improvisatory approach to his concertos — he would never play the solo part the same way twice — is reflected in a freedom and a sense of spontaneity that no modern-piano account quite achieves. Similarly, Roel Dieltiens and Ensemble Explorations, heard in the Vivaldi cello concertos CD 468, represent the full HIP approach applied to Baroque chamber music: all gut strings, period bows, harpsichord continuo, with a transparency and a rhythmic energy that perfectly illuminates Vivaldi's concerto style.

Beyond Baroque: HIP Meets the Romantics

For its first two decades, the HIP movement confined itself primarily to music written before roughly 1750 — Baroque and early Classical. It was widely assumed that the movement's tools and insights had no relevance to the fully Classical era, let alone the Romantic. Then, gradually and controversially, some musicians began to argue otherwise.

Roger Norrington (born 1934) was the most provocative early advocate of HIP applied to Beethoven. His recordings of the Beethoven symphonies with the London Classical Players in the late 1980s — including the Ninth Symphony CD 83 — used the instruments of Beethoven's own time and insisted on the literal observation of Beethoven's own metronome markings, which are considerably faster than the tempos most conductors use. The results were electrifying and divisive in roughly equal measure: electrifying because the music in these performances had a rhythmic drive and a textural transparency that genuinely illuminated new aspects; divisive because many listeners felt that Beethoven's greatest music requires a weight and a grandeur that smaller period ensembles and faster tempos cannot provide. Gardiner's Beethoven 7 and 8 with the Orchestre Révolutionnaire et Romantique CD 94, slightly later and marginally less polemical in intent, found a better balance: the period instruments and historically informed tempos reveal new things without denying the music its grandeur.

The OAE and Sir Charles Mackerras conducting Schubert's Great C major Symphony CD 392 is perhaps the most successful single HIP recording of a Romantic-era work: Mackerras, who was never primarily a HIP practitioner but who absorbed the movement's lessons thoroughly, uses the period orchestra to give Schubert's famously long work a sense of inevitability and flow that modern-instrument accounts often lack. The OAE, playing gut strings on historical instruments, achieves a warmth of string tone that is actually quite different from what period-instrument recording of Bach produces — richer, more Romantic, appropriate to music that is simultaneously the last word in Classicism and the first word in the Romantic era. The Orchestra of the Age of Enlightenment also provides the orchestral backing for Renée Fleming in her Handel recital CD 228, an interesting case of a major operatic voice working with a period ensemble — the results more polished than some purists would prefer, but genuinely beautiful.

The Musica Antiqua Köln under Reinhard Goebel, one of the most technically accomplished of all period-instrument ensembles, brings a similar rigour to the Handel Marian Cantatas with Anne Sofie von Otter CD 230, one of the finest recordings of Handel's Italian secular vocal music. Von Otter is one of the very few major operatic mezzo-sopranos who has successfully adapted to the stylistic demands of period performance, and her partnership with Goebel's ensemble is a model of how the HIP approach and first-class vocal artistry can work together without compromise on either side.

And the Vivaldi Gloria and Scarlatti Dixit Dominus on Archiv CD 471 rounds out the picture of HIP applied to Italian Baroque choral music: a combination of Italian vocal style and historically informed playing that shows what the movement had achieved technically by the time this recording was made.

What the Revolution Has Permanently Changed

The HIP movement has, in its fifty or sixty years of serious activity, permanently changed the way classical music is performed and heard. Some of these changes are now so thoroughly absorbed that they are no longer perceived as radical.

The first and most fundamental change is the dismantling of the nineteenth century's assumption that more is better: that larger orchestras, more vibrato, slower tempos and greater weight of tone are the marks of serious music-making. Bach performed with a chamber-scale ensemble is now the norm, not the experiment. A soprano singing Purcell without wall-to-wall vibrato is now a standard choice, not an eccentric one. Harpsichords appear in Baroque recordings as a matter of course. Natural trumpets are routinely trained and deployed. None of this was true in 1960.

The second change is the general raising of awareness that musical performance involves historical choices. Modern orchestras still play on modern instruments, but conductors like Simon Rattle, who trained alongside the HIP movement and absorbed its lessons, bring to Bach and Handel an awareness of historically appropriate articulation, ornamentation and tempo that conductors of the 1950s entirely lacked. The Berlin Philharmonic playing Bach today sounds nothing like the Berlin Philharmonic playing Bach in 1950 — not because they have adopted period instruments, but because the HIP movement changed the interpretative culture in which they work.

The third change is the repertoire that has been recovered. Rameau's operas and keyboard music, Purcell's instrumental works, the Bach cantatas as a living performing tradition, the full range of Handel's operas — none of these were part of the regular concert repertoire in 1960. The HIP movement provided both the tools and the motivation to recover them, and it is difficult to imagine returning to a world in which they were not performed.

The debates continue. There are still serious musicians who believe that the HIP approach, for all its historical information, sacrifices too much of the music's emotional depth on the altar of authenticity. There are HIP practitioners who push the historical claims further than the evidence supports. The question of how far the approach should extend into the Romantic era remains genuinely open. And the fundamental philosophical question — whether it is possible or even desirable to hear music as its original audience heard it, and whether that hearing would be more or less revelatory than our own — has never been satisfactorily resolved and probably never will be. What is not in doubt is that the music is better served by these debates than it was by their absence.

Portrait Gallery: The Major Figures

Nikolaus Harnoncourt (1929–2016) was the founding philosopher of the movement — the man who most completely articulated why historical performance practice mattered not just as scholarship but as music. His Concentus Musicus Wien recordings of the 1970s and 80s, rough-edged and intellectually demanding, were the works that forced the musical establishment to take the questions seriously. He later extended his work into opera, conducting at the Vienna State Opera and at Zurich with the same rigorous intelligence, and his recordings of Mozart operas are among the finest available.

Christopher Hogwood (1941–2014) provided the scholarly rigour and the institutional momentum that turned HIP from a fringe activity into an established practice. The Academy of Ancient Music became a model for dozens of similar ensembles around the world, and his complete Bach orchestral recording project was the first demonstration that HIP could be applied systematically to an entire composer's output.

John Eliot Gardiner (born 1943) has done more than anyone to show that HIP need not choose between historical accuracy and emotional engagement. His work across fifty years — from Monteverdi to Brahms, from Bach cantatas to Schumann oratorios — is the fullest demonstration available of what a historically informed musician, working at the highest level, can achieve across the broadest possible repertoire.

Trevor Pinnock (born 1946) brought the keyboard player's particular clarity and rhythmic precision to HIP orchestral conducting, and his English Concert recordings are among the most consistently satisfying in the HIP discography. His Handel is particularly outstanding.

Philippe Herreweghe (born 1947) is the vocal specialist among the great HIP conductors — the most refined choral director of his generation, with a sense of text and a quality of choral sound that is immediately recognisable. His Bach Passions and his Rameau are the standards against which all others are measured.

Ton Koopman (born 1944) is the movement's most comprehensive practitioner: there is almost no major Baroque composer whose complete works he has not recorded. His musicological thoroughness sometimes outpaces his musical inspiration, but at his best — in Bach, Handel, Buxtehude — he is outstanding.

Harry Christophers (born 1953) has made The Sixteen into one of the finest choral ensembles in the world through a combination of vocal refinement, historical understanding and sheer musical intelligence. His Handel is the most consistently distinguished body of Handel recording made by any single conductor.

Monica Huggett (born 1953) is the finest baroque violinist of her generation — technically the equal of any modern violinist, with an additional command of historical style that makes her Bach the most complete available on period instruments. Her influence as a teacher of baroque violin has been equally significant.

Emma Kirkby (born 1949) remains the iconic voice of HIP singing — not the most powerful, not the most dramatically varied, but the most purely beautiful and the most historically informed. Her recordings of Purcell, Handel and Bach have defined the sound world of early music vocal performance for four decades.

A Musical Portrait

The Organ and Its Music

The King of Instruments  ·  Four Centuries of Pipes, Wind & Glory

The King of Instruments

Mozart called it the king of instruments. Handel could not resist one whenever he found himself near a church. Bach organised his whole professional life around access to the finest examples he could find, walking two hundred miles when necessary to hear a great player on a great instrument. The organ is the largest, most complex and most powerful of all musical instruments, and it has attracted a body of repertoire — from the austere grandeur of the North German Baroque through to the almost orchestral opulence of the French Romantic school — that is unlike anything written for any other instrument.

What makes the organ unique is, paradoxically, also what makes it the most neglected major instrument in most listeners' experience. It cannot be taken to a concert hall. It cannot be carried from city to city. It lives where it was built, in a church or a concert hall, and it is shaped by that building as much as the building is shaped by it. Every organ is different — different pipes, different stops, different acoustics, different character. You cannot hear the same instrument in two different places, and two organists playing the same instrument will sound quite different depending on what stops they choose and how they use the acoustic. This means that listening to organ music, more than any other repertoire, requires an awareness of where you are listening from and what instrument is making the sound.

Your collection is exceptionally rich in organ music. Simon Preston alone appears eight times, playing instruments that include Westminster Abbey, the Archiv studio harpsichord, and various German historic organs. Ton Koopman appears three times as organist. Marie-Claire Alain, Helmut Walcha, Peter Hurford, Piet Kee, Chantal de Zeeuw, Philip Ledger, and a host of others bring their own instruments and their own interpretative personalities to repertoire spanning four centuries. What follows is an attempt to trace the organ's history and its music, with your own collection as the guide throughout.

How the Organ Works

The organ is, at its simplest, a machine for blowing air through pipes. A pump — historically bellows, now almost always an electric blower — maintains a constant supply of compressed air in a reservoir called the windchest. When the organist presses a key, a valve opens and air is admitted to the appropriate pipe or pipes. The pipe vibrates at a frequency determined by its length and internal diameter, producing a musical note. Multiple pipes for each note, of different materials (wooden or metal), different shapes (open or stopped, flue or reed) and different scales, produce different qualities of tone — each combination called a stop. The organist chooses which stops to engage, building up a tone colour from a vast palette.

The pipes are arranged on multiple manuals (keyboards played by the hands) and a pedalboard played by the feet. A large organ might have four or five manuals and sixty to a hundred different stops, controlling several thousand pipes. The diversity of tonal combination available is essentially limitless: the same melody can sound like a single flute, a full reed chorus, a bright mixture of high harmonics, a warm string ensemble, or a sound resembling a full orchestra. No other instrument has this range.

The physical scale of a large organ is extraordinary. The case of the famous Müller organ at St Bavo's Church in Haarlem — the instrument on which Piet Kee plays Bach in your collection CD 519 and on which the young Mozart played during his visit to the Netherlands in 1766 — is nearly fifteen metres tall. Its 5,000 pipes range from the thirty-two-foot Pedal Open Diapason, whose lowest note vibrates at approximately eight cycles per second (below the threshold of what most ears can hear as a definite pitch, felt as much as heard) to pipes a fraction of an inch long producing the highest harmonics. The acoustic at St Bavo's has a reverberation time of several seconds, which means that the organ and its building are in a continuous acoustic conversation: every note lingers long after it has been played, and the organist must account for this in every phrase.

The Instrument's Origins: From Alexandria to the Gothic Cathedral

The organ is among the oldest keyboard instruments in existence. Its ancestor, the hydraulis or water organ, was invented in Alexandria around 250 BC by Ctesibius, a Greek engineer who used water pressure to regulate the wind supply and a keyboard to control the valves. Roman audiences heard it at the Colosseum and the circus; the Emperor Nero was reportedly an enthusiast. The instrument disappeared from Western Europe with the fall of Rome and survived only in Byzantium, from where it was reintroduced to the Frankish court of Pepin III in 757 as a diplomatic gift from the Byzantine Emperor.

The medieval organ was a very different creature from its descendants: small, portable (“portative”), with a single manual and a narrow compass. The first “positive” organs — larger instruments installed in a fixed position — appeared in churches from about the ninth century onwards, and by the fourteenth century the organ had become the standard instrument of Catholic liturgical music across Europe. The early church organ had no individual stops: the pipes were combined in fixed mixtures (called “blockwerk”), producing a single, massive, undifferentiated sound. The development of individual stops — the ability to select specific groups of pipes — began in the fifteenth century and transformed the instrument's expressive possibilities.

By the sixteenth century, the great Renaissance organs of Germany and the Netherlands had acquired something approaching their modern form: multiple manuals, a pedalboard, individual stops covering a wide range of tonal colours. The instrument-building traditions of the Netherlands, North Germany and South Germany developed in parallel, each producing instruments with a distinct character: the North German organ bright and transparent, ideal for contrapuntal clarity; the South German instrument warmer and more homogeneous; the Dutch organ combining elements of both in instruments of extraordinary richness and variety.

The North German Baroque: Buxtehude and the Tradition Bach Inherited

The North German organ school of the seventeenth century produced some of the most adventurous keyboard music of the Baroque era — music of improvisatory freedom, harmonic daring, and a sense of theatrical drama that later composers, including Bach himself, found endlessly stimulating. Its central figure was Dietrich Buxtehude (c.1637–1707), the Danish-German organist of the Marienkirche in Lübeck, whose evening concerts — the Abendmusiken — attracted audiences from across northern Europe.

It was to hear Buxtehude that the twenty-year-old Bach walked approximately four hundred kilometres from Arnstadt to Lübeck in 1705 — a journey that was supposed to last four weeks and extended to four months as Bach absorbed everything Buxtehude could teach him. The influence was permanent: Bach's early organ works, with their improvisatory preludes and massive pedal solos, show the North German style absorbed and already being transformed. Buxtehude's own organ music — the Praeludia with their alternation of free fantasia and strict fugue, the Ciacona and Passacaglia that apply Baroque variation forms to the pedal instrument — appears in your collection on the Seraphim double disc that also includes Couperin and further Bach CD 520, played by Hans-Christoph Becker-Foss and Franz Lehrndorfer on various German historic organs.

François Couperin's organ music occupies a quite different world from the German tradition: more measured, more decorative, rooted in the French liturgical tradition of plein jeu and grand jeu, the five organ masses he wrote for the Paris churches combining formal propriety with harmonic subtlety. French organ music of this period is among the least familiar and most rewarding in the repertoire, and CD 520 provides an ideal introduction alongside the German works.

Johann Sebastian Bach: The Absolute Summit

Bach's organ music is the summit of the instrument's repertoire — not because later composers failed to produce significant work, but because no subsequent composer combined Bach's contrapuntal mastery with his organistic imagination and his sheer melodic fertility in the way he did. His output for the instrument spans his entire career, from the youthful Arnstadt toccatas and fantasias to the mature Leipzig Preludes and Fugues and the chorale-based collections of his last decades, and it covers every form available to a Baroque organist: prelude, toccata, fantasia, passacaglia, fugue, chaconne, and the uniquely Lutheran chorale prelude.

The story of his organ posts is a story of perpetual dissatisfaction. At Arnstadt (1703–1707) he had a fine new instrument but a difficult congregation and a mediocre choir. At Mühlhausen (1707–1708) he obtained permission to rebuild the organ at considerable expense but stayed less than a year. At Weimar (1708–1717) he finally had access to an instrument and a court worthy of his abilities, and it was here that most of his greatest organ works were written. His subsequent move to Cöthen (a Calvinist court without organ music) and then to Leipzig (where he was employed as Cantor, not as organist) meant that his organ career was effectively over by his mid-thirties, though he continued to advise on organ building and to perform occasionally for the rest of his life.

The most famous single piece he wrote for the instrument — indeed, the most famous organ piece ever written — is the Toccata and Fugue in D minor BWV 565. It is also the most debated: some scholars believe it was originally written for solo violin and only later transcribed for organ; others argue that the opening gesture, with its plunging octave and the held diminished seventh chord, can only have been conceived for an instrument capable of sustaining indefinitely. Whatever its origins, it is a work of extraordinary theatricality: the Toccata improvises freely over sustained bass notes, building harmonic tension with a recklessness that is almost improper; the Fugue is more contained but builds with inevitable momentum to a conclusion of massive grandeur. Simon Preston plays it twice in your collection: once on a studio organ for Deutsche Grammophon CD 62 and once in a broader survey of organ works CD 515. Ton Koopman offers a contrasting period-instrument account CD 61, somewhat faster and more transparent in texture, reminding us that the work was written for instruments very different from modern concert organs.

But the D minor Toccata, for all its fame, is not Bach's greatest organ work. That distinction belongs to a small group of large-scale Preludes and Fugues — the E flat major BWV 552, the C minor BWV 546, the B minor BWV 544, the A minor BWV 543 — and to the collections of chorale preludes that form the deepest stratum of his organ output. The Chorale Preludes of the Orgelbüchlein are concentrated miniatures, each one a meditation on a Lutheran hymn tune, the cantus firmus typically in the top voice while the other voices weave their own independent commentary. They are among the most intimate things Bach wrote for any medium: not public music, not theatrical, but deeply inward, as if the organist is thinking aloud in the presence of the divine.

Your collection represents the Bach organ repertoire through four very different lenses. Helmut Walcha's Archiv recording of the Great Organ Works CD 60 is one of the most celebrated Bach organ recordings ever made. Walcha (1907–1991) lost his sight at the age of nineteen through a smallpox vaccination and learned the entire Bach organ corpus by having it read to him while he sat at the keyboard. His playing has a quality that is difficult to describe and easy to recognise: an absolute clarity of contrapuntal texture, a rhythmic security that never becomes metronomic, and a sense of the overall architecture of each piece that comes from having lived with these works for decades without the distraction of reading music. He recorded the complete Bach organ works twice — the first time on harpsichord and positive organ in the early 1950s, the second time on the great Schnitger organs of Germany — and both cycles are monuments of the discography.

Marie-Claire Alain (1926–2013) recorded the complete Bach organ works three times over a fifty-year career and appears in your collection in the fourth volume of her third complete cycle CD 59. She was the most prolific Bach organist of the twentieth century — not always the most profound, but always technically accomplished, historically well-informed, and possessed of a natural musicality that makes even the most demanding works feel organic rather than mechanical.

Ton Koopman's approach on CD 61 CD 61 and the Organ Masterpieces disc CD 518 is the most explicitly period-informed: he plays on historic or historical-copy instruments, uses ornamentation from Baroque practice, and brings the harpsichordist's rhythmic precision to music that on a large modern organ can easily become over-sustained and vague. His tempos are generally faster than the traditional German approach, the articulation crisper, the registration more transparent.

Piet Kee's recordings at St Bavo's in Haarlem CD 519 occupy a unique place in the discography because of the instrument he is playing. The Müller organ at St Bavo's is one of the supreme surviving Baroque organs of Europe: built between 1735 and 1738, modified over the centuries but largely preserved in its original character, it was the instrument that Christian Müller — one of the finest builders of the era — intended as his masterwork. Kee has spent decades as its principal interpreter, and his recording brings to the familiar Bach works a quality of acoustic richness and period character that studio recordings cannot replicate. You can hear the building breathing around the instrument, the reverberation adding its own voice to Bach's counterpoint.

Handel's Organ Concertos: Entertainment as Art

George Frideric Handel's organ concertos are among the most delightful and historically significant contributions to the instrument's repertoire, and they represent something quite different from Bach's solitary contrapuntal meditations. Where Bach wrote for the organ as an instrument of spiritual depth and intellectual rigour, Handel wrote for it as an entertainer — quite literally. He would perform an organ concerto between the acts of his oratorios, improvising freely within the written framework, dazzling his London audiences with the same combination of technical display and musical intelligence that had made him famous.

The concertos are in two published sets: Op. 4 (six concertos, 1738) and Op. 7 (six more, published posthumously in 1761), with a number of additional works. They are typically in three or four movements — slow introduction, allegro, slow movement, brisk finale — with the solo part ranging from rigorously written passages to sections marked simply “ad libitum” where the organist was expected to improvise. Handel himself almost certainly never played the same concerto the same way twice.

Your collection holds four recordings, which makes this the most comprehensively covered single repertoire area in your organ holdings. Simon Preston with the Menuhin Festival Orchestra under Yehudi Menuhin CD 209 is the oldest recording and shows a more traditional approach: a larger ensemble, a fuller organ sound, with Preston's natural musicality and complete technical security providing exactly what the music needs even if the style is not quite period-informed. His second set with the English Concert under Trevor Pinnock — Op. 4 CD 211 and Op. 7 CD 212 — represents the HIP approach and is notably different: smaller orchestra, more transparent texture, period instruments throughout, and an organ sound that is more registrally varied and more historically appropriate to Handel's own instruments. These Archiv recordings are among the finest in the HIP Handel discography and repay comparison with the earlier EMI set as an illustration of how profoundly the performance-practice revolution changed the sound of this music. Ton Koopman with his Amsterdam Baroque Orchestra on Erato CD 210 adds a third perspective: less refined perhaps than the Preston/Pinnock, but with a swagger and an energy that recalls the theatrical context in which these works were born.

The Classical Organ: Haydn's ‘Little Organ Mass’

The organ occupied a more modest position in the Classical era than it had in the Baroque, largely because the concert hall was displacing the church as the primary venue for serious music-making, and the organ could not follow. Haydn and Mozart wrote relatively little solo organ music; neither regarded the instrument with Bach's consuming passion. But the organ continued to play an essential role in sacred music, and Haydn's Missa brevis Sancti Joannis de Deo — nicknamed the “Little Organ Mass” because of its prominent organ obbligato part — is a charming example of the instrument in its liturgical role, the solo organ weaving a graceful melody over the choir's harmonic foundation in the Benedictus. Your collection includes this work as part of a Naxos disc of Haydn sacred music CD 235.

The Romantic Organ: Liszt, Brahms and the German Legacy

The Romantic era brought the organ a new repertoire of considerable ambition and variety, shaped by the particular qualities of the large Romantic instruments that were being built across Europe: instruments with more stops, more power, more flexibility than their Baroque predecessors, capable of sustaining a kind of orchestral grandeur that the eighteenth-century organ could not achieve.

Franz Liszt (1811–1886) brought to the organ the same combination of technical brilliance and visionary imagination that made his piano music so transformative. His two major organ works — the Prélude et Fugue sur le nom BACH and the Ad nos, ad salutarem undam Fantasy and Fugue — are among the most ambitious pieces in the Romantic organ repertoire, both in the complexity of their structure and in the sheer scale of their demands on instrument and player. Chantal de Zeeuw's recording of the B-A-C-H Prelude and Fugue for Pierre Vérany CD 264, paired with Liszt's arrangement of a Bach cantata movement, shows the Romantic organ at its most theatrical: the fugue builds from the four-note motto with almost unbearable patience to a conclusion of massive architectural grandeur, the instrument functioning as a one-player orchestra. The contrast with Bach's own treatment of the same notes in Contrapunctus XIV of the Art of Fugue is fascinating and deeply revealing about the difference between Baroque and Romantic musical thought.

Johannes Brahms (1833–1897) represents the completely opposite face of the Romantic organ. His complete organ works — eleven chorale preludes and a prelude and fugue, all written in the last years of his life — are among the most inward and least theatrical music he ever produced. They were written, like Schubert's last piano sonatas, in the shadow of approaching death; Brahms completed the chorale preludes in the summer of 1896, the year before he died, and never heard them performed. They are not large: most last only two or three minutes. But they have a concentrated emotional depth that makes them — to those who know them — among the most moving things Brahms ever wrote. The chorale melody appears in each piece like a remembered voice, the harmonisation around it drawn from a lifetime's accumulated wisdom. Anne Horsch's complete Brahms organ works on CPO in an SACD recording CD 117 is an important disc: this repertoire is rarely recorded at all, and rarely recorded as thoughtfully as this. It is the necessary counterweight to Liszt's extroversion: the organ as a medium for private meditation rather than public display.

Aristide Cavaillé-Coll and the French Romantic School

If one man is responsible for creating the French Romantic organ tradition, it is Aristide Cavaillé-Coll (1811–1899), the Toulouse-born instrument builder who installed his magnificent organs in the great Paris churches — Saint-Sulpice, Notre-Dame, the Madeleine, the Trinité — and in doing so created the sonic world that inspired César Franck, Charles-Marie Widor, Camille Saint-Saëns and the entire French organ school of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. A Cavaillé-Coll organ is a different creature from a North German Baroque instrument: warmer, more homogeneous, with a characteristic tone in the foundations that is simultaneously full and smooth, and a capacity for crescendo — the gradual addition of stops to build from a single soft flute to the full power of the instrument — that Baroque organs could not match. His instruments respond to the player with a physical immediacy, the wind pressure and valve mechanism designed so that each note speaks cleanly and promptly regardless of how many stops are engaged.

Your collection contains a unique document of this tradition: the Hommage à Aristide Cavaillé-Coll disc recorded on the Grand Orgue of the Abbatiale Saint-Gilles de Montoire CD 511, with Marie-Andrée Morisset-Balier at the organ and Michel Morisset on trumpet. This particular instrument, built by Cavaillé-Coll himself, preserves the sound world of the French Romantic tradition in something close to its original state: the tone colours available, the way the instrument breathes and responds, the acoustic of the abbey church combining with the organ to produce that characteristic French grandeur. Hearing this disc alongside any of the Bach recordings gives a vivid impression of how differently two great traditions conceived the same instrument.

The central figure of the French Romantic school was César Franck (1822–1890), the Belgian-born organist of the Trinité in Paris, whose three Chorals for organ are among the supreme achievements of late Romantic keyboard music. Written in the last year of his life — Franck completed them on his deathbed — they combine the rigour of the chorale prelude tradition inherited from Bach with a harmonic language of extraordinary richness and the full sonority of a great Cavaillé-Coll instrument. They are not well-known outside organ circles but they deserve the widest possible audience: the E major Choral in particular has a quality of sustained lyrical beauty that is unlike anything else in the organ repertoire. Peter Hurford's survey disc of organ favourites on Decca includes Franck alongside Widor, Boëllmann and others CD 514, providing a useful introduction to the French Romantic tradition in a single accessible disc.

Charles-Marie Widor (1844–1937) composed ten organ symphonies between 1872 and 1900 — extended multi-movement works designed to exploit every resource of the Cavaillé-Coll instrument, combining Franck's harmonic seriousness with a more overtly theatrical grandeur. The famous Toccata from his Fifth Symphony, with its motoric repeated-chord accompaniment and soaring melody, has become the most played piece of organ music after Bach's D minor Toccata and Fugue. It appears, among other works, on Peter Hurford's disc CD 514.

The Organ Symphony: Saint-Saëns

The most famous work for organ and orchestra — arguably the most famous piece of French symphonic music — is Camille Saint-Saëns's Symphony No. 3 in C minor Op. 78, known universally as the Organ Symphony. Composed in 1886 and dedicated to the memory of Liszt (who died while Saint-Saëns was finishing the work), it is one of the most exhilaratingly scored pieces in the repertoire: two movements each divided into two linked sections, with the organ appearing first in a chorale of hushed grandeur at the movement's climax, then in the finale as the foundation of an overwhelming statement of triumph.

Saint-Saëns himself said of it: “I have given everything to it I was able to give. What I have here accomplished, I will never achieve again.” The finale, which transforms the gloomy C minor of the opening into a blazing C major, with the organ pedals underpinning a theme of unstoppable momentum and the full orchestra above it, is one of the great thrilling moments in all of nineteenth-century music. The work became the most popular orchestral showpiece using organ until Poulenc's Organ Concerto of 1938 offered a twentieth-century alternative.

Your collection holds it in two recordings that offer an instructive contrast. Peter Hurford with the Montreal Symphony Orchestra on Decca CD 381 pairs it with the Carnival of the Animals and piano works: a Francophone recording in which the organ playing has the idiomatic naturalness of a player steeped in the French tradition, the sound of the Montreal organ warm and resonant. Herbert von Karajan with the Berliner Philharmoniker on Deutsche Grammophon CD 382 takes a quite different approach: grander, more symphonic, with Karajan treating the organ almost as one voice among many in a vast orchestral canvas. The Berlin Philharmonic's string sound, in particular, gives the opening pages a weight and warmth that the smaller Montreal strings cannot match. The two recordings illuminate different aspects of the same work: the Hurford is more specifically French in character; the Karajan is more internationally symphonic. Both are worth hearing, and the comparison illustrates the fundamental difference between understanding a composer's cultural tradition and imposing a broader interpretative personality on the music.

The Great Organs of Britain and Europe

One of the most pleasurable aspects of the organ discography is the way recordings serve as portraits of specific instruments in specific buildings. Your collection contains several such portraits, each illuminating a different aspect of the organ's variety.

Simon Preston at Westminster Abbey CD 516 presents one of the great British cathedral organs in its natural habitat. The Westminster Abbey organ — like most British cathedral organs, installed high in a stone screen above the choir — has a particular tonal character: bright and clear in the upper registers, with a quality in the foundations that reflects the building's long reverberation and vast acoustic. Preston, who was Sub-Organist at Westminster Abbey before his appointment at Christ Church Oxford, knows the instrument's character intimately, and the recital programme is chosen to show what it does best.

Philip Ledger at King's College Chapel, Cambridge CD 512 offers a collection of organ voluntaries — the English tradition of short pieces played before and after services — that ranges from Baroque through to twentieth-century British composers. King's College Chapel is one of the most acoustically generous buildings in England, its stone vaulting creating a reverberant sound that gives organ music a sustained, almost infinite quality; Ledger's programme of voluntaries is the ideal vehicle for exploring what this acoustic does to the instrument.

The Baroque Organ of St Anne's Church in Warsaw CD 513, played by Przemysław Kapituła, documents a specific historical instrument in Central Europe: the kind of modestly scaled, carefully preserved church organ that Bach himself might have played, quite unlike the massive modern instruments of the concert hall or the vast cathedral organs of England and France. The intimacy of the sound — the smaller scale, the characteristic tone of the eighteenth-century pipes — puts the Bach repertoire in a different light from any of the large-instrument recordings.

The Father Willis organ at Salisbury Cathedral, heard in a choral recital of the Cathedral choir CD 517, represents yet another tradition: Henry Willis (1821–1901) was the nineteenth century's greatest British organ builder, whose instruments — at the Royal Albert Hall, St Paul's Cathedral, and Salisbury — defined the sound of the Victorian Anglican church. The Father Willis tone is warmer and more blended than the bright clarity of a Baroque organ, with a characteristic quality in the reeds that became the identifying mark of the English cathedral sound. The Salisbury disc shows this organ in its proper liturgical context, accompanying the choir in choral evensong and voluntaries.

Portrait Gallery: The Organists in Your Collection

Simon Preston (born 1938) is the most significant single figure in your collection's organ holdings, appearing in eight discs. He was Sub-Organist of Westminster Abbey, then Organist of Christ Church Oxford (succeeding Thomas Armstrong), then Organist and Master of the Choristers of Westminster Abbey, and he maintained a parallel career as a solo recitalist and recording artist of the first rank. His Handel recordings with Trevor Pinnock and the English Concert CD 211 CD 212 are among the finest HIP recordings of the repertoire. His Bach CD 62 CD 515 has a clean, well-paced intelligence that makes it consistently satisfying. And his Westminster Abbey recital CD 516 shows what a great organist can do with a great instrument that he knows intimately from years of daily use. He also appears as conductor in several of your Handel choral recordings CD 225, demonstrating the breadth of a career that extended well beyond the organ bench.

Helmut Walcha (1907–1991) was, by the consensus of several generations of musicians and listeners, the greatest Bach organist of the twentieth century. Born in Leipzig and trained at the conservatory there, he lost his sight completely at the age of nineteen as a result of a vaccination against smallpox. Rather than abandoning the keyboard, he adapted: his students read music to him note by note while he sat at the keyboard and memorised it; his concert programmes were learned in this way, piece by piece, over years. His two complete recordings of the Bach organ works for Archiv — the second of which, made in the 1970s on the great Schnitger organs of Germany and the Netherlands, is represented in your collection CD 60 — have a quality that many listeners find irreplaceable: an absolute purity of contrapuntal voice, a rhythmic precision that owes nothing to visual distraction, and a depth of interpretative thought accumulated over decades of living with this music.

Marie-Claire Alain (1926–2013) was the most prolific Bach organist of the twentieth century — not the most profound, but the most comprehensive and historically well-informed. The sister of the composer Jehan Alain (killed at the age of twenty-nine in 1940), she carried on the family's musical legacy through an extraordinarily productive career that included three complete Bach organ cycles CD 59 and recordings of virtually the entire French organ repertoire. Her contribution to the historical understanding of French Baroque organ music was particularly significant: she commissioned the building of historical-copy instruments and brought the scholarly rigour of the HIP movement to repertoire that had previously been played on entirely inappropriate Romantic instruments.

Ton Koopman (born 1944) has already appeared extensively in the HIP article, but his work as an organist — distinct from his role as conductor of the Amsterdam Baroque Orchestra — deserves separate acknowledgment. His organ recordings in your collection CD 61 CD 518 show an organist of complete historical awareness and great rhythmic vitality, at his best in the toccatas and fantasias where the combination of contrapuntal rigour and improvisatory freedom is most demanding. His Handel Organ Concertos CD 210 have a particular energy and wit that reflect his theatre instincts: these are concert pieces, he reminds us, not sacred meditations.

Peter Hurford (born 1930) was the most prolific British organist of the late twentieth century — extraordinarily productive as a recording artist, most notably in the ten-volume complete Bach organ works for Decca that remains one of the standard complete cycles available. His survey disc of organ favourites CD 514 and his Saint-Saëns Organ Symphony CD 381 show two different aspects of his gifts: the ease with which he moves between styles, and the warmth and confidence of his playing in the French Romantic tradition.

Piet Kee (born 1927) spent most of his career as organist of the Grote Kerk in Haarlem — which means he had decades of daily access to the great Müller organ that is heard in your collection CD 519. His relationship with that instrument is the defining fact of his musicianship: he understands its character as only someone who has played it every day for years can, and his Bach on the Müller organ has a combination of historical appropriateness and deep personal knowledge that is quite different from any studio recording.

The Trumpet and Organ: An Ancient Partnership

The combination of trumpet and organ is one of the oldest and most natural of all musical partnerships — both instruments are at their best in large resonant spaces, both belong to the tradition of outdoor ceremonial music, and the brightness of the natural trumpet is the ideal complement to the organ's fuller, more sustained tone. Alison Balsom and Quentin Thomas's disc on EMI CD 533 brings together two players of exceptional quality in a programme that exploits this combination to the full: Balsom, arguably the finest trumpet soloist of her generation, and Thomas at the organ in a varied programme that ranges from Baroque to the twentieth century. The disc is a reminder that the organ is not only a solo instrument or a cathedral presence, but a flexible vehicle for ensemble music-making of many kinds.

The Twentieth Century and the Continuing Tradition

The organ's repertoire in the twentieth century was dominated by the French tradition that Franck had founded. Olivier Messiaen (1908–1992), who served as organist at the Trinité in Paris for over sixty years, wrote music for the instrument of staggering ambition: La Nativité du Seigneur (1935), Les Corps glorieux (1939), the Livre du Saint Sacrement (1984) — long, complex, deeply theological works that pushed the instrument's sonic boundaries as far as they would go. Messiaen's organ music is not represented in your collection but belongs on the wishlist: it is unlike anything else in the repertoire, simultaneously the summit of the French organ tradition and a complete world unto itself.

Maurice Duruflé (1902–1986) and Jean Langlais (1907–1991) continued the Franck/Widor tradition with rather more restraint: Duruflé's few but exquisitely crafted organ works — the Prélude, Ada&gio et Choral varié and the four Motets — are among the finest French organ pieces of the century, their modal harmonies and dignified restraint placing them firmly in the sacred tradition their teacher Vierne had established.

Recommended Recordings Not in the Collection

Bach: Complete Organ Works — Helmut Walcha
Deutsche Grammophon Archiv  ·  14-disc box set  ·  recorded 1947–52 and 1956–71

The complete cycles by the musician who, despite blindness from the age of nineteen, became the undisputed master of this repertoire. The second cycle, recorded on the great Schnitger organs of Germany and the Netherlands, is the one to seek: the combination of Walcha's absolute clarity of contrapuntal voice with these historic instruments is uniquely compelling. Your collection already has the Great Organ Works disc; the complete set extends this across all fourteen volumes.

Franck: Complete Organ Works — various organists
Various labels  ·  2-disc set

The three Chorals, the Six Pièces and the Trois Pièces represent the summit of the French Romantic organ tradition, and they are tragically underrepresented in most collections. Marie-Claire Alain's Erato recording, on a Cavaillé-Coll instrument, is the most stylistically authentic; Wayne Marshall's more recent account on Virgin Classics is more broadly appealing. Either is essential for understanding what the French organ school was actually about.

Messiaen: Livre du Saint Sacrement — Gillian Weir
Collins Classics  ·  2-disc set  ·  1988

The summit of the twentieth-century organ repertoire: Messiaen's last and largest organ work, completed in 1984 at the age of seventy-six. Eighteen pieces of vast scope and spiritual intensity, drawing on bird-song, Hindu rhythms, modes of limited transposition and the full resources of a great modern organ. Gillian Weir is the supreme Messiaen interpreter; her Collins recording remains the finest available. An essential document of what the organ can do in a composer's hands who loved it completely and understood it profoundly.

Widor: Complete Organ Symphonies — Jeanne Demessieux
Various labels  ·  5-disc set

The ten symphonies complete, on Cavaillé-Coll instruments in the Paris churches for which they were written. Demessieux (1921–1968) was the finest woman organist of the twentieth century and the most naturally gifted Widor interpreter on record; her early death at forty-six cut short a career that had already produced recordings of exceptional quality. The famous Toccata from Symphony No. 5 in context of the complete work reveals how much more there is to Widor than his most popular page.

Further Reading

Books
The Organ: An Encyclopedia
Douglas E. Bush & Richard Kassel (eds.)  ·  Routledge, 2006

The comprehensive reference work on the instrument, its builders, its composers and its performers, with entries covering all aspects of organ history from the hydraulis of Alexandria to the digital instrument of the present day. Authoritative and exhaustively cross-referenced, it is the standard English-language reference for anyone who wants to go deeper into the instrument's history than any general survey can take them.

The Organ Music of J.S. Bach
Peter Williams  ·  Cambridge University Press, 3 vols., 1980–84; revised 2003

The standard analytical study of Bach's organ works, covering every piece with a combination of historical context, source criticism and musical analysis. Williams is the world's leading Bach organ scholar and writes with great clarity and occasional wit. Not a book to read from cover to cover, but invaluable as a companion when exploring the recordings in your collection: the entries on the major Preludes and Fugues and the chorale preludes are particularly illuminating.

Messiaen: A Critical Introduction
Paul Griffiths  ·  J&W Chester, 1985

The most readable introduction to Messiaen's life and work, by the same Paul Griffiths who wrote the string quartet history. The chapter on the organ works is the best short account available, giving sufficient musical detail to make the recordings comprehensible while remaining accessible to the general reader. Essential preparation for exploring the Messiaen recordings recommended above.

Online
Organs of the World
organdatabase.com

A comprehensive database of organs worldwide, with photographs, technical specifications and in many cases audio recordings. Particularly useful for finding information about the specific instruments heard in your collection: the Müller organ at St Bavo's Haarlem, the Father Willis organ at Salisbury, and the Cavaillé-Coll instruments of Paris are all well-documented here.

BBC Radio 3 — Choral Evensong and Organist's Choice
bbc.co.uk/radio3

Radio 3's weekly Choral Evensong broadcast from a different British cathedral or chapel every week is the ideal vehicle for hearing the organ in its natural liturgical context — accompanying the choir, playing voluntaries, providing an acoustic foundation for the entire service. The archive on BBC Sounds goes back several years and provides a remarkable survey of the great British cathedral and collegiate organs. The occasional Organist's Choice programmes, in which a major organist discusses and plays their favourite pieces, are similarly valuable.
